Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/12/2000)

Hickory Lake is located on the Ripple River chain of lakes between Pine Lake and Spirit Lake. The lake consists of 197 acres and is classified as a northern pike and bluegill type lake. The public access is limited to a small sand access and shoreline within a Minnesota Department of Transportation wayside area at the inlet from the Ripple River. A small dam just downstream from the sand access limits everything except canoes and other very small watercraft. Boaters can also gain access for a fee through a private campground on the west side of the lake.

Fish population indices have remained relatively stable over the past several surveys in Hickory Lake. Northern pike abundance was above average this assessment and has been the last three surveys. The northern pike catch index for gill nets was 10.5 fish per net and this abundance of pike should provide anglers consistent pike fishing. The average northern pike was 18 inches in length and weight about 1.5 pounds. The largest northern pike observed during the survey was 32.8 inches in length. The number of walleyes has remained stable when compared to past surveys. Though, the abundance of walleyes was a little below average, they were above average in size. The average walleye captured was 19 inches, while the largest was nearly 29 inches in length and weighed 8.25 pounds. Hickory Lake has been stocked every other year with walleye fingerlings. Walleye-spawning habitat exists just upstream of Hickory Lake over which spawning fish have been observed, however no small walleyes were caught. The abundance of yellow perch, a favorite prey species of both walleye and northern pike, was low. Anglers' efforts can increase the abundance of yellow perch, an important forage species, by releasing northern pike greater than 22 inches and harvesting smaller pike. This voluntary action may reduce predation pressure on yellow perch while at the same time increasing angler catch rates of larger pike. Net catches of black crappies were similar to past surveys and are near the low range for this lake type. Growth rates for black crappies were good with sizes ranging from 5 to 11 inches in length. Trap net catches of bluegills decreased from the previous survey and averaged 14.4 fish per net. This decrease however, represents a good trend for bluegills in this lake. A lower abundance of bluegills may reduce competition for food resources between panfish allowing for better growth. Growth rates were generally good with 25 percent larger than 6 inches. Largemouth bass abundance indicated by trap nets was near the low end of the normal range for this lake type. Other species sampled in the fish community include bowfin, white sucker, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, and black and yellow bullheads.
