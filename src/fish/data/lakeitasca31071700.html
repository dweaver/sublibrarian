Status of the Fishery (as of 07/29/2002)

Rice Lake is a class 27 lake located five miles southwest of Grand Rapids. There is a public access on the southwest end of the lake. The lake management plan indicates that northern pike was the primary species for management while largemouth bass was the secondary species.

The black crappie population has typically had higher numbers but somewhat smaller fish. In 2002, black crappie sampled in gill nets (4.3/net) exceeded the 3rd quartile of 2.7/net, while trap net sampled fish (1.3/net) exceeded the lake class median of 0.9/net. Black crappie from gill nets ranged from 4.5 to 10.0 inches with a mean length of 7.3 inches. The size range was similar for fish sampled from trap nets. Fish from one to seven years old were sampled with age-3 fish representing 62% of the sample. Growth was slower than statewide averages for all ages.

In the last three assessments, bluegill trap-net catch rates have declined from 34.8 fish/net in 1990 to 9.3 fish/net in 2002. However, the 2002 catch was more normal since four of the six assessments had catch rates between 9.0 and 13.9 fish/net. The sampled fish ranged from 3.3 to 7.6 inches with a mean length of 5.6 inches. Fish sampled from gill nets had a similar catch rate and size structure. Bluegill were not aged in this assessment.

Although spring night electrofishing for largemouth bass would have provided a better representation of the population, a number of fish were sampled in the standard summer gear. Both gill-net and trap-net catch rates were higher than normal for this type of lake. Largemouth bass sampled from gill nets ranged from 5.5 to 18.0 inches with a mean length of 11.0 inches. A similar size distribution was observed for fish sampled in trap nets. Fish from one to 11 years old were determined from scales samples. There was no dominant year class based on the netting information. Growth was similar to statewide averages.

Northern pike gill-net catch rates have been near or above the lake class median of 5.1/net in all assessments. In 2002, the gill-net catch rate was 6.8 fish/net. Size structure was good compared to many area lakes; having PSD, RSD-P, and RSD-M values of 62, 13 and 0, respectively. Age analysis documented fish from age 1 to age 8 were present. Two and three year old fish comprised 55% of the sample. Growth was higher than statewide averages for one to five year old fish, but similar for the remaining ages.

Yellow perch are an important prey source for predators in many fish communities. Yellow perch numbers have always been low in Rice Lake with a gill-net catch rate only attaining the 1st quartile of 7.0 fish/net in 1985. The gill-net catch was 2.3 fish/net in 2002. The sampled fish ranged from 5.6 to 9.3 inches and had a mean length of 6.7 inches. Yellow perch were not aged in 2002.

The tullibee gill-net catch was 3.9 fish/net in 2002, which falls within the lake class median and 3rd quartlie of 2.1 and 6.2 fish/net. Tullibee numbers have been at or above average in all assessments except in 1985. It is important to protect healthy tullibee populations due to their importance as a prey species for large predators.

Other species sampled in 2002 included black bullhead, bowfin, brown bullhead, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, walleye, white sucker, and yellow bullhead.

In order to maintain or improve fish and wildlife populations, water quality and habitat must be protected. People often associate water quality problems with large-scale agricultural, forestry, urban development or industrial practices in the watershed. In reality, the impact of land use decisions on one lake lot may be relatively small, yet, the cumulative impact of those decisions on many lake lots can result in a significant decline in water quality and habitat. For example, removing shoreline and aquatic vegetation, fertilizing lawns, mowing to the waters edge, installing beach sand blankets, failing septic systems and uncontrolled run-off, all contribute excess nutrients and sediment which degrade water quality and habitat. Understanding these cumulative impacts and taking steps to avoid or minimize them will help to insure our quality fisheries can be enjoyed by future generations.
