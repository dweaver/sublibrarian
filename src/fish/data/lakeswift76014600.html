Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/11/2007)

Oliver Lake is located in western Swift County, approximately 9 miles north of Appleton. Original maps of the lake indicated a lake area of 513 acres, but a wet period during the mid-90s increased the lake area to over 600 acres, which is split into an east (455 acres) and west (211 acres) basin by Swift County Highway 5. Primary management species include walleye, yellow perch, black crappie, and bluegill.

Walleye catch rates in gill nets almost doubled since 2004. Gill nets captured 6.9 walleye per net in 2004 compared to 12.8 walleye per net during this survey. This increase is likely due to the increase in fish from the 2003 year class, which made up 56% of the walleye sampled in gill nets during this survey. Only one walleye from the 2003 year class was sampled in gill nets in 2004. Walleye from gill nets averaged 15.5 inches long and 1.5 lbs whereas walleye in trap nets averaged 13.2 inches and 1.2 lbs. Although the 2003 year class was abundant, walleye from the 2005 and 2006 year classes were also numerous.

Walleye fry were not stocked into Oliver Lake in 2007, but 10,146 yearlings and 25 adult walleye were stocked into the west basin of Oliver Lake in April of 2007. Fall electrofishing for young-of-year (YOY) walleye sampled 8.6 YOY walleye per hour of electrofishing. This indicates a small naturally reproduced population of walleye in Oliver Lake. All of the YOY were sampled in the east basin, but several yearling walleye were observed in the west basin. Based on the current population of adult and yearling walleye present, anglers can expect good fishing for walleye in the future on Oliver Lake.

Yellow perch abundance in gill nets (22 per net) was slightly lower than the 2004 survey (23.5 per net). Nonetheless, the catch rate observed during this survey was higher than the average catch rate for lakes similar to Oliver. The 2006 year class was most abundant, followed by the 2004 and 2001 year classes. Perch averaged 8 inches long and 0.3 lb, with growth being above average. YOY perch were abundant in 1/4-inch trap nets (20.8 per net), indicating natural reproduction of perch in 2007. Yellow perch up to 12 inches long were found during this survey, but anglers can expect the abundant 2006 year class of yellow perch to reach 10 inches in a few years.

Black crappie were the most abundant fish in 3/4-inch trap nets (21.2 per net), which was similar to the catch rate observed in 2004 (23.6 per net). These catch rates are the highest observed in Oliver Lake since 1995, when ?-inch trap nets captured 38.4 crappie per net. Average size was similar to 2004, with black crappie averaging 8.1 inches long in 2004 and 8.9 inches during this survey. Crappie three-years-old (2004 year class) were the dominant year class, with the oldest crappie being five-years-old. Black crappie were also the most abundant YOY fish in 1/4-inch trap nets, indicating an abundant 2007 year class.

In 2002, bluegill were introduced into Oliver Lake by stocking 1,635 adults. Bluegill adults (2,780) were also stocked in 2003. The introduction appears successful, as the bluegill population is maintaining itself. Bluegill were captured at a record-high level (3.6 per net) in 3/4-inch trap nets, but still below the average catch rate for lakes similar to Oliver. Only 0.8 per trap net were captured in 2004. Several year classes were present, with average size being 6.9 inches and 0.4 lb. Natural reproduction was observed in 2007, as 1/4-inch trap nets caught 17 YOY bluegill per net. Although the bluegill population is not abundant, individuals over 9 inches long were sampled and may provide opportunities for anglers.

Northern pike first appeared in Oliver Lake in 1995, but its abundance has been small. Only one northern pike was sampled during this survey, which was 28.6 inches long and 5.1 lbs. Quarter-inch trap nets sampled no YOY pike, indicating a missing 2007 year class.

The abundance of non-game species appears to be low in Oliver Lake. Black bullhead were sampled at their lowest levels on record, and only 0.4 common carp were caught per gill net. Bigmouth buffalo and yellow bullhead were also sampled at low levels.

Other fish species present were green sunfish, hybrid sunfish, orangespotted sunfish, pumpkinseed, Johnny darter, and fathead minnow.

Future management activities planned for Oliver Lake include fall electrofishing for YOY walleye, fish stocking as needed, monitoring of winter dissolved oxygen and fishing pressure, and a resurvey in 2010. ?
