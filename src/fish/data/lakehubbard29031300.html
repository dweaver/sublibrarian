Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/06/2007)

Little Mantrap is located on the Hubbard and Becker County line, sixteen miles north of Park Rapids. Little Mantrap has a surface area of 348 acres and a maximum depth of 54 feet. The lake is irregularly shaped with two main basins and several small bays. A county-owned public access with an earthen ramp is located on the southern bay. Little Mantrap is a popular lake for largemouth bass, panfish, and northern pike fishing, but also contains a bonus walleye population.

The Minnesota Dept. of Natural Resources (DNR) has classified Minnesota's lakes into 43 different types based on physical and chemical characteristics. Little Mantrap is in lake class 25. Other area lakes in this same classification include: Bad Axe, Belle Taine, West Crooked, Third Crow Wing, Eagle, Island, Big Mantrap, Ojibway, Spider, Straight, and Two Inlets.

Demand for larger fish by Minnesota anglers has led to the development of restrictive harvest regulations on certain lakes. These regulations are part of an experiment to increase the numbers of large fish in Minnesota's lakes. Little Mantrap was chosen for a special regulation for largemouth bass based on its present and past largemouth bass population and its estimated capacity to produce large bass. Good water quality, aquatic vegetation cover, and spawning substrate provide excellent habitat for largemouth bass in Little Mantrap. All bass from 12.0 to 18.0 must be immediately returned to the water (12-18 inch protected slot). This protected slot length limit for largemouth bass has been in place since 1997. Spring electrofishing was conducted in 2007 to evaluate largemouth bass abundance and size structure. Electrofishing results found high numbers of largemouth bass within the protected slot (12-18 inches) with bass measured up to 19.0 inches. The excellent largemouth bass population found in Little Mantrap will be maintained with the continued strong angler support and compliance of the protected slot regulation.

Northern pike and panfish are also popular species on Little Mantrap. While larger northern pike are present, anglers will find lots of small, "hammer handle" pike in the lake. Sampled northern pike had and average length and weight of 19.6 inches and 1.8 pounds, with pike measured up to 31.1 inches. Moderate to high recruitment of young pike, and slow growth rates limit the northern pike population in Little Mantrap. Bluegill were sampled in moderate numbers (30.6 bluegill/trapnet), similar to past surveys and within the range "typical" for this lake class. The bluegill population in Little Mantrap is probably known more for numbers than size, however, there are enough fish in the 6-8 inch size range that you can sort through the small ones and still get enough "eaters". Little Mantrap can provide good crappie fishing in the spring. Anglers can expect to find black crappie in the 9-11 inch size range.

Though not known as a walleye lake, Little Mantrap does support a "bonus" walleye population. Walleye abundance (1.5 walleye/gillnet) was near the current management goal of 2 walleye/gillnet and similar to past surveys. Little Mantrap is stocked with walleye fingerlings during even numbered years.

Other species sampled included high numbers of yellow bullhead, and low numbers of brown bullhead, rock bass, and hybrid sunfish. ?
