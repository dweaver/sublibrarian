Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 09/03/2002)

Parent Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 5, which consists of 64 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are average in size, have shorelines that drop off quickly, and have soft (unmineralized) and very clear water. Parent Lake is typical of lakes in this lake class.

Parent Lake was thermally stratified on 9/3/2002 with a surface temperature of 68 F and a bottom temperature of 45 F. Five ppm oxygen was retained to a depth of 30 ft, where the temperature was 52 F, and 2 ppm oxygen was retained to a depth of 34 ft, where the temperature was 48 F.

Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline on Parent Lake are mostly boulder, rubble, and gravel. Aquatic plants are sparse and grow to a depth of 13 ft; the most common plants are pondweeds and arrowhead. The inlet from Disappointment Lake and the outlet to Snowbank Lake have no barriers to fish movement at high water.

This fish population assessment consisted of nine gillnet sets. Seven previous investigations, dating back to 1950, consisted of 6-9 gillnet sets. Most of these investigations were done in August or early September. Trapnets have not been used in this lake because of the difficult portage access.

The total catch of fish (all species combined) in the gillnets in 2002 of 19.9 fish/net was in the first quartile for this lake class and was similar to the median catch of 22.1 fish/net in all investigations on this lake. The total catch of fish by weight in the gillnets in 2002 of 31.4 lb/net was in the third quartile for this lake class and was higher than the median catch of 26.2 lb/net in all investigations on this lake. Lake Class 5 is one of the more productive lake classes in northeast Minnesota, in terms of gillnet catches of fish.

Fish populations in 2002 were dominated by walleye, white sucker, and smallmouth bass, followed by rock bass. Fewer cisco were captured in 2002 than in previous investigations. Notable by their absence in this assessment, and for their infrequent presence in previous investigations, were yellow perch.

Walleye numbers in 2002 (2.7/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median catch of 2.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes in 2002 averaged 21.2" (4.9 lb), which was in the fourth quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 18.6" in all investigations on this lake. The largest walleye in 2002 was 28.0" (11.1 lb). Walleye scales were difficult to read, but it appears that 83 percent (20 of 24) of walleye captured in 2002 were from year classes stocked with fingerlings. Growth of walleye captured in 2002 was faster than normal (in the fourth quartile) by area standards.

Sucker numbers in 2002 (4.1/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 5.3/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Sucker sizes in 2002 averaged 17.7" (2.2 lb), which was at the third quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 17.3" in all investigations on this lake.

Smallmouth bass were not captured in the initial Fisheries lake survey in 1950, but were present in the next Fisheries investigation in 1978. Smallmouth numbers in 2002 (3.4/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 1.5/gillnet in all investigations on this lake since they first appeared. Many young smallmouth have been observed in the shallows during Fisheries investigations. Smallmouth sizes in 2002 averaged 13.1" (1.3 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 11.2" in all investigations on this lake. The largest smallmouth in 2002 was 18.7" (3.4 lb). Growth of smallmouth captured in 2002 was slower than normal by area standards.

Rock bass numbers in 2002 (6.7/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 5.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Rock bass sizes in 2002 averaged 6.0" (0.18 lb), which was at the first quartile for this lake class and was identical to their average size in all investigations on this lake.

Cisco numbers in 2002 (2.4/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 11.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Cisco sizes in 2002 averaged 8.2" (0.16 lb), which was in the first quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 8.8" in all investigations on this lake. The largest cisco in 2002 was 9.4".

Neascus was observed on a few of the fish examined during this investigation. Bass tapeworm was found in most of the smallmouth examined, and triaenophorus was found in most of the cisco examined. All of these parasites are native to the area and are common. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.
