Status of the Fishery (as of 08/03/2004)

The fishery in Bear Creek Reservoir (BR-1) is generally made up of channel catfish, largemouth bass, bluegill and black crappie.

Many of the 6000 yearling catfish stocked in 1996 and 1997 have survived and are growing well. Netting assessments have shown individual fish to be growing from a little over a half pound in 1998 to almost 3 and a half pounds in 2004. Anglers can expect to find channel catfish in 2005 around 22-24 inches.

Largemouth bass, stocked to control the abundance of bluegill, are reproducing and continue to be abundant. Individual growth is fast at first, but slows down once they reach about 9 inches long (4 years old). Between the age of 4 and 9, largemouth bass are only growing about 3-4 inches. This slow growth of adult bass is probably due to a lack of preferred forage. Faster growing, young bass are perhaps making use of small bluegill as prey, hence the good growth. This is what we like to see. However, once they reach adult size, their food requirements change and growth is slowing dramatically.

Bluegill, the main species of management in BR-1, are reproducing and growing well. On average, individual fish reach about 7 inches in 5 years. Abundance (12.3/trapnet) is about what we'd expect to see in a water body like BR-1. For the last 2-3 years, the most common sized bluegill has been about 7 inches. At 8 inches however, abundance drops. Data collected from spring netting assessments combined with the results of a creel survey conducted during the summer-winter 2004/2005 will help us determine the best strategy at providing a quality bluegill fishery.

Black crappie were originally stocked as young adults in 1996 and 1997 to provide an additional angling opportunity. Spring and summer trapnetting (2003-2004) collected fish that averaged about 8 inches in length. Abundance is low to moderate. Adult crappie growth, as with largemouth bass, will likely be slow due to inadequate forage.

Access to BR-1 is controlled by the Olmsted County Park Department. A small fee is charged to access the lake through Chester Woods Park, located on the north side of the reservoir.

Two experimental regulations are in place on BR-1. A 15-inch minimum length limit for largemouth bass and a 10 fish bag limit (with a 7-inch minimum from November-April) for bluegill are intended to help develop a quality bluegill fishery. Annual fall electrofishing for largemouth bass and spring trapnetting for bluegill are on-going and are the primary means by which the regulations are being evaluated. A comprehensive evaluation report for both regulations will be prepared in 2010.
