* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/10/2002)

Perch Lake is a 270 acre lake located within the city of Baxter in Crow Wing County. A public access is located on the south end of the lake. The lake is heavily developed with 83 homes/cabins (as of 1997) along 4 miles of shoreline. The maximum depth is 40' and about 61% of the lake is 15' deep or less. Shallow water substrate is primarily sand and/or muck. The aquatic plant community is relatively diverse with 29 species present. Emergent plants such as bulrush and water lilies are important for shoreline protection, maintaining water quality, and provide essential spawning habitat for bass and panfish species. Submerged plants provide food and cover needed by fish and other aquatic species.

Walleyes are currently stocked annually as fry at a rate of 1,000 per littoral acre. Past catches have been variable and ranged from 1.0 to 7.5/gill net. The 2002 walleye catch of 1.2/gill net was average when compared to similar lakes. Compared to 1997, mean length and weight increased from 19.5" and 2.6lbs to 20.8" and 3.6lbs.

Northern pike were caught in above average numbers for the second consecutive netting at 16.7/gill net in 2002. This is the second highest catch to date. Past catches have ranged from 6.7 to 20.7/gill net. The 2000 year class was the largest and contributed 32% of the catch with most of these fish measuring about 13" to 16.5". Average length and weight in 2002 (17.5" and 1.3lbs) remained similar to that in 1997 (17.7" and 1.2lbs).

The largemouth bass abundance of 0.4/trap net and 1.3/gill net was average compared to similar lakes. Spring electrofishing resulted in a largemouth bass catch rate of 31.9/hr with an average length of 12.3" and 55% measuring 12" or larger.

The bluegill catch of 11.2/trap net was average and 47% of trapnetted bluegills measured 7" or larger, compared to 64% in 1997. The 1996, 1997, and 1998 year classes represented 88% of the catch. Black crappie abundance was below average in 2002 at 0.6/trap net, but was similar to past catches ranging from 0 to 1.0/trap net.

Yellow perch are an important forage species for the lake's game fish. The 2002 abundance of 2.8/gill net was the highest in 7 nettings to date and is average when compared to similar lakes.
