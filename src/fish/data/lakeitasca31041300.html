* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

No fish consumption guidelines are available for this lake. For more information, see the "Fish Consumption Advice" pages at the Minnesota Department of Health.


Status of the Fishery (as of 07/15/2002)

Burrows Lake is 291 acres and is located within the Chippewa National Forest in northeastern Itasca County. The lake contains relatively "soft water" (alkalinity=11.5 ppm) with excellent water clarity (Secchi Disk =12.0 ft) and low fertility due to low phosphorus levels which limit biological productivity. Bulrush and three-way sedge were rare in the shoals while water lilies, water shield, and burreed were growing in a band around most of the lake with larger stands in shallow bays. Various species of pondweeds, coontail, and muskgrass were growing on most of the lake bottom down to a depth of 14 feet due to the excellent water clarity. Submerged plants provide dissolved oxygen to support gamefish throughout the littoral (<15 feet) zone, which comprises about 78% of the surface area of this lake. A temperature and dissolved oxygen profile was conducted during this survey which indicated that the lake was stratified and the thermocline existed between 15 and 26 feet. Suitable dissolved oxygen levels to support gamefish (>2 ppm), were recorded down to a depth of 24 feet while dissolved oxygen levels remained at 1.8 ppm to the lake bottom. These dissolved levels, although too low to support fish for long periods are adequate for short forays to the lake bottom for food items such as bloodworms.

Walleye were sampled at a rate of 3.2/gill net during this assessment, which was similar to previous assessments and exceeded the expected range of 1.5 to 2.3/net for similar lakes. Nineteen walleye were sampled, which had a mean length of 15.5 inches and a mean weight of 1.3 lbs. Fourteen walleye (74%) were age 3, which correlated with fry stocked in 1999, while four walleye corresponded to year classes in which no fry were stocked indicating natural recruitment. Growth was determined to be normal for all age classes.

Northern pike were sampled in gill nets at a rate of 9.2/net, which exceeded all previous catch rates, and slightly exceeded the upper range of 8.9 fish/net, for similar lakes. Although high northern pike abundance is usually associated with small fish, the average weight was 2.24 lbs, which was near the median mean value of 2.1 lbs for similar lakes. The average length of all northern pike (55) sampled was 20.7 inches. The sample of northern pike contained fish from age 1 through 10 and growth was normal for northern pike compared to similar lakes.

Smallmouth bass were sampled for the first time during this lake survey. Twenty smallmouth bass were sampled in gill nets and varied in length from 12 to 18 inches. Spring electofishing also captured 10 smallmouth bass, which ranged from 11.6 to 13.5 inches in length. Smallmouth bass were aged and varied from three to six years old. Growth was similar to the statewide average. Since smallmouth bass have never been sampled in any of the previous five net surveys or assessments, this species was most likely introduced into this lake by anglers. Smallmouth bass have been suspected of competing directly with walleye populations and reducing their abundance in many northeastern Minnesota Lakes. It has yet to be determined what impact introduced smallmouth bass will have on the walleye population in Burrows Lake.

Burrows Lake is considered by local anglers to provide a quality largemouth bass fishery. Summer test netting is ineffective for sampling largemouth bass, therefore, electrofishing was attempted to more adequately sample bass species. Electrofishing sampled largemouth and smallmouth bass at rates of 23.4 and 7.5 fish per hour, respectively. Catch rates exceeded mean catch rates (13.7 largemouth bass and 2.0 smallmouth bass) for similar lakes in the Grand Rapids area. Largemouth bass varied in length from 9 to 18 inches, 67% were over 12 inches and 17% exceeded 15 inches. Largemouth bass ranged from age 3 to 6, however 59% were age 4. Largemouth bass attained 15 inches during their fifth year of growth, while statewide they usually attain this length during their sixth year of growth.

The population of yellow perch can best be defined as a low abundance of small fish from 6.0-8.0 inches. The catch rate of 5.3 fish/net was within the expected range of 1.0-10.3/net for similar lakes and was similar to the values for previous surveys. The yellow perch population contained similar proportions of age 3 and 4 fish. Growth was slow for each year of growth from ages 2 through 4. Yellow perch provide prey for northern pike and walleyes and the small size structure usually indicates heavy predation.

Black crappie were sampled at a relatively low catch rate of 1.7/gill net. Black crappie are difficult to sample because they often suspend over open water in the summer months. Sixteen black crappie were aged and 69% were age 2 while the remaining were ages 4-6. The combined sample of trap net and gill net captured black crappie had 29% of the fish over 10 inches in length. Black crappie had fast growth in this lake as the average crappie attained 10 inches in 4 years, while statewide they usually attain this size during their sixth year of growth.

Bluegill are also a popular species on this lake, but anglers generally regard them as too small to be acceptable. Only 46 % were over six inches and 2% exceeded 8 inches in length. The catch rate for trap nets was 6.2 fish/net, which was below the expected range for similar lakes. The trap net catch consisted of fish from ages 2 through 6, however 53% were age 4 and 35% were age-3 fish. Bluegill growth was above average as they attained 6 inches during their fifth growing season, while bluegill usually require eight growing seasons to attain this length in similar lakes.

Other species sampled included golden shiners and white suckers. White sucker were sampled at a rate of 4.3/gill net, which exceeded the expected range for similar lakes. The catch contained large fish with a mean length of 17.5 inches and a mean weight of 2.3 lbs. Although most of the white sucker were too large for northern pike to prey upon, their offspring will provide future prey.
