Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

Status of the Fishery (as of 09/22/2008)

Tofte Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 4, which consists of 47 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are small, deep, and have clear and soft (unmineralized) water. Tofte Lake is larger than most of these lakes, has a more irregular shoreline shape, and has harder water.

Tofte Lake was thermally stratified on 9/22/2008 with a surface temperature of 61 F and a bottom temperature of 39 F. Good oxygen for trout (more than 5 ppm) was retained to a depth of 33 ft, where the temperature was 49 F. Adequate oxygen for warm water fish (more than 2 ppm) was present to a depth of 36 ft, where the temperature was 46 F.

Aquatic plants, mostly Chara and pondweeds, are limited to a narrow fringe along the steep shoreline and to shallow bays on the north and west ends of the lake. Bottom substrates are evenly divided among gravel, boulder, muck, rubble, sand, and ledge rock. There are no inlets. The outlet has a filter barrier built over an old beaver dam that washed out about 1994. Beavers rebuilt a dam at this site with a head of about 3 ft.

Tofte Lake has been stocked with stream trout since 1939. In recent years rainbow trout yearlings have been stocked annually and splake fingerlings have been stocked biennially.

The 2008 fish population assessment consisted of ten standard 3/4" bar mesh trapnets and two 3/8" bar mesh trapnets for catching minnow species. Fourteen previous investigations have been conducted on Tofte Lake dating back to 1969. All but the 2000, 2003, and 2008 investigations used low numbers of gillnets, and nine used 3/4" bar mesh trapnets. Small mesh trapnets were also used in a few of these previous investigations. Gillnets have not been used in recent investigations to eliminated sampling mortality. The 2008 assessment was done in late September, while most previous investigations were done in late May or early June.

Fish species captured in 2008 included fair numbers of rainbow trout and splake, and high numbers of white sucker, fathead minnows, and dace.

White sucker numbers in 2008 (5.6/standard trapnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class, but were lower than in recent investigations, probably because recent investigations were done early in the spring when suckers were engaged in spawning activity. Sucker sizes in 2008 (averaging 11.8") were smaller than in recent investigations, probably because adult sucker were not engaged in spawning activity at this time of year.

Rainbow trout numbers and weight in 2008 (1.2/standard trapnet, 1.0 lb/standard trapnet) were higher than in recent investigations. Rainbow sizes averaged 12.9" and the largest rainbow (caught in a small mesh trapnet) was 19.4".

Splake numbers and weight in 2008 (0.5/standard trapnet, 0.7 lb/standard trapnet) were lower than in recent investigations. Splake sizes in 2008 averaged 14.3" and the largest splake was 18.4".

Fewer fathead minnows and darters were caught in 2008 than in previous small-mesh netting, probably because 3/8" bar mesh nets were used in 2008 while 1/4" bar mesh trapnets were used previously. Small fish like darters may have passed through the 3/8" mesh.

Rusty crayfish (Orconectes rusticus)were first reported in Tofte Lake by a winter angler in 2006. Six rusty crayfish and six northern clearwater crayfish (O. propinquus) were caught in the trapnets during the 2008 fish assessment. Rusty and northern clearwater crayfish are native to areas to the southeast of Minnesota and were probably introduced to this area by anglers using them for bait. They are aggressive and can replace native crayfish. They can become very abundant and eliminate beds of aquatic vegetation. ?
