Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/16/2008)

A resurvey of Mud/Middle Fork Crow Reservoir "MFCR" was conducted in June of 2008. The MFCR is a large (1,565 acres) and productive reservoir located in Kandiyohi County with three distinct basins starting upstream to downstream include: Mud Lake also known as Monongalia, the Middle Fork of Crow River Reservoir, and the Mill Pond located within the City of New London. The MFCR maximum depth is 14 feet, which occurs in the Mill Pond near the New London Dam. The depth in Mud Lake is usually less than 4 feet. The reservoir basin maximum depth is about 6 feet deep. A helixor aeration system operates in the mid-portion of the reservoir basin during the winter months. Low dissolved oxygen and high levels of hydrogen sulfide can be persistent problems during the late winter/early spring months for MFCR basins. Residential development (153 homes/cabins) has occurred primarily along the west shoreline and bay areas of the reservoir portion adjacent to the city of New London. There are four public access sites located adjacent to the MFCR basins, which include one on the north shore of Mud Lake and three adjacent to the reservoir. Storm water and residential lawn runoff from the city of New London and residential homes enters the reservoir portion. Agricultural row crop drainage into the MFCR occurs via several inlets/tiles mostly into the Mud Lake basin. The Middle Fork of the Crow River enters Mud Lake near the north shore, flows through Mud and into the reservoir basin, then the Mill Pond, and eventually outlets at the New London Dam (southwest portion of the Mill Pond).

Water quality (total phosphorus=0.032 mg/l) was moderate during June of 2003 for MFCR. Water clarity was good (12.5 feet) during June of 2008. Emergent vegetation (cattail species, wild rice, and waterlily species) densities were prevalent within the MFCR, especially in the Mud Lake basin. Submergent vegetation species are varied and often dense within the basins. Submergent vegetation densities were lower in 2008 compared to 2003, especially within the Mud Lake basin. Boat travel was almost impossible during 2003 due to abundant vegetation, unlike 2008. The most commonly occurring submergent species present within the MFCR basins were celery, coontail, marestail, and northern milfoil. Filamentous algae blooms were uncommon during the start of the survey, but extensive by the end of the 2008 survey week. The most commonly occurring substrates included detritus, muck, and silt. Local anglers had good fishing success for walleye during the May opener and large sunfish (bluegill, pumpkinseed) during the summer of 2003. A 2007-08 partial winterkill of mostly largemouth bass and carp were observed dead along the shoreline in the spring of 2008. Dissolved oxygen levels were low during January of 2009 in many aerated and non-aerated shallow lakes in the Spicer Area. A winterkill assessment in the 2009 spring will determine whether restocking of gamefish is needed. MFCR is also a popular fishery for northern pike and largemouth bass. MFCR is managed primarily for northern pike, bluegill, black crappie, and walleye.

Largemouth bass were not captured in either gillnet or trapnet sets during the 2008 or 2003 MFCR resurveys. Largemouth bass were captured in low numbers during other previous surveys and assessments. A spring electrofishing survey was not conducted in 2008 due to time constraints.

Black crappie numbers were low to moderate in 2008 (1.60 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal range for similar lakes, but at the high end of the normal range for MFCR (25th=0.80, 50th=0.90, 75th=1.60 fish/gillnet). The black crappie gillnet historical average catch rate was 1.17 fish/gillnet. The 2008 black crappie catch rate from trapnets (0.33 fish/trapnet) was below the historical average (0.75 fish/trapnet). The 2008 black crappie average size was small (0.08 pounds and 5.13 inches) from gillnets. The 2008 black crappie average size was moderate (0.35 pounds and 8.17 inches) from trapnets. The black crappie historical average weights were 0.32 and 0.34 pounds from gillnets and trapnets respectively. Black crappie growth rates in MFCR were generally below the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-6. The 2006 black crappie year class comprised 69% of the 2008 total black crappie catch. Black crappie adults were last stocked in 2008 (468 fish, 117 pounds).

Northern pike numbers were abundant in 2008 (15.20 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal ranges for MFCR (25th=5.75, 50th=9.20, 75th=11.60 fish/gillnet) and similar lakes. The northern pike historical average catch rate is 9.35 fish/gillnet. The 2008 northern pike average size was small (1.92 pounds and 20.76 inches) from gillnets. The northern pike historical average weight is 1.41 pounds from gillnets. The 2008 catch rate of quality size (21.00 inches) and larger northern pike was high (6.40 fish/gillnet). The 2008 catch rate of preferred size (28.00 inches) and larger northern pike was low (0.20 fish/gillnet). Northern pike growth rates in MFCR were generally below the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-6. The three dominant northern pike year classes captured in the 2008 nets were from the 2005 (36%), 2004 (27%), and 2003 (19%) year classes.

Yellow perch numbers were low in 2008 (0.80 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal ranges for MFCR (25th=0.80, 50th=1.60, 75th=4.75 fish/gillnet) and similar lakes. The yellow perch historical average catch rate is 3.81 fish/gillnet. The 2008 yellow perch average weight (0.09 pounds) from gillnets was small and below the historical average (0.23 pounds). Yellow perch growth rates in MFCR were generally below the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-3. The 2006 and 2005 year classes each comprised 50% of the yellow perch captured in the 2008 gillnets and trapnets.

Walleye were absent in 2008 gillnets and trapnets. The 2003 walleye gillnet catch rate was 4.40 fish/gillnet (MFCR 25th=0.20, 50th=0.75, 75th=3.63 fish/gillnet). The walleye historical catch rate is 1.80 fish/gillnet. The walleye historical average weight is 2.08 pounds from gillnets. The 2007-08 partial winterkill was probably responsible for the lack of walleye in the 2008 MFCR survey. Walleye fry were recently stocked (1.7 million each year) in the MFCR during 2004, 2006, and 2008.

Bluegill numbers were moderate to abundant in 2008 (22.80 fish/trapnet) compared to the normal ranges for MFCR (25th=1.40, 50th=3.60, 75th=10.67 fish/trapnet) and similar lakes. The bluegill historical average catch rate is 7.96 fish/trapnet. The 2008 bluegill average size was small (0.10 pounds and 4.97 inches) from trapnets. The bluegill historical average weight is 0.22 pounds from trapnets. The 2008 catch rate of quality size (6.00 inches) and larger bluegill was low (1.87 fish/trapnet). Bluegill growth rates were within the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-5. The 2005 year class comprised 66% of the total bluegill catch in the 2008 trapnets. Bluegill adults were last stocked in 2004 (1,365 fish, 195 pounds).

Other sunfish species were present in moderate numbers for the MFCR during 2008. Pumpkinseed sunfish numbers were abundant in 2008 (4.93 fish/trapnet) compared to the normal range for similar lakes. The pumpkinseed sunfish historical average catch rate is 6.04 fish/trapnet for MFCR. The 2008 MFCR pumpkinseed sunfish average size was small (4.47 inches and 0.11 pounds). The MFCR pumpkinseed sunfish historical average weight is 0.17 pounds. Hybrid sunfish numbers were high in 2008 (3.47 fish/trapnet) compared to the historical average (1.83 fish/trapnet). The hybrid sunfish average size was small (4.18 inches and 0.08 pounds) in 2008. The hybrid sunfish historical average weight is 0.33 pounds in MFCR.

Black bullhead numbers were abundant in 2008 (43.00 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal range for MFCR (25th=27.40, 50th=28.00, 75th=43.00 fish/gillnet). The black bullhead historical average catch rate is 32.49 fish/gillnet for MFCR. The 2008 black bullhead average weight was moderate to small (0.27 pounds) from gillnets. The black bullhead historical average weight is 0.26 pounds from gillnets. Black bullhead sampled from 2008 nets appeared in poor condition.

Yellow bullhead numbers were low in 2008 (2.20 fish/trapnet) compared to the normal range for the MFCR (25th=3.50, 50th=18.13, 75th=20.47 fish/trapnet). The 2008 yellow bullhead average weight was moderate to small (0.24 pounds). The yellow bullhead historical average weight is 0.29 pounds.

Other fish species of special interest captured in the 2008 nets included abundant numbers of carp (5.13 fish/trapnet) and white sucker (4.40 fish/gillnet). The carp and white sucker historical average catch rates were 2.68 carp/trapnet and 2.82 white sucker/gillnet respectively for MFCR. The 2008 carp and white sucker average weights were 9.28 pounds and 2.72 pounds respectively. The carp and white sucker historical average weights were 9.76 pounds and 2.38 pounds respectively.

Current fish management activities on MFCR include monitoring the fish population on a periodic basis, monitoring winter dissolved oxygen levels, assisting local residents and Kandiyohi County in the operation of the aeration system, protecting aquatic vegetation through the permit process, participating in local watershed initiatives, and stocking various fish species as warranted. A winterkill assessment will be conducted during the 2009 spring to determine the extent of winterkill due to low oxygen levels. The MFCR fishery will be assessed in the 2013 summer for all fish species. ?
