* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/23/2004)

Sixth Crow Wing Lake is located three miles southwest of Nevis in southern Hubbard County. Sixth Crow Wing has a surface area of 321 acres and a maximum depth of 40 feet. A state-owned public access with a concrete ramp is located on the southwest shore of the lake. Sixth Crow Wing is connected to Fifth Crow Wing Lake by a small channel (Crow Wing River), and boating is possible between the two lakes. Sixth Crow Wing supports an excellent panfish fishery, as well as good fishing for largemouth bass, northern pike, and walleye. Sixth Crow Wing has good water quality and excellent fish habitat such as submerged and emergent aquatic vegetation. Hardstem bulrush beds ring the south shore and the outlet areas and provide excellent cover for bass and panfish.

The Minnesota Department of Natural Resources (DNR) has classified Minnesota's lakes into 43 different types based on physical and chemical characteristics. Sixth Crow Wing is in class 31. Other area lakes in this same classification include: Second Crow Wing, Fifth Crow Wing, Seventh Crow Wing, Eighth Crow Wing, Duck, Hinds, and Lower Twin.

Panfish are highly sought after year-around on Sixth Crow Wing. Moderate numbers of bluegill were sampled (12.4 bluegill/trapnet), down slightly from past surveys, however, still within the range "typical" for this lake class. Anglers will find good numbers of bluegill in the 6-8 inch size range. Black crappie abundance in Sixth Crow Wing has fluctuated from low to high numbers over the years. Black crappie were sampled in moderate numbers in 2004 and anglers will find good numbers of crappie in the 9-11 inch size range. Both bluegill and black crappie had good growth rates when compared to other class 31 lakes.

Good numbers and sizes of walleye were sampled during the 2004 survey. The walleye catch rate of 8.5 walleye/gillnet is above the current management goal of 7.0 walleye/gillnet and above the range "typical" for this lake class. Past surveys have shown that walleye abundance has been high since the late 1980's, with low numbers of walleye sampled in the 1976 and 1983 surveys. Sampled walleye had an average length and weight of 17.5 inches and 2.0 pounds. The walleye population in Sixth Crow Wing is being maintained by a combination of fingerling stocking by the DNR as well as some sporadic natural reproduction. Age analysis showed that walleye fingerling stocking is contributing heavily to the population in Sixth Crow Wing with 74% of the sampled walleye corresponding to stocked years. Sixth Crow Wing is stocked with walleye fingerlings during odd years. Important walleye and northern pike forage species such as yellow perch and tullibee (cisco) were sampled in good numbers.

Sixth Crow Wing has a history of having a low to moderate northern pike population composed of quality-sized fish. The 2004 catch rate of 2.0 northern pike/gillnet is below the range "typical" for this lake class, but similar to past surveys. Northern pike up to 38.1 inches were sampled. Northern pike have very fast growth rates in Sixth Crow Wing. With the backing of strong public support, a 40-inch minimum size limit was implemented in the spring of 2003.

Sixth Crow Wing supports a good largemouth bass population. Past and present surveys have shown Sixth Crow Wing to have good numbers of largemouth bass when compared to other lakes within the Crow Wing chain and other class 31 lakes. Largemouth bass up to 19.9 inches were sampled.

Other species sampled included moderate numbers of bowfin (dogfish), white sucker, and yellow bullhead. Brown bullhead, rock bass, and golden shiner were sampled in low numbers.
