Status of the Fishery (as of 06/08/1998)

Black Bear is a 220 acre lake located about two miles west of Trommald, in Crow Wing County. There is a public access located on the north shore. Development in 1998 included about 7.9 homes/cabins per shoreline mile.

The lake has a maximum depth of 48 feet and about 25% of the surface area is less than 15 feet deep. Black Bear has moderately hard water and low phosphorus fertility. Water clarity is somewhat above average for the area. Soils in water less than four feet deep are mostly sand and marl, with areas of gravel and muck also present.

The aquatic plant community is fairly diverse. Plants grew to a depth of eighteen feet in 1998. Healthy aquatic plant communities are important. Emergent plants, like bulrush, cattail and wild rice help regulate nutrient levels and often provide quality spawning habitat for northern pike, bass and panfish. Submergent species provide food and cover for a wide variety of aquatic species.

Black Bear was test netted in the summer of 1998. The lake is currently a reference lake for an experimental regulation study. As such, the bass population is frequently monitored in spring, but no special regulations are in place.

Northern pike were the most abundant gamefish in 1998. The catch rate of 8.5/gill net was typical when compared to past nettings of the lake, and is in the "average" category when compared to similar type lakes. Seven year classes of pike were sampled and growth was good. The 1996 and 1995 year classes were most common in the catch.

The walleye catch was in the "low" category, when compared to similar type lakes but was similar to past catches in Black Bear. Two walleyes were caught. Migration from the Mississippi River is a likely source for walleyes found in the lake.

Largemouth bass are poorly sampled in stationary nets and the summer catch was low. Spring electrofishing samples collected in 1996, '97 and '98 show that a good bass population is present. Bass as large as 19 inches were present in spring samples.

Bluegill abundance appears to be fairly stable. The '98 catch rate was similar to past catches and was in the "average" category when compared to similar type lakes. Eight year classes were sampled and growth was good. About 17% of bluegills were at least 7.0 inches long.

Black crappies were caught most readily in gill nets in '98. A large majority of crappies were in the six-inch length group and from the 1994 year class. Crappie growth was slow.

Important forage species in Black Bear include yellow perch and tullibee. Perch have had low catch rates in past nettings. The '98 catch was the highest to date and the first to be "average" compared to similar type lakes. Tullibees have been and continue to be present in "high" numbers compared to similar type lakes.

Other species sampled in 1998 included: bowfin (dogfish), black bullhead, yellow bullhead, rock bass, green sunfish, pumpkinseed, hybrid sunfish and common shiner. Abundances were generally in the "low" or "average" categories when compared to similar type lakes.
