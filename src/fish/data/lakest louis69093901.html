* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/22/2002)

Sturgeon Lake is a 1664 acre lake in West-Central St. Louis County. It is a low fertility lake managed for walleye, northern pike and bluegill. The Sturgeon chain of lakes, because of its close proximity to Hibbing and Chisholm, receives moderately heavy recreational use. There is one public access within the state park and a private access in the southwest bay.

The walleye gill net catch rate was well below the expected range. While this was the lowest catch rate yet observed on Sturgeon Lake, catch rates have typically been low. The walleye that were sampled were large, averaging 2.4 lbs. They ranged from 13.2 to 25.2 inches long and had an average length of 18.8 inches

The northern pike gill net catch rate exceeded the expected range for this type of lake and was within the range seen in past assessments. Northern pike sampled in gill nets had a mean weight of 2.2 pounds. They ranged from 9.3 to 34.2 inches long and had an average length of 21.1 inches. Growth rates appeared to be normal for northern pike in Sturgeon Lake.

The bluegill trap net catch rate was within the expected range and was within the range observed in the past assessments. Bluegill in trap nets ranged from 3.1 - 7.6 inches long and had a mean length of 4.6 inches. Bluegill in Sturgeon Lake appear to grow slowly.

The yellow perch gill net catch rate was below the expected range. Yellow perch catch rates have typically been low in the past. Gill net sampled yellow perch ranged from 5.3 to 7.9 inches long and had a mean length of 6.3 inches.

The black crappie gill net catch rate was within the expected range but the trap net catch rate is below the expected range. Gill net sampled fish had a mean length of 9.8 inches and trap net captured fish had a mean length of 6.8 inches.

Other fish sampled include: brown bullhead, golden and shorthead redhorse, golden shiner, largemouth bass, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, tullibee (cisco) and white sucker.

In order to maintain or improve fish and wildlife populations, water quality and habitat must be protected. People often associate water quality problems with large-scale agricultural, forestry, urban development or industrial practices in the watershed. In reality, the impact of land use decisions on one lake lot may seem relatively small, yet the combined effects of many lakeshore owners "fixing up" their property can result in a significant decline in water quality and habitat. For example, removing shoreline and aquatic vegetation, installing sand beach blankets, fertilizing lawns, mowing to the waters edge, uncontrolled run-off and failing septic systems all contribute excess nutrients and sediment which degrade water quality and habitat. Understanding these cumulative impacts and taking steps to avoid or minimize them will help to insure our quality fisheries can be enjoyed by future generations. Refer to http://www.shorelandmanagement.org/ for more information.
