Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/28/2008)

Loon Lake is a class 43 lake with considerable agricultural row crop drainage. Prior to 1979, when a helixor aeration system was installed, Loon Lake had a history of winterkill. Dissolved oxygen readings during most winters since 1979 have not been sufficiently low to cause extensive winterkill. This 738-acre lake, with a maximum depth of 7 feet, has a watershed to lake ratio of 27 to 1. This large catchment area for the lake might explain some of the poor water quality values from the lake over the years. In 2002, the MN PCA conducted water quality assessments on several southwestern MN lakes and found total phosphorus levels in Loon Lake that was nearly 5 times higher than the normal upper range of the Western Corn Belt Plains Ecoregion. Loon Lake is managed primarily for walleye and secondarily for yellow perch and northern pike.

The walleye population abundance has not changed much since the last survey in 2004. Although the walleye population was within the expected range for this type of lake, it is not attaining the long-range management goal. Nearly 40,000 walleye fingerlings have been stocked into Loon Lake since 2005 with little change in their abundance. The condition of walleye was slightly below the desired range although growth rates were average. There didnt appear to be a year class that was more abundant with total lengths ranging from 7.2 to 27.6 inches. As this report is being written during the winter of 08-09, Loon Lake has experienced lower dissolved oxygen readings. It is unclear if the lake experienced a partial winterkill or not. Although walleye numbers are not exceedingly high, it is still possible to catch a few walleyes.

Historically yellow perch catch rates have ranged from 1.7 to 25.7 fish/set. The 1993 survey found abundant yellow perch, but numbers have remained low since. Total lengths of yellow perch ranged from 5.9 to 10.5 inches with a mean of 9.3 inches.

A total of 3 northern pike were sampled in both gill nets and trap nets despite 3 different stockings of northern pike fingerlings in 2004, 2005, and 2007. Historically northern pike abundance appears to be variable and might be most dependent on climatic conditions if brood stock is present. Targeting any cooler water during summer may be a key spot to catch a few northerns.

The channel catfish catch rate in gill nets was 10.0 fish/set, which was the highest ever-documented on Loon Lake. Total lengths ranged from 9.2 to 25.8 inches with an average of 21.0 inches. Most of the fish sampled were 13-15 inches and 5-6 years old. The lack of harsh winters the last 10 years has likely contributed to a naturally reproducing channel catfish population. If you fish Loon Lake, you will have good odds of catching a few catfish. It is possible that channel catfish could be controlling black bullhead numbers. Good numbers of 9-10 inch bullheads can be caught with some regularity.

Other species sampled were bigmouth buffalo, black crappie, bluegill, common carp, freshwater drum, largemouth bass, shortnose gar, white bass, and white sucker.

Efforts to improve the health of the lake should focus on best management practices within the watershed. Improvements and education in land stewardship will often have secondary benefits to the lake in the way of quality and quantity of habitat. Generally, improvements in the quality and quantity of lake habitat will mean direct improvements of the fishery.
