Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/15/2005)

Swan Lake is a 2,472 acre lake located on the south side of Pengilly, MN. The lake has two public access and the shoreline is highly developed with approximately 500 homes/cabins. The 2001 lake management plan indicates walleye and northern pike as the primary species of management with black crappie as secondary species.

The walleye catch rate (7.7 fish/net) did not attain the management goal of 10.0 fish/net but was identical to the 2000 catch. Previous assessments have found catch rates from as low as 2.0 in 1985 to as high as 11.1 fish/net in 1954. In 2005, the sampled fish ranged from 6.9 to 26.4 inches and had an average length of 12.6 inches.

Ten year classes were identified from scale analysis with fish from one to 14 years old. Walleye from one to four years old were well represented in the sample. The strong 2004 and 2001year classes corresponded to years with fry stocking. However, both 2003 and 2002 year classes were well represented in the sample. It is clear that natural reproduction is contributing to the walleye population but it is impossible to determine to what extent since fry are not marked. Many of the fish produced in years with fry stocking (for example 2004 & 2001) may be a result of natural reproduction. Growth was similar to statewide averages. Walleye averaged 14.0 inches after four years of growth.

In general, northern pike catch rates have increased from being below the 1st quartile of 3.0 fish/net from 1948 through 1975 to being above the 3rd quartile of 7.9 fish/net in 1995 and 2000. In 2000, the northern pike catch was the highest on record at 9.8 fish/net but has since declined to 6.9 fish/net in 2005. The catch exceeded the management goal catch rate of 5.0 fish/net and was near the size structure goals established for the lake. Fish ranged from 13.8 to 33.8 inches and averaged 22.9 inches. The long-term trend of higher numbers of northern pike may be in response to angler harvest.

The black crappie trap-net catch was 0.3 fish/net which was below average for this type of lake. Catch rates have fluctuated from 0.3 to 1.3 fish/net since 1975. In 2005, the fish ranged from 8.9 to 12.3 inches and averaged 10.2 inches. Black crappie were not aged in the 2005 assessment.

The bluegill population has never been very abundant with catch rates never attaining the lake class average. The 2005 trap-net catch (1.1/net) was below the normal range for this lake type. Bluegill ranged from 3.5 to 8.1 inches with an average length of 6.7 inches. Age and growth information was not collected in this assessment.

Yellow perch catch rates have fluctuated from a low of 10.4 fish/net in 1954 to a high of 63.0 fish/net in 1984. The catch increased from 11.2 fish/net in 2000 to 30.2 fish/net in 2005. The catch was near the 3rd quartile of 33.9 fish/net for lakes similar to Swan Lake. At this time, it appears the yellow perch population is more important in the fish community as a prey source than for anglers to target.

Tullibee are a species that can be difficult to sample but are important to fish communities as a prey source. The catch rate declined from 8.4 fish/net in 2000 to 2.7 fish/net in this assessment. Although the 2005 catch was the lowest on record for Swan Lake, it remained above the lake class average of 1.6 fish/net. Tullibee catch rates have been as low as 2.7 fish/net and as high as 22.1 fish/net with no apparent trends.

Other species observed during the population assessment included bigmouth buffalo, black bullhead, bowfin, burbot, common shiner, golden shiner, largemouth bass, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, shorthead redhorse, white sucker, and yellow bullhead.

In order to maintain or improve fish and wildlife populations, water quality and habitat must be protected. People often associate water quality problems with large-scale agricultural, forestry, urban development or industrial practices in the watershed. In reality, the impact of land use decisions on one lake lot may be relatively small, yet, the cumulative impact of those decisions on many lake lots can result in a significant decline in water quality and habitat. For example, removing shoreline and aquatic vegetation, fertilizing lawns, mowing to the water's edge, installing beach sand blankets, failing septic systems and uncontrolled run-off, all contribute excess nutrients and sediment which degrade water quality and habitat. Understanding these cumulative impacts and taking steps to avoid or minimize them will help to insure our quality fisheries can be enjoyed by future generations.
