* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/06/2004)

Jeanette Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 16, which consists of 38 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are very large, shallow, and have bog-stained and very soft (unmineralized) water. Jeanette Lake is smaller (580 acres) than the lake class average of 1,242 acres.

Jeanette Lake was not thermally stratified on 07/06/2004 and retained good oxygen to within a foot of the bottom. Jeanette Lake has no inlets from other lakes, and the outlet has boulder drops and falls that prevent fish immigration. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline are 40 % boulder, 22% gravel, 15% muck, 12% ledge, 9% sand, and 2% rubble. Aquatic plants are sparse and grow to a depth of 3 ft.

The 2004 fish population assessment consisted of 9 gillnet sets and 9 trapnet sets. Twelve previous fisheries investigations, dating back to 1973, used 1-12 gillnet sets. Six of these previous investigations used 2-8 trapnet sets. Trapnets in Jeanette Lake have historically caught very few fish.

Fish populations in 2004, as in previous investigations on Jeanette Lake, were dominated by walleye, white sucker, yellow perch, and northern pike. No other fish species were captured in 2004.

Walleye numbers in 2004 (10.4/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 8.7/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes in 2004 averaged 13.9" (1.1 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 13.5" in all investigations on this lake. The largest walleye captured in 2004 was 26.4". Most walleye captured in 2004 were ages two and three, from the 2002 and 2001 year classes. Growth of walleye captured in 2004 was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile) by area standards.

Yellow perch numbers in 2004 (20.1/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median catch of 22.6/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Perch sizes in 2004 averaged 8.2" (0.3 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 7.7" in all investigations on this lake. The largest perch captured in 2004 was 12.1". Most perch captured in 2004 were ages two and three, from the 2002 and 2001 year classes. Growth of perch captured in 2004 was faster than normal (in the fourth quartile) by area standards.

Northern pike numbers in 2004 (5.2/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 2.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2004 averaged 20.6" (1.9 lb), which was near the median for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 21.0" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike captured in 2004 was 31.9". Most pike captured in 2004 were ages two through four. Growth of pike captured in 2004 was normal (in the second or third quartiles) by area standards.

Some of the game fish examined in 2004 were infected with neascus. A few of the perch also had yellow grub. Neascus (black spot) and yellow grub are common parasites that are native to the area. They cannot infect humans, are often removed when filleting the fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.
