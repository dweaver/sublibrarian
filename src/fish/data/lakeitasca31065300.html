* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/22/2005)

North Star Lake is a 1,059 acre lake located just south of Marcell, MN. The lake has one public access and moderate shoreline development. The 2001 lake management plan indicates muskellunge and walleye as the primary species of management with northern pike and smallmouth bass as secondary species.

Muskellunge are rarely sampled with our standard summer assessment gear and none were captured in 2005. However, while conducting spring electrofishing for largemouth and smallmouth bass, three yearling muskellunge and two five year old fish were sampled. In addition, four other muskellunge less than 40 inches long were observed. A special large trap net assessment was conducted during the spring of 2005 to better assess the population.

The walleye catch rate of 4.3 fish/net did not attain the aggressive management goal of 6.0 fish/net. However, the catch was the second highest on record behind the 5.2 fish/net in 1984. Since walleye were stocked on a regular basis beginning in 1972, the catch rates have varied from 2.3 to 5.2. In 2005, the sampled fish ranged from 7.5 to 27.2 inches and averaged16.0 inches.

Walleye age and growth information was determined from scale and opercle analysis. Seven year-classes were identified with fish from one to 13 years old present. The 1998 year class accounted for 70% of the sample in 2001 and 22% in 2005. Three other year classes were also well represented: 2000 (24%), 2001 (20%), and 2002 (18%). The 2001 assessment evaluated a period of reduced stocking and it was clear that stocking was supporting the walleye population in North Star Lake, as nearly all fish corresponded with years of stocking. In 2001, growth was similar to statewide averages and walleye averaged 18.0 inches after four years of growth. The 2005 assessment evaluated a period of frequent stocking (1998, 2000, 2001, 2002, and 2004) and found higher gill net catch rates but reduced growth. Although growth was still within the normal range, age-4 fish only averaged 13.9 inches. In addition to more fish competing for food, at least one of the summer growing seasons prior to the 2005 assessment was unusually cold and likely affected the growth rates observed.

North Star Lake has had a low to moderate density northern pike population with moderate size structure in most assessments. In the spring of 2004, a 24-36 inch protective slot length limit for northern pike was implemented to improve or at least maintain the quality of the pike population. Northern pike catch rates have ranged from a low of 1.4 fish/net in 1949 to a high of 5.2 fish/net in 1979. In 2005, the northern pike catch was 1.9 fish/net, down from 4.7 fish/net four years earlier. Fish ranged from 14.9 to 29.5 inches and had a mean length of 22.6 inches. Six year-classes were identified from cliethra and scales ranging from two to seven years old. Northern pike averaged 22.9 inches after four years of growth compared to the statewide average of 20.5 inches.

In 2005, spring electrofishing was conducted which resulted in 30 largemouth bass and four smallmouth bass being sampled. Largemouth bass were caught at a rate of 15.4 fish/hour (on-time) compared to 28.8 fish/hour in 2001. The sampled fish ranged from 4.7 to 11.7 inches and had a mean length of 7.2 inches. Scale analysis indicated three age classes from two to four years old. Age-3 fish represented more than 57% of the sample. Mean back-calculated length-at-ages were within 15% of the statewide mean but generally slower.

The bluegill population increased from a low of 1.8 fish/trap net in 1949 to an all-time high of 27.7 fish/trap net in 1975. In 2005, the trap net catch of 12.7 fish/net declined from 20.8 fish/net in 2001. The catch fell below the lake class median of 17.3 fish/net after being above it for only the second time. Bluegill ranged from 3.2 to 7.0 inches with a mean length of 4.7 inches. Age and growth information was not collected in this assessment.

Yellow perch gill-net catch rates have fluctuated from a low of 2.0 fish/net in 1996 to a high of 30.9 fish/net in 1984. The population has generally been sampled in low numbers with the next highest catch at 7.5 fish/net. In 2005, the catch was 6.7 fish/gill net, up slightly from 2001 (5.4 fish/net). The catch rates were both between the lake class 1st quartile and median. Age and growth information was not collected in this assessment.

Tullibee can be difficult to catch because they frequently live over deep water, an area that our standard sampling gear was not designed to sample. However, they are important to fish communities as a prey source for many predator species, especially muskellunge, northern pike and walleye. Tullibee gill-net catch rates have been as low as 0.3 fish/net and as high as 14.5 fish/net with no apparent trend in numbers. In 2001, the tullibee catch was 8.2 fish/net which exceeded the 3rd quartile of 6.5 fish/net for class 25 lakes. In 2005, the catch declined to 0.9 fish/gill net which was near the 1st quartile. The fish ranged from 6.3 to 12.1 inches and had a mean length of 8.3 inches.

Other species observed during the population assessment included black crappie, golden shiner, hybrid sunfish, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, white sucker, and yellow bullhead.

In order to maintain or improve fish and wildlife populations, water quality and habitat must be protected. People often associate water quality problems with large-scale agricultural, forestry, urban development or industrial practices in the watershed. In reality, the impact of land use decisions on one lake lot may be relatively small, yet, the cumulative impact of those decisions on many lake lots can result in a significant decline in water quality and habitat. For example, removing shoreline and aquatic vegetation, fertilizing lawns, mowing to the water's edge, installing beach sand blankets, failing septic systems and uncontrolled run-off, all contribute excess nutrients and sediment which degrade water quality and habitat. Understanding these cumulative impacts and taking steps to avoid or minimize them will help to insure our quality fisheries can be enjoyed by future generations.
