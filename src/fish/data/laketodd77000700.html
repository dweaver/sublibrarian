Status of the Fishery (as of 08/08/2005)

Mound Lake is a relatively small, scenic lake in southeast Todd County which supports a diverse fish community and offers many angling opportunities. The lake has excellent water clarity with secchi disk reading of 18 feet in August which allows the growth of aquatic vegetation down to 20 feet. The most common submerged plants, plants which grow primarily under the water surface, in Mound Lake were northern water milfoil and coontail which are both native to Minnesota. For more indepth information, a vegetation report was conducted in June of 2005 and can be found at the DNR website under Lakefinder. " Aquatic Vegetation of Mound Lake, Todd County by Donna Perleberg". Though only 273 acres in size, the lake offers a shoreline diversity ranging from heavy residential development on the south and east shores, to steep slopes covered by mixed hardwoods on the north shore, to quiet isolated bays good for watching wildlife. Sand was the most common shallow water bottom type found around the lake. There appeared to be several changes to the fish communtity since the last survey in 1999. Northern pike numbers showed a decrease as did black crappie abundance. With the decrease in pike numbers, size structure of the population appears to have improved. More northern pike over 24 inches were observed in the summer catch and some trophy size fish near 40 inches have been netted in other DNR assessments on the lake. Harvest of the smaller pike is encouraged to maintain a healthy pike population of fast growing individuals by reducing competition for food. Mound Lake had been known for it's quality black crappie population but numbers appear to have seriously declined since the last survey with no crappies being netted in the summer survey and far fewer in the spring assesment. Local residents reported a good crappie bite in the winter of 2004 - 05 which may negatively affected the population. While there are still some of the quality crappies left, the numbers were down from previous surveys. Crappie growth has been excellent but it will take some time for the population to rebound as few young crappies were observed. Both largemouth and smallmouth bass are present in Mound Lake with the largemouth being the more abundant species. Average size of the largemouth bass was between 12 to 13 inches but bass up to 18 inches were netted in the spring and fish up to 20 inches have been observed in past assessments. The smallmouth bass that have been caught were between 15 and 19 inches. The species was first documented in the lake in 2003 and abundance appears to be increasing. Walleye abundance appears to be normal when compared to similar type lakes but local residents report catching few. Average length seen in the lake survey was about 19 inches but fish up to 29 inches have been observed as well as ones down to 13 inches. Walleye fingerlings are stocked in the lake in even numbered years which seems to be sustaining the population. With the clear water, anglers may have more luck fishing at night. Yellow perch numbers continue to be low which may have been a contributing factor in the decrease in northern pike numbers. An abundant perch population is desirable as it may increase survival of walleye fingerlings and promote fast growth rates for both northern pike and walleye. If anglers are not harvesting perch for a meal, releasing the fish immediately back into the water is encouraged. It is hoped that the perch population may rebound if the northern pike numbers continue to stay down for a period of time. Bluegill abundance is hard to discern from the lake survey as the gear type used was not meant for panfish. An earlier spring netting did show some quality size fish up to 9 inches but average size was about 6 inches. Other species sampled during the survey included rock bass, hybrid sunfish, pumpkinseed, brown bullheads, and yellow bullheads. Some very nice size bullheads over 11 inches of both species were observed. Bowfin, white sucker, golden shiners, and green sunfish are also known to be present in the lake as they were netted in the spring assessment. Neascus or black spot was common on many of the northern pike, walleye, yellow perch, and rock bass. Neascus is a trematode or parasite that is usually found in the skin of the fish. The life cycle of the parasite includes snails and fish-eating birds, such as herons and bitterns. Skinning the fish removes most of the parasites and cooking will kill the rest. Humans cannot be infested. Proper land use practices are encouraged in the watershed and by lakeshore owners to protect and maintain the good water quality and fish habitat in Mound Lake. Protecting fish habitat is important to help sustain the quality and balance of the fish communtity. Information on shoreline regulations, restoration, and best management practices can be found at the Todd County Planning and Zoning website as well as the DNR website. www.co.todd.mn.us/PlanningZoning/planning.htm and www.dnr.state.mn.us
