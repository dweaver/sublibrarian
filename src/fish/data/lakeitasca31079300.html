Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/11/2007)

Big Too Much Lake is located just north of Talmoon, MN in the Chippewa National Forest and the Big Fork River watershed. The lake has a surface area of 232 acres and a maximum depth of 95 feet. Big Too Much Lake is in ecological lake class #23. Other area lakes in this class include LaCroix, Hatch and Clubhouse Lakes. The US Forest Service maintains a concrete ramp boat access at the northwest corner of the lake.

The northern pike gill-net catch rate in the 2007 assessment was 3.8/net. This is within the lake class normal range and similar to the previous assessment. Northern pike size structure was good. Sampled pike ranged from 19.1 to 36.7 inches, with an average length of 25.3 inches and an average weight of 4.1 pounds. Growth was above the statewide average for ages 1-5, and similar to the statewide average for ages 6-9.

Big Too Much Lake has historically supported a modest walleye population through natural reproduction, in addition to occasional stocking. Only one walleye was captured in the most recent assessment for a catch rate of 0.2/net. This catch rate is below the lake class normal range but similar to recent assessments. The one walleye sampled was large, with a length of 28.9 inches and a weight of 8.9 lbs.

The gill-net catch rate for tullibee remained above the expected range at 25.3/net. Sampled fish ranged from 8.0 to 13.4 inches, with an average length of 11.8 inches. Tullibee are likely an important prey source for the large northern pike and walleye in Big Too Much Lake.

The trap-net catch rate for bluegill was high at 45.9/net. This is slightly above the expected range for this lake class and the highest catch recorded for Big Too Much Lake. Size structure was poor; sampled bluegill ranged from 2.9 to 7.9 inches, with an average length of 5.6 inches. Growth was similar to the lake class average for all ages.

The gill-net catch rate for yellow perch was also the highest on record at 9.3/net. This catch rate is above the lake class median (4.6/net) but still within the normal range. Sampled perch ranged from 5.1 to 7.0 inches, with an average length of 5.9 inches.

Black crappie were sampled at 3.6/trap net, above the lake class normal range and the highest catch on record. Size structure was modest; sampled crappie ranged from 7.0 to 9.2 inches, with an average length of 8.3 inches.

A night electrofishing assessment for largemouth bass was also conducted in 2007. This is the second time this type of sampling has been used on Big Too Much Lake. The 2007 catch rate of 83.5 fish/hour of on-time is considered relatively high and much higher than the 1999 catch rate of 15.0/hour. Spring electrofishing catches are often highly affected by local weather conditions around the time of sampling. In addition, small bass (< 8 inches) made up a large portion of the 2007 catch, indicating strong recruitment of bass in recent years. These two factors may explain much of the difference in catches between the two sampling periods. As in 1999, size structure of largemouth bass in 2007 was relatively modest. A strong 2005 year class (age 2 fish) made up 58% of the sample. Sampled largemouth ranged from 3.8 to 17.0 inches, with an average length of 8.8 inches. Ages 1 through 9 were represented in the sample. Growth was below the statewide average for ages 1 and 2, and similar to the statewide average for all other ages. ?
