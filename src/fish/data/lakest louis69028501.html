Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/05/2005)

Eagles Nest Lakes 1 & 2 are in Ecological Lake Class 5, which consists of 64 lakes in northeast Minnesota that have clear and soft (unmineralized) water. Eagles Nest Lakes 1 & 2 are broadly connected by a navigable channel 75 ft wide and 10 ft deep. Since fish populations are similar and fish may move freely between the two lakes, they are managed as a single body of water.

Eagles Nest Lake 1, with a surface area of 322 acres, total alkalinity of 50 ppm, and Secchi water clarity of 11 ft, is typical of lakes in Lake Class 5. Eagles Nest Lake 1 is deeper (76 ft) than the lake class average of 44 ft, and has a smaller proportion littoral area (27 percent) than the lake class average of 38 percent. Eagles Nest Lake 1 ranks as mesotrophic according to Carlson's Trophic State Index, with chlorophyll-a of 2.9 ppb and total phosphorus of 30 ppb.

Eagles Nest Lake 1 was thermally stratified on 08/15/2005 with a surface temperature of 70 F and bottom temperature of 45 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 40 ft, where the temperature was 48 F.

Eagles Nest Lake 2, with a surface area of 408 acres, maximum depth of 39 ft, total alkalinity of 52 ppm, and Secchi water clarity of 11 ft, is typical of lakes in Lake Class 5. Eagles Nest Lake 2 has a larger proportion of littoral area (56 percent) than the lake class average of 38 percent, and has a higher Shoreline Development Factor (2.76) than the lake class average of 1.87. Eagles Nest Lake 2 ranks as mesotrophic according to Carlson's Trophic State Index with chlorophyll-a of 5.0 ppb and total phosphorus of 28 ppb.

Eagles Nest Lake 2 was thermally stratified on 08/15/2005 with a surface temperature of 70 F and a bottom temperature of 52 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 25 ft, where the temperature was 64 F.

The major inlet to these lakes, from Eagles Nest Lake 3, enters Eagles Nest Lake 2 on the south shore. The outlet, the East Two River to Lake Vermilion, exits Eagles Nest Lake 2 in the northwest bay. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline of both lakes are mostly rubble and gravel. Aquatic plants grow to a depth of about 15 ft, and are moderately abundant in the shallows, especially in Eagles Nest Lake 2, which has more shallow water. Aquatic plants have not been affected by rusty crayfish in these lakes as much as in some other lakes (Eagles Nest Lake 3, for example) where they are abundant.

Fish populations in 2005 were sampled with nine standard survey gillnets, twelve standard survey trapnets, three small mesh smelt gillnets set in deep water, and six electrofishing runs along the shoreline that were done at night in the fall. Nine previous investigations dating back to 1955 used 4-12 standard gillnets. Trapnets (6-12 nets) were used during five of these previous investigations. Shoreline seining or small mesh trapnetting for young-of-year fish was done in three of these investigations. Fall night electrofishing was done in the three most recent investigations. Smelt gillnetting was done in seven years from 1980 through 2005.

Fish populations in Eagles Nest Lakes 1 & 2 in 2005 were dominated by walleye, bluegill sunfish, smallmouth bass, and rainbow smelt. Fair numbers of northern pike, yellow perch, and black crappie were also present.

Walleye numbers in 2005 (13.6/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median walleye catch of 6.6/gillnet in all investigations on these lakes. Walleye fingerlings have been stocked every other year (in odd-numbered years), and 102 of 122 (84 percent) of walleye captured in 2005 were from years stocked with fingerlings. Many of these walleye (48 percent) were from a strong 2001 year class. Walleye sizes in 2005 averaged 19.3" (3.0 lb), which was in the fourth quartile for this lake class and was larger than the median size of 18.3" in all investigations on these lakes. The largest walleye was 29.7" (10.6 lb). Growth of walleye captured in 2005 was faster than normal (in the fourth quartile) by area standards.

Bluegill numbers were fairly low in the first two investigations on these lakes: 2.2/trapnet in 1955 and 10.5/trapnet in 1980. Bluegill numbers then exploded, with catches of 106/trapnet in 1990, 130/trapnet in 1995, 54/trapnet in 2000, and 68/trapnet in 2005. Bluegill are more vulnerable to trapnetting early in the summer, and trapnetting dates in these lakes was switched from mid August in early investigations through 1990, to mid July in 1995 and later investigations. The fact that high numbers of bluegill were captured in 1990, prior to switching the trapnetting date, means that the increase in bluegill catches represents a real increase in the bluegill population.

Bluegill sizes in 2005 averaged 6.1" (0.19 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 5.6" in all investigations on these lakes. The largest bluegill captured in 2005 was 9.3". Bluegill year classes from 1998 through 2002 were all well represented. Growth of bluegill captured in 2005 was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile) by area standards.

Smallmouth bass numbers in 2005 (3.8/gillnet, 1.3/trapnet) were in the fourth quartiles for this lake class and were higher than the median smallmouth catches of 3.2/gillnet and 0.6/trapnet in all investigations on these lakes. Bass are "net shy" and difficult to sample well in standard gillnets and trapnets. Good numbers of smallmouth (39.1/hour of on-time) were caught during electrofishing in 2005. Smallmouth sizes in 2005 averaged 14.9" in the gillnets, 9.2" in the trapnets, and 7.7" in the electrofishing. The largest smallmouth (caught in a gillnet) was 18.4". Growth of smallmouth captured in 2005 was slower than normal (in the first or second quartile, depending on the age) by area standards. Largemouth bass are also present in these lakes, but in lower numbers than smallmouth. Largemouth up to 18" have been caught during electrofishing.

Smelt were first observed in Eagles Nest Lakes 1 & 2 in 1980. Smelt numbers in Eagles Nest Lake 1 in 2005 (98/smelt net) were higher than in Eagles Nest Lake 2 (3/smelt net). This is similar to previous smelt investigations in these lakes since smelt were introduced. Smelt are a cold-water species and Eagles Nest Lake 1 is deeper and has more cold water with adequate oxygen in the summer. Many of the walleye examined in these lakes in previous investigations had smelt in their stomachs. Smelt are a rich, oily food source, and are largely responsible for the fast growth of walleye in these lakes. Smelt are native to the Atlantic Ocean and made their way through the Great Lakes. They were likely introduced to Eagles Nest Lakes 1 & 2 intentionally, or by smelt netters from Lake Superior releasing fertilized eggs into the lakes while cleaning their gear.

Northern pike numbers in 2005 (2.3/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 4.6/gillnet in all investigations on these lakes. Pike sizes in 2005 averaged 24.1" (3.6 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 21.0" in all investigations on these lakes. The largest pike caught in 2005 was 39.6". Pike scales were moderately difficult to read and growth was too inconsistent to draw conclusions about growth rates.

Black crappie numbers in 2005 (3.3/trapnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 2.2/trapnet in all investigations on these lakes. Crappie sizes in 2005 averaged 8.3" (0.35 lb), which was at the median for this lake class, but was smaller than the average size of 8.9" in all investigations on these lakes. The largest crappie was 13.1". Most of the crappie captured in 2005 (23 of 30) were age three. Crappie growth was faster than normal (in the fourth quartile) by area standards.

Yellow perch numbers in 2005 (6.2/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 2.3/gillnet in all investigations on these lakes. Perch sizes in 2005 averaged 7.1" (0.16 lb), which was at the median for this lake class, but was smaller than the average size of 7.7" in all investigations on these lakes. Perch growth was somewhat faster than normal (in the third quartile) by area standards.

Many of the game fish examined in 2005 were infected with neascus (black spot). Many of the smallmouth bass had bass tapeworm larvae in their viscera. Some of the yellow perch had yellow grub cysts. All of these parasites are native to the area and are common. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.

Rusty crayfish were first observed in Eagles Nest Lakes 1 & 2 in 1987. They get entangled in the gillnets and have been counted in all four investigations from 1990 to 2005. Their numbers in 2005 (97/gillnet) were similar to previous counts (range: 93-116/gillnet). Rusty crayfish are native to the Ohio River valley and were likely introduced to this area by anglers using them for bait. They out compete and displace native crayfish. They are very aggressive and have been associated with the destruction of rooted aquatic vegetation in lakes and the subsequent decline of bluegill populations.
