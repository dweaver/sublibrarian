Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/19/2006)

Lake Minnewaska is a 7,110-acre lake located in central Pope County between the towns of Glenwood and Starbuck. It is the largest lake in the county and is a popular destination for many anglers and recreational boaters in west-central Minnesota.

Fish survey records provide insights into the influence of water quality on habitat and fish community composition. Land use changes in the 70s and 80s and diversion of effluent discharges from Lake Minnewaska resulted in reduced phosphorus concentrations, less frequent or severe algal blooms, much improved water clarity, expansion of aquatic vegetation, and ultimately, a much different fish community and fishery than previously existed. Vegetation dependent and sight feeding fishes such as sunfishes, largemouth and smallmouth bass, and northern pike responded favorably to habitat changes.

This lake is primarily managed for walleye with bass and sunfish being species of secondary management importance. Walleye are abundant. Catch rates in 2006 were the highest documented since 1989. Mean size of captured walleye was 17.2 inches and 2.1 pounds. Growth rates of walleye are relatively fast in Lake Minnewaska with fish typically exceeding 17.0 inches in length after their fourth growing season. Typical of most large walleye fisheries, spikes in population abundance are driven by natural reproduction. Fry and fingerling stockings by the DNR and Lake Minnewaska Association supplements sporadic natural reproduction.

Gamefishes prospering with water quality and habitat improvements include largemouth and smallmouth bass, bluegill, and northern pike. Expanded vegetative coverage affords nursery and escape cover. Improved water clarity enhances feeding efficiency and survival of sight-feeding predators like bass and pike.

Largemouth and smallmouth bass populations have greatly expanded in recent years. Growth rates are above average. Anglers can expect good catch rates of medium- to large-sized fish. With development of a high-quality bass fishery, Lake Minnewaska has become a popular choice for fishing tournaments.

Bluegill numbers have also increased but average size captured remains short of management objectives. The population tends to be dominated by medium-size individuals. Mean size of 2006 survey captures was 5.9 inches and 0.2 pounds. Sunfish fishing and harvest pressure has increased with population expansion. Size selective harvest pressures may account for limited numbers of 8.0 inch and larger fish in the population.

Too few black crappie have been captured in recent netting surveys to accurately describe population attributes, but crappie continue to support a popular fishery in spring and winter months. Fishing success and size harvested has been inconsistent in response to variable reproductive success or survival of young crappie. This member of the sunfish family is more tolerant of past habitat conditions, thus benefits of water quality improvements are less evident. Resident anglers suggest crappie were more abundant and fishing was better prior to habitat improvements and development of a more complex fish community.

Northern pike abundance has increased to support a good fishery. Climatic instability has influenced annual reproductive success, but present population attributes can be qualified as a moderately abundant population of fast growing individuals. Pike catches during the 2006 survey were largely young fish. Mean length was only 22.0 inches. However, growth is above average and pike exceeding 30.0 inches were fairly common in the total catch. Review of catch statistics from prior surveys indicates average size of northern pike has increased.

Fish community stability and above-average growth of most gamefishes is partially attributable to an abundant yellow perch prey base. In response to a balanced fish community, growth and survival rates are sufficient to allow some portion of the population to reach a harvestable size and add to fishing experiences.

Lake Minnewaska supports a wide range of fishing opportunities. Fishing pressure can be heavy at times, during both open water and winter seasons. To help maintain fishing quality, anglers are encouraged to adopt a selective harvest mindset. Release larger fish and direct harvest towards surpluses of small- to medium-size fish. Releasing medium- to large fish will ensure that the lake will have enough spawning age fish and should provide anglers with opportunities to catch more large fish in the future.

Eurasian watermilfoil and curly-leaf pondweed are present in this basin. To reduce spread of these deleterious exotics, all boaters should make concentrated efforts to remove all aquatic vegetation from their boat and trailer before leaving the public access.
