* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/28/2003)

Cedar Island Lake is located near Richmond, in south-central Stearns County. This 998 acre lake is part of the Sauk River Chain of Lakes, which was formed by the construction of a dam on the Sauk River at Cold Spring. The chain is very popular for angling and other forms of recreation.

The Sauk River drains a large portion of central Minnesota before flowing through the chain of lakes. Combined with drainage from the Long Lake sub-watershed to the south, the total watershed of the chain is nearly 600,000 acres. Although non-point sources (agricultural run-off) continue to be a concern, some major contributors or nutrients (such as municipal wastewater discharge) were greatly reduced by 1991. Efforts are being made to address existing sources, but water quality in most of the lakes remains in the hypereutrophic range.

The fishery of Cedar Island Lake is managed for walleye and northern pike, although panfish, bass, and channel catfish are also popular with many anglers. Walleye management relied on fingerling stocking in the 1980's and 1990's. In 2001, walleye fry were stocked in an attempt to increase the population, with very positive results. Netting in 2003 produced catch rates that were in the range expected for lakes similar to Cedar Island, but lower than the management goal. It is expected that with continued success of fry stocking, this goal can be reached. The 2001 year class was well represented in the catch throughout the chain, but many age 5-8 individuals were also sampled. The average size of walleye in the 2003 survey (1.5 pounds) was higher than in 1995 (1.0 pounds), but below the expected range for the lake class. Fall electrofishing showed strong evidence of success of the 2003 fry stocking, though less than in 2001. Some natural reproduction may occur within the chain or in the Sauk River, but it does not appear to be a significant contribution to the walleye population.

The catch rate of northern pike in 2003 was low compared to lakes similar to Cedar Island, but near the management goal. Low densities of northern pike usually provide higher growth rates and better survival of walleye. Mean size of northern pike sampled in 2003 was relatively high (3.1 pounds), and nearly all were greater than 20 inches.

Yellow perch abundance appeared low in 2003 for Cedar Island Lake as well as the other lakes in the Sauk River chain.

Bass were sampled by electrofishing in mid-May. The catch rate of largemouth bass was near the average for lakes in this part of the state, and much higher than for smallmouth bass. In nearby Horseshoe Lake, equal numbers of smallmouth and largemouth bass were sampled. Largemouth bass up to 18.6 inches were sampled from Cedar Island Lake; the average was 12.1 inches.

The black crappie catch rate in 2003 was lower than catch rates from the 1980's and 1990's, although the crappie fishery remains very popular. By weight, black crappie accounted for more of the fish harvested on the chain in 1999 (open water season) than any other species. Mean weight of crappie sampled in 2003 (0.32 pounds) was above the average for lakes similar to Cedar Island, and higher than in recent surveys. The catch rate of bluegill in 2003 also was lower than catch rates from the 1980's and 1990's, but average size (0.28 pounds) was high. Mean weight of bluegill was higher than reported in previous surveys, and most of the individuals sampled in 2003 were 6-8 inches in length.

Channel catfish were introduced to Horseshoe Lake in the 1980's, and first sampled in Cedar Island in the 1985 survey. Catch rates and average weight has been variable since then. The catfish sampled in 2003 were mostly 17-22 inches. Catfish sampled in the lakes downstream of Cedar Island were generally smaller. Other species sampled during the 2003 survey included white sucker, redhorse (primarily shorthead), carp and bullhead.
