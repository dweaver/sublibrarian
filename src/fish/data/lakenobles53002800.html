* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/25/2008)

Okabena Lake, in Nobles County, is a 751-acre lake located entirely within the city limits of Worthington. Okabena receives over 14,000 acres of drainage from agricultural row cropping and has a watershed to lake ratio of 19 to 1. Due mostly to historical dredging, Okabena has a maximum depth of about 14 feet and 6.5 miles of shoreline that is nearly all developed. Much of the shoreline that is not in private ownership is owned by the City of Worthington and allows public access for fishing. Walleye and channel catfish are the primary species of management along with bluegill, yellow perch, black and white crappie as secondary species of management. A population assessment was conducted on Okabena during the week of August 25, 2008 using 3 gill nets and 10 trap nets.

The Okabena walleye population continues to provide one of the better fisheries in the Windom Area. The walleye catch rate in gill nets was 23.3 fish/set, which is nearly twice the statewide normal range. Over the years, DNR survey catch rates ranged from 11.2 to 34.0 fish/set. Total lengths of walleyes in 2008 ranged from 8.2 to 25.6 inches and averaged 14.0 inches. Growth appears to be average to above average as Age 2 fish at the time of capture had a mean length of 14.0 inches. Although angling for walleye can be erratic on Okabena Lake, keeper walleye can be caught with some regularity.

The channel catfish numbers are way up, with most of the fish in the 13-17 inch range. Channel catfish have been stocked twice (2002 and 2004) with over 22,000 fingerlings and likely contributed to the high catch rate. The channel catfish catch rate in gill nets in 2008 was an astounding 30.7 fish/set compared to the 8.7 fish/set in 2006. Dont be surprised when you catch some channel catfish speed trolling for walleyes in the next few years.

The yellow perch catch rates peaked in 2006, but the catch rate in 2008 was still nearly twice the upper normal range. Total lengths ranged from 5.7 to 9.8 inches and averaged 8.2 inches. In the next couple years, some good keepers can be found in Okabena Lake.

Yellow perch and bluegills abundance tend to be inversely related. For example, bluegill and pumpkinseed were most abundant during the 2000 survey when there was a downward trend in the yellow perch abundance. Both bluegill and pumpkinseed need aquatic vegetation in order to reproduce and thrive. Okabena does not have much in the way of dense stands of aquatic vegetation and as a result tend to be limited in the reproductive capacity. Although bluegill and pumpkinseed numbers have been higher historically, an occasional bluegill and pumpkinseed can be caught near the remaining weed edges.

Both black and white crappie numbers have never been lower on Okabena Lake. In the spring of 2008 an apparent significant die-off of white crappie occurred on Okabena Lake. The fish that were collected and sent to the state pathology lab showed signs of bacterial gill disease. Typically this outbreak does not catastrophically affect the entire fish population, but based on our sampling the white crappie population may have been significantly impacted. Crappies have been known to spawn multiple times during the summer and I suspect their abundance will begin to increase even with the limited spawning stock.

Other species sampled were bigmouth buffalo, black bullhead, common carp, golden shiner, largemouth bass, northern pike, and white sucker.

Efforts to improve the health of the lake should focus on best management practices within the watershed. Improvements and education in land stewardship will often have secondary benefits to the lake in the way of quality and quantity of habitat. Generally, improvements in the quality and quantity of lake habitat will mean direct improvements of the fishery.
