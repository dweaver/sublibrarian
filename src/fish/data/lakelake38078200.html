Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/23/2008)

Garden Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 7, which consists of 41 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are large, have irregular shoreline shapes, and have very soft (unmineralized) water. Garden Lake is smaller (670 acres) than the lake class average of 1,807 acres, but it is part of a reservoir and is broadly connected to Farm and South Farm lakes; the combined area of this reservoir is 2,606 acres.

Garden Lake was thermally stratified on 08/11/2008 with a surface temperature of 72 F and a bottom temperature of 57 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 20 ft, where the temperature was 66 F. The Kawishiwi River flows through Garden Lake with flows of 400-1,000 CFS, depending on the rainfall. The outlet has a hydroelectric dam and the reservoir is used for power generation. The "inlet" from Farm Lake is about 500 ft wide and 10 ft deep. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline are mostly boulder, muck, and rubble. Aquatic plants are sparse and grow to a depth of 6 ft; wild rice and various pondweeds are the most common plants.

The 2008 fish population assessment used 7 gillnets and 12 trapnets. The entire reservoir (Garden, Farm, and South Farm Lakes) was assessed, and 20 gillnets were distributed proportionally among the three basins. Fourteen previous investigations on Garden Lake, dating back to 1965, used 2-12 gillnets. Trapnets (2-25 nets) were used during seven of these previous investigations. Gillnet and trapnet catches in this lake have historically been very poor, with median catches of only 13 fish per gillnet and 7 fish per trapnet.

Fish populations in 2008 were dominated by northern pike, walleye, cisco, white sucker, and yellow perch. Bluegill numbers were lower than in recent investigations. No smallmouth bass were captured in the nets in 2008, but anglers catch fair numbers of smallmouth.

Northern pike numbers in 2008 (2.3/gillnet) were near the median for this lake class and were higher than the median pike catch of 1.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes averaged 22.6" (3.0 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class. The largest pike was 37.0". Pike scales were difficult to read, but most pike appeared to be ages 3 and 4, and pike growth appeared to be normal by area standards.

Walleye numbers in 2008 (2.7/gillnet) were in the first quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median walleye catch of 2.1/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes averaged 12.8" (1.1 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class. The largest walleye was 28.4". Most of the walleye captured in 2008 were ages two and three. Walleye growth was slower than normal by area standards.

Cisco numbers in 2008 (1.7/gillnet) were in the first quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median cisco catch of 3.2/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Cisco sizes averaged 10.0" (0.4 lb), which was near the median for this lake class and was similar to the median size of 10.2" in all investigations on this lake. The largest cisco was 12.6". Cisco are a cold water fish and their abundance is probably underrepresented in the gillnet catches because only a small portion of the nets extend into cold water. Cisco are a rich forage food for predatory fish and their presence is probably responsible for occasional large walleye and northern pike that are caught in this lake.

Yellow perch numbers in 2008 (3.1/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were at the median perch catch of 3.1/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Perch sizes averaged 6.9" (0.17 lb), which was near the median for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 6.5" in all investigations on this lake. The largest perch (caught in a trapnet) was 10.8". Most perch were age 3, and perch growth was normal by area standards.

Bluegill sunfish and black crappie are not abundant in Garden Lake, but some nice-sized bluegill (over 9") and black crappie (over 13") are present. Smallmouth bass are more abundant than indicated by the net catches and are commonly caught by anglers.

Many of the game fish examined in 2008 were infected with Neascus. Many of the yellow perch were also infected with yellow grub. Neascus (black spot) and yellow grub are common parasites that are native to the area. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.

Ten native crayfish (probably Orconectus Virilis) were captured in one of the trapnets in 2008. Rusty crayfish (O. rusticus) were captured in Birch Lake, White Iron Lake, Farm Lake, and Garden Lake (near the dam) in crayfish traps by the U.S. Forest Service in the summer of 2007. Rusty crayfish have been present in Fall Lake for over 20 years, and experience tells us that they will eventually become abundant in this chain of lakes. Rusty crayfish are native to the Ohio River Valley and were probably brought to this area by anglers using them for bait. They are very aggressive and usually displace native crayfish. They can destroy beds of rooted aquatic vegetation and will nip swimmer's feet. ?
