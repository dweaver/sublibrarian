* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/19/2006)

Floyd is a 1,212 acre lake with a heavily developed shoreline located just north of the city of Detroit Lakes. The lake experienced a 64% increase in home construction between 1955 (191 homes) and 2001 (314 homes). It has a relatively diverse fish community including cold water species like tullibee and is popular for angling.

A special regulation for northern pike has been in effect on both Floyd (including the north basin) and Little Floyd lakes since 2003. This regulation requires anglers to immediately release all pike between 24 and 36 inches in length. Although early in the regulation, there are some indicators that the regulation may be having a positive effect on the size structure of northern pike in this lake. The average length of sampled pike was longer in 2006 (21.4 inches) than in all but one of the eight previous studies since 1955. Another measure used to define size structure is the proportional stock density (PSD). For pike, this is simply the percent of stock (sexually mature) fish at least 21 inches in length. In Floyd Lake in 2006, the northern pike PSD was second highest of those previously recorded at 54%. The lake's historic mean is 26%. Hopefully, these pike will grow to trophy sizes and opportunities will exist to catch more fish over 30, or even 36 inches. With the presence of cisco, this lake has the potential to produce large pike.

At 4.7 walleyes per gill net, catches were not significantly different from those during the 1990's even though stocking rates were doubled in 1998 from 1.0 pounds to 2.0 pounds of fingerlings per littoral acre, biennially. Floyd's walleye net catches have remained very stable under a variety of stocking schemes. Future investigations will help determine if the current stocking plan will add to walleye catches. Walleyes sampled in 2006 averaged nearly 2.5 pounds each.

Trap net catches of bluegill declined in 2006, but the numbers were similar to those reported prior to 1981 when more large bluegills were found in this lake. As in many area lakes, the portion of bluegills over eight inches in length is smaller than it was before the 1980's. However, these larger bluegills can still be found in Floyd Lake. Bluegill growth rates were never considered fast in Floyd Lake, but it is likely that fishing pressure has increased. In other words, more of the larger bluegills are harvested as soon as they reach "keeper" sizes than in the past.

The Minnesota Pollution Control Agency lists Floyd Lake as borderline eutrophic. This makes it particularly vulnerable to nutrient input and other development related activities. Good shoreland and watershed stewardship practices are essential to slow the degradation of water quality, fish populations, and property values for Floyd Lake. A couple of excellent sources for help in restoring or protecting shorelines are available through the Minnesota Bookstore. These include "Lakescaping for Wildlife and Water Quality" and "Restore Your Shore."?
