* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/16/2004)

The population assessment mainly targeted the lake trout population in Little Trout Lake. Eight experimental gill nets were set in deep water (29-72 feet), optimal habitat for lake trout. Assessments during the past 20 years have also used eight gill-net sets; however, they were generally fished in shallower water. For example, the 1999 assessment averaged 12.8' for a starting depth, while 2004 sets started in 36.9'. Likewise, the 1999 assessment averaged 46.2' for a ending depth, while 2004 sets ended in 54.4'. Furthermore, the mean depth for both ends and all nets was 29.5' in 1999 and 45.6' in 2004.

Cisco, lake trout, smallmouth bass, and walleye were the four species present in the 2004 assessment. Cisco was the most common species captured, making up 54% of the catch by number. Walleyes (20%) and smallmouth bass (16%) were the next most abundant.

The first recorded lake trout stocking occurred in 1965 when 75,000 fry were introduced. Stocking has continued and currently, 1,200 yearlings are stocked every other year and all receive a fin clip.

Lake trout gill-net abundance in 2004 was 1.00 fish/set, which was similar to the historical mean for the lake (1.13). Sixty-two percent of the lake trout captured had fin clips. Several year-classes were present, including fish up to 27 years old. Total length (TL) ranged from 7.2 to 35.4 inches with a mean of 20.3 inches. Mean weight was 5.45 pounds, which was the best recorded in the International Falls area for the past 10 years.

Walleye gill-net abundance was 1.88 fish/lift, which was the lowest recorded in the last 20 years. However, comparison to previous investigations may not be valid because 2004 was the first time all sets were fished in deep water. Eight year-classes were present and age ranged from 4-16 years. No single year-class stood out as strong and young fish were absent in our sampling. Total length ranged from 18.5-28.3 inches and the mean was 23.0. Total length at age 4 was 17.1 inches, which was well above the area mean of 14.4. The mean weight of 4.8 pounds was the second highest ever recorded in the International Falls management area.

Cisco gill-net abundance was 5.13 fish/set, which was the highest ever recorded for Little Trout Lake and near the median of 5.60 for standard sets in Class-5 lakes. Ciscoes were generally small; TL ranged from 7.0-11.5 inches with a mean of 8.3 inches.

Although experimental gill nets are not standard capture gear for smallmouth bass, 3.50 fish/set were captured in the two shallowest sets, which was greater than the third quartile when compared to standard gill-net sets in Class-5 lakes. Total length ranged from 6.5-18.9 inches for fish from deep and shallow sets. Seven year-classes were present and age ranged from 2-9 years. Growth was slightly greater than the area mean.
