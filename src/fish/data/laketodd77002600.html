Status of the Fishery (as of 08/02/2004)

Moose Lake is a popular panfish lake north of Burtrum along Highway 28. Bluegills and black crappies provide fishing opportunities all year round while spring time seems to be the most popular time for walleye fishing on the lake. The lake does not have a lot of structure other than a sunken island and edges of waterlily beds. Water quality has been an ongoing problem in the lake with algae blooms common in the lake. Filamentous algae and star duckweed were the most common aquatic plants in the lake. Flatstem pondweed was the most abundant submerged aquatic plant and only grew out to a depth of 7 feet due to the poor water clarity. Vegetation will typically grow out to a depth of 12 to 15 feet in most area lakes. Moose Lake falls in the catagory of a eutrophic lake charaterized by limited transparency, algae blooms common, rich in nutrients, and oxygen depleted in area of the lake below the thermocline. At the time the survey was conducted in early August little oxygen was available to fish below 13 feet. Two of the most notable changes from the last survey in 1998 were the northern pike and yellow perch catches. Pike numbers showed a significant decline and appear to be low, while perch abundance increased and rose above the normal range when compared to similar type lakes. Anglers have a good opportunity to catch a nice size northern pike as the average size in the survey was 4 pounds with several fish in the 8 pound range. The higher yellow perch population should help to maintain good growth rates in the pike and walleye. Perch can also be instrumental in helping maintain a well balanced bluegill population with fast growing, quality individuals. The perch can also provide a fishery as some of the fish measured up to almost 11 inches, however, about half of the perch caught in the survey had neascus or black spot. The primary management species for Moose Lake is largemouth bass. A regulation limiting the size of bass in possession to 12 inches or under is in effect on the lake. The goal of the regulation is to increase the number of bass over 17 inches while allowing harvest of bass under 12 inches which are more numerous. Moose Lake appears to have an excellent bass population with a wide range of sizes available to anglers including fish up to 20 inches. Along with an improvement in number of larger fish since the implementation of the regulation, has been an improvement in growth rates for the bass. The walleye fishery in the lake is maintained by fall fingerling stocking in odd numbered years. Walleye numbers appeared to be within the normal range for that type of lake and were similar to the catch seen in 1998. Anglers have a wide range of sizes available to them as walleye from 10 to 29 inches were seen in the survey. The lower northern pike and higher perch number could be a positive for walleye stocking. There should be better survival of the fingerlings from less predation by the pike and the perch providing a good food source. Moose Lake has abundant populations of both bluegills and black crappies. Despite the high numbers some quality size bluegills can be found in Moose Lake with fish up to almost 10 inches being documented in a late spring assessment. Average size of the bluegills, however, was about 6 inches. Along with the high crappie numbers comes a low average size of about 7 inches. Even with fertile water the crappies showed variable growth rates, but few fish over 10 inches have been seen in surveys suggesting anglers are cropping them off as the fish approach 9 inches in length. The bluegills in Moose Lake grow fast but they also show evidence of high harvest at 5 - 6 years of age and 6.5 to 7 inches of length. Other species in the fish community include black bullhead, hybrid sunfish, pumpkinseeds, white sucker, and yellow bullhead. Both species of bullheads had quality individuals up to 12 inches with some yellow bullheads up to 15 inches in length. Releasing larger northern pike and walleye is encouraged along with voluntary release of large bluegills and black crappies to work toward a more balanced fish community. Water quality in Moose Lake is a major concern. Surrounding land use practices are having a detrimental effect on the water and recreational value of the lake. Improving the water quality in Moose Lake should be a high priority of the county and landowners around the lake. Some effort may be needed to identify any sources of excess phosphorus and plans developed to reduce these sources. Buffer strips along inlets and lakeshore property are encouraged to reduce nutrients entering the lake. Maintaining adequate septics, keeping grass clippings and raked leaves out the lake, and the use of zero phosphorus lawn fertilizer will also help reduce nutrients entering the lake, thus slowing algae and excess weed growth. The Todd County Planning and Zoning office in Long Prairie has a good website which provides information for shoreline property owners. (www.co.todd.mn.us/PlanningZoning/planning.htm)
