Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/16/2007)

Sugar Lake, a general development lake, is located in northern Wright County, approximately five miles north of Annandale. There are two public accesses, one each on the north and south sides of the lake. Sugar Lake was designated for muskellunge management in 1967. With its larger size (1,015 acres), depth (69 feet), and water clarity (10 feet or more), Sugar Lake is one of the outstanding lake resources of the county. Past stocking has included walleye fingerlings (since 1945) and muskellunge (since 1967).

This is the first survey since 1998 and it included a spring muskellunge assessment, electrofishing for largemouth bass, and summer netting. This survey is also considered part of the background for the new fishing regulations that went into place in 2007. Those regulations protect northern pike from 24-36 inches and reduce the limit of crappie to five per day.

The 2007 survey provided a unique opportunity to estimate the northern pike, walleye and muskellunge populations.

The northern pike population was estimated to consist of 10,803 adults, or 10.6 adult northern pike per acre. This is a large population but the numbers are consistent with what has been found in the past. Northern pike average 19 inches in length and weigh less than two pounds. About 12% of the northern pike were larger than 24 inches, a percentage we hope increases as the regulations begin to work.

The walleye and muskellunge populations were substantially less than the northern pike. The walleye population was estimated to consist of 1,465 adults or 1.4 adults per acre. DNR net catches of walleye since 1971 show that walleye have existed within the normal or expected range for Sugar Lake, exceeding it only in 1976 and 1986. The average catch for all years is slightly less than five walleye per gill net. Relatively low numbers of walleye are present because there is no natural reproduction and the fish community is unbalanced with few yellow perch and large numbers of small northern pike.

The muskellunge population was estimated to be 266 adults (30 inches or larger). This estimate of muskellunge, 0.26 fish/acre, is within the range of reported values for Minnesota lakes (0.2 - 0.4 muskellunge/acre). Muskellunge average 41 inches and for the first time, a fish larger than 50 inches was captured. It was 51 inches and 33.5 pounds. Management has achieved its goal of providing a high quality but low-density population. Angling interest is high also, with one-third of all anglers seeking muskellunge.

Largemouth bass were sampled by electrofishing on May 2, 2007. A total of 149 largemouth bass were captured in slightly less than one hour. The catch rate of 169 fish/hr was 2 ? times greater than the average catch rate for Montrose Area lakes. Largemouth bass sampled ranged in length from 5 to 16 inches with an average length of 11 inches. Anglers have reported that bass fishing is excellent in Sugar Lake.

Sugar Lake has abundant populations of bluegills and a smaller population of black crappies. Few quality size bluegills can be found in Sugar Lake. Only one fish larger than eight inches was captured with trap nets during the summer survey. The average size of the bluegills was six inches. Black crappies are less abundant but the average size is greater. Crappies up to 13 inches were captured and the average size was nine inches.

Other species taken during the survey were bowfin, hybrid, green and pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, white sucker, black, brown and yellow bullheads.
