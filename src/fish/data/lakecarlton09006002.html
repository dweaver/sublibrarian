* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

No fish consumption guidelines are available for this lake. For more information, see the "Fish Consumption Advice" pages at the Minnesota Department of Health.


Status of the Fishery (as of 07/23/2007)

Lower Island is a 281-acre lake with 207 (74%) acres of littoral area and a maximum depth of 22 feet. The lake is located just east of Cromwell, Minnesota off Highway #210. The lake has a DNR administered, gravel, back-in access located on the southeast shore. Lower Island has been actively managed with stocking of fry, fingerling and yearling walleye from 1948 through 2006. Most recently, walleye fry have been stocked in even years from 1994 through 2006. The stocking rate was increased from 1,000 to 2,000 per littoral acre in 2000.

Walleye abundance of 4.8 per gillnet lift was above average compared to other Minnesota lakes of similar type. Walleye gillnet catch rates from six investigations since 1972 have ranged from 2.0 to 8.7 per lift. Mean size of 18.0 inches for walleye sampled in 2007 was considered large and rate of growth has been fast over the last four investigations. Of the total walleye sampled during 2007, 97% were aged to years that walleye fry were stocked. Aging of walleye from the previous three investigations resulted in correlations to years of stocking which ranged from 57 to 76%.

Northern pike abundance of 7.0 per gillnet lift was average compared to other Minnesota lakes of similar type. Average size was 21.4 inches. Rate of growth had increased from typical to what is considered fast compared to other Duluth Area lakes. Age analysis revealed a strong 2004 year-class and all year-classes from 2001 through 2006 represented.

Largemouth bass abundance of 12.2 per hour of electrofishing was considered low compared to other Duluth Area lakes. Average size of 12.3 inches was relatively large and growth was fast. Age analysis from 2007 revealed a relatively strong 2005 year-class and six year-classes represented from 2000 through 2005.

Bluegill abundance of 16.0 per trapnet lift had increased since 2002 (6.3) but was still considered average compared to other Minnesota lakes of similar type. Average size of 6.9 inches was large and growth was typical compared to other Duluth Area lakes. Age analysis revealed strong 2001 and 2002 year-classes and all year-classes from 1999 through 2003 represented.

Black crappie abundance of 0.4 per trapnet lift was below average compared to other Minnesota lakes of similar type. Average size of 6.6 inches was small and rate of growth was average. Age analysis revealed a strong 2005 year-class and four year-classes from 2002 through 2005 represented.

Yellow perch abundance of 1.2 per lift was average compared to other Minnesota lakes of similar type. Size of yellow perch was small and rate of growth was considered typical compared to other Duluth Area lakes. Age analysis revealed three year-classes represented from 2001 through 2004.

Shoreline seining was conducted during the 2007 survey. Young-of-the-year (YOY) bluegill, largemouth bass, rockbass and yellow perch were sampled. No minnow species were identified.

Other fish species sampled include bowfin, brown bullhead, pumpkinseed sunfish, white sucker and yellow bullhead.

Information was collected on aquatic vegetation, substrate and water quality. Diversity of aquatic macrophytes was average with a total of 29 species or species groups identified along 20 transects. Clasping-leaf pondweed, flat-stem pondweed and northern water milfoil were abundant along greater than 89% of the transects. Seven other species had abundance ratings of common. The shoalwater substrates most often encountered were detritus, muck and sand, which were identified along 75, 50 and 60% of the transects, respectively. Results of laboratory water analysis indicate Lower Island is an alkaline lake with moderate fertility. ?
