* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/06/2007)

Jay Gould Lake is a 426-acre lake located 2 miles south of Cohasset, MN. The lake is relatively shallow with a maximum depth of 33 ft and a littoral area of 292 acres. Jay Gould Lake is part of a chain of lakes including Little Jay Gould, Pokegama Lake and the Mississippi River. Navigation by boat is possible between all lakes. The lake has a public access on the south shore.

Northern pike gill net catch was high at 11.2/net and was similar to the upper end of the expected range for similar lakes. Catch rates in past assessments were lower and varied from 4.1 to 9.8/net. Although size structure was generally poor, a few fish up to 35 inches were sampled. An increase in northern pike abundance is a concern on Jay Gould Lake, since increasing abundance generally leads to a decrease in size structure. The trend is likely a reflection of overharvest of larger fish. Larger northern pike help to control the abundance of smaller northern pike through predation, which results in faster growth rates and better size structure. Anglers can help to improve size structure by harvesting fish less than 24 inches and releasing larger fish.

Walleye gill-net catch was 3.3/net and was similar to the upper end of the expected range for similar lakes. Gill net catch rates have historically been moderate and have varied from 3.0 to 4.6/net in the last three assessments. Size structure was excellent with fish up to 28 inches sampled. Walleye fingerlings and yearlings were stocked in 2004 and 2006. Walleye were present from ages one to seven with no differences in stocked vs non stocked years. Six of the 30 walleye sampled were ages 11 to 13 indicating a long-lived population. Growth was similar to the statewide average with fish averaging 18 inches by age five.

Yellow perch gill net catch was 13.0/net, which was within the expected range for similar lakes. Historical catch rates have varied between 20.0 and 31.4/gill net in the last three assessments. Size structure was poor with only one fish exceeding 9 inches sampled. Growth was slower than the statewide average with fish reaching 7.7 inches at age 6, compared to 9.3 inches for the statewide average.

Largemouth bass were sampled by nighttime electrofishing in May of 2007. The catch rate was 32.5/hour, indicating a moderately abundant population compared to area lakes. Electrofishing conducted in 1997 sampled 9.1/fish/hr. Size structure was good with fish up to 17 inches sampled. Growth was similar to the statewide average with fish reaching 15.6 inches in seven years. In addition to largemouth, two smallmouth bass between 15 and 16 inches were sampled by spring electrofishing.

Black crappie were sampled in low numbers with a gill net catch rate of 1.1/net and a trap net catch rate of 0.2/net. Historically, catch rates have also been low, only reaching the lower end of the expected range of 1.0/gill net, once before.

Other species sampled include, bowfin, brown bullhead, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, shorthead redhorse, white sucker, and yellow bullhead.

In order to maintain or improve fish and wildlife populations, water quality and habitat must be protected. People often associate water quality problems with large-scale agricultural, forestry, urban development or industrial practices in the watershed. In reality, the impact of land use decisions on one lake lot may be relatively small, yet, the cumulative impact of those decisions on many lake lots can result in a significant decline in water quality and habitat. For example, removing shoreline and aquatic vegetation, fertilizing lawns, mowing to the waters edge, installing beach sand blankets, failing septic systems and uncontrolled run-off, all contribute excess nutrients and sediment, which degrade water quality and habitat. Understanding these cumulative impacts and taking steps to avoid or minimize them will help to insure our quality fisheries can be enjoyed by future generations.
