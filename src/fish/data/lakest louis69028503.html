Status of the Fishery (as of 06/27/2005)

Eagles Nest Lake 3 is in Ecological Lake Class 5, which consists of 64 lakes in northeast Minnesota that have soft (unmineralized) and clear water. Eagles Nest Lake 3 is larger, shallower, and has a more irregular shoreline shape than typical for this lake class. Eagles Nest Lake 3 is a mesotrophic lake.

Eagles Nest Lake 3 was thermally stratified on 08/15/2005 with a surface temperature of 69 F and a bottom temperature of 50 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 27 ft, where the temperature was 65 F. The inlet, from Eagles Nest Lake 4, has a beaver dam that limits fish movement. The outlet, to Eagles Nest Lake 2, has no barrier to fish movement. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline of Eagles Nest Lake 3 are mostly boulder, rubble, and gravel. Aquatic vegetation has largely disappeared since rusty crayfish first appeared in 1987.

Fish sampling in the 2005 fish population assessment consisted of nine gillnet sets, twelve trapnet sets, and three small mesh smelt gillnets. Nine previous investigations on this lake dating back to 1956 consisted of 4-12 gillnet sets. Trapnets (9-12 sets) were used in five of these previous investigations. Small mesh smelt gillnets were also set in 2000.

Fish populations in 2005 were dominated by walleye, followed by white sucker and northern pike. Bass populations were likely greater than indicated by their abundance in this assessment because bass are known to be difficult to catch in these sampling nets. Bluegill numbers were much lower in 2005 than in other recent investigations on this lake.

Walleye numbers in 2005 (12.8/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 6.9/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. The high walleye catches in 2000 (12.3/gillnet) and 2005 indicate that walleye are reproducing very successfully despite the abundant population of rusty crayfish in the lake. Smelt, should they become abundant in this lake, may threaten walleye reproduction as they have negatively affected walleye populations in other lakes.

Walleye sizes in 2005 averaged 13.9" (1.1 lb), which is in the second quartile for this lake class. The largest walleye was 28.0". Many of the walleye captured in the standard gillnets (42 percent) were age three, from the 2002 year class. Good numbers of small walleye (young-of-year and age one) were also captured in the small mesh smelt gillnets in 2005. Growth of walleye through age three was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile for area lakes), while growth of walleye age four and older was somewhat faster than normal (in the third quartile). Of 96 walleye stomachs examined in 2005, 57 percent were empty, 24 percent contained perch, 14 percent contained unidentified fish remains, and 5 percent contained rusty crayfish.

White sucker have always been fairly common in this lake, and minnow dealers used to trap spawning suckers at the inlet from Eagles Nest Lake 4 many years ago. Sucker numbers in 2005 (7.3/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median sucker catch of 6.8/gillnet in all investigations on this lake.

Northern pike numbers in 2005 (1.3/gillnet) were in the first quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 2.9/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2005 averaged 24.0" (3.0 lb), which is in the third quartile for this lake class. The largest pike was 36.6". Pike scales were moderately difficult to read, and little confidence can be placed in the age and growth information for this species. All nine pike stomachs examined were empty.

Smallmouth bass numbers in 2005 (1.7/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median catch of 2.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Smallmouth sizes in 2005 averaged 14.8" (2.1 lb), which is in the fourth quartile for this lake class. Smallmouth growth was faster than normal (in the fourth quartile) by area standards. Six of seven smallmouth stomachs examined in 2005 contained rusty crayfish, and one stomach was empty.

Bluegill numbers in 2005 (1.6/trapnet) were much lower than in most previous investigations on this lake. Young sunfish use aquatic vegetation as nursery areas, and it is likely that the loss of vegetation due to rusty crayfish has increased sunfish mortality.

Rusty crayfish get entangled in the gillnets, and they were first observed in Eagles Nest Lake 3 in 1990, when 13/gillnet were captured. Their numbers increased to 135/gillnet in 1995, 247/gillnet in 2000, and 130/gillnet in 2005.

Rainbow smelt were first observed in Eagles Nest Lakes 1 & 2 in the 1980 investigation. Three were captured in the smelt gillnets in Eagles Nest Lake 3 in 2005, the first appearance of this species in this lake. Smelt are predatory fish and can negatively affect reproduction of other fish species.

Neascus was present on many of the game fish examined in 2005. Bass tapeworm were present in many of the smallmouth bass, and yellow grub was present on some of the yellow perch. All of these parasites are native and common to the area. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting the fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.
