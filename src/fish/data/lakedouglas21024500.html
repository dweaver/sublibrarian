Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/12/2006)

Lake Moses is an 822-acre basin located just west of Millerville in Douglas County. Maximum depth is 32.0 feet. Mean depth is 16.0 feet. Water clarity is exceptional given surrounding agricultural land uses and relative shallowness of the basin. MPCA Citizens Monitoring Program information from 1998 to present, documents improving water clarity. Transparency can exceed 15.0 ft. during summer months. Lake Moses supports a diverse aquatic plant community that includes large expanses of emergent vegetation (hardstem bulrush and cattail). Basin morphometry is bowl-shaped with limited bottom relief or defined structure. Moses Lake offers diverse, quality fishing opportunities.

Most angling interest is directed at walleye. Lake Moses supports a naturally recruiting walleye population. Natural recruitment has been too inconsistent among years to sustain desired population objectives, so supplemental stockings occur on an alternate year basis. Survey catches have been within the range of normal for similar lakes, yet remain below management objectives. Mortality of young walleye appears high in probable response to northern pike predation. Walleye growth can be qualified as "near-average". Adult walleye captured in the survey averaged 15.1 inches in length at age-4. Even though survival of young fish is less than optimal, the lake supports a very good fishery. Annual contributions from natural reproduction and stocking are fairly consistent. Nine consecutive age groups were represented in the 2006 population sample. In response to their longevity and near-average growth, size structure of the walleye population is near optimal to support a consumptive yet high quality recreational fishery. The majority of walleye captured in 2006 were between 15.0 and 19.0 inches in total length. Individuals exceeding 25.0 inches have been measured in each of the five recent surveys.

Sunfishes, specifically largemouth bass, bluegill, and black crappie are presently abundant. These species are also live to be old in Moses Lake. Bluegill exceeding ten-years-old were common in 2006 catches. Age distribution of the black crappie catch included some nine-year-old individuals. Bass and bluegill numbers may push Lake Moses carrying capacity. Growth rates are slow. Those 10-year-old bluegill captured had not yet grown to exceed 8.0 inches in length. Only seven percent of 8.0-inch or larger largemouth bass captures exceeded 15.0 inches. Catch statistics for black crappie deviated from that of other sunfish species in probable response to greater variability in the annual influx of young fish into the population. Average crappie catches in 2002 and 2006 have exceeded the range of normal. As stated, crappie live to be very old in Lake Moses. In contrast to other sunfishes, growth rate is average rather than slow. The end result is a healthy population with good numbers of large crappie. Thirty-three percent of all black crappie captured in 2006 exceeded 10.0 inches in length. Average weight of crappie caught was 0.5 lbs. All in all, bass and panfish fishing experiences should be very good.

All is not well within the fish community. Northern pike continue to be excessively abundant. Gillnet catches during the 2006 survey averaged 22.2 pike/net. The upper range of normal for similar lakes is 9.0 pike/gillnet. Correspondingly, growth is slow. Larger pike did not attain 28.0 inches in length until late into nine growing seasons. Fishing quality, in terms of average size caught, is marginal. Mean weight of pike captured in the 2006 survey was 1.5 lbs. Only nine pike measured exceeded 28.0 inches in length. Catch rates of walleye and yellow perch are inversely related to northern pike abundance.

Perhaps anglers have become too specialized and focused on fishing for walleye. There is strong justification to redirect fishing pressure and harvest onto small northern pike, eight- to 12.0-inch largemouth bass, and five- to seven-inch bluegill. Reducing numbers of small northern pike and releasing larger pike may even improve survival of young walleye and walleye fishing.
