Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/07/2003)

The St. Louis Bay is a 11,500 acre estuary that extends from the Fond du Lac dam to the Wisconsin Entry on Minnesota Point. The St. Louis Bay, which is entirely interstate water between Wisconsin and Minnesota, has 11,385 (99%) acres of littoral area and a maximum depth of approximately 35 feet. The Bay is a heavily used water body due to its proximity to the cities of Duluth and Superior, and status as an international shipping port. Impacts of international shipping have included the introduction of exotic species such as Eurasian ruffe, round goby, tubenose goby, white perch, spiny water flea and zebra mussel. Water quality improved dramatically after Western Lake Superior Sanitary District began treating point sources for domestic and commercial effluent from Cloquet and Duluth in 1979. The fishery has changed significantly since treatment began. The St. Louis Bay has been actively managed with stocking of walleye, northern pike, muskellunge, lake sturgeon and black crappie since 1980. Walleye, northern pike and muskellunge were stocked at a high rate from 1989 through 1993 in an effort to control Eurasian ruffe. Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources has also stocked muskellunge and lake sturgeon. Walleye are managed with a minimum length for harvest of 15 inches and a bag limit of two. Northern pike are managed with a bag limit of two. Smallmouth bass are managed with a bag limit of five and a minimum length for harvest of 14 inches. There is a no kill regulation for lake sturgeon. No commercial minnow harvest is allowed on the St. Louis Bay and possession of alewife, Eurasian ruffe, round goby, tubenose goby and white perch is prohibited. Filamentous algae was very abundant during the 2003 investigation. Gillnet efficiency was reduced due to fouling of nets by algae. Conclusions drawn from the 2003 assessment should be applied with caution. Lake sturgeon stocking has established 14 year classes from 1983 through 2000. A total of 3,185,700 fry and 136,405 fingerlings have been stocked. Lake sturgeon abundance of 1.5 per gillnet lift was near the bottom of the range for 13 previous investigations. Mean length of 22.6 inches was the largest compared to 13 previous investigations. Walleye fry are stocked annually at a rate of at least 10% of the total number of eggs taken at the St. Louis River spawning station. Walleye abundance of 4.2 per gillnet lift was average compared to other Minnesota lakes of similar type. Mean length of 14.3 inches was average. Growth was slow with length at age four of 13.6 inches. Age analysis revealed strong 1999 and 2001 year-classes and all year classes from 1994 through 2002 represented. A total of 38,324 Leech Lake strain muskellunge fingerlings have been stocked from 1988 through 2002. A total of three muskellunge were captured during the 2003 investigation. Mean length was 20.3 inches. Northern pike abundance of 1.7 was slightly below average compared to other Minnesota lakes of similar type. Mean length of 20.9 inches was averge. Growth was also average with a length at age four of 24.0 inches. Age analysis revealed a strong 2001 year-class and seven year-classes represented from 1995 through 2002. Yellow perch abundance of 1.3 per gillnet lift was below average compared to other Minnesota lakes of similar type. Mean length of 7.3 inches was average. Growth was fast with length at age IV of 9.2 inches. Age analysis revealed a strong 2001 year-class and five year-classes represented from 1997 through 2002. Eurasian ruffe abundance of 1.3 per gillnet lift was near the low end of the range for 10 investigations since they became established. Mean length was 5.4 inches. Length at age four was 5.3 inches. Age analysis revealed a strong 2000 year-class and no fish older than age four. Channel catfish abundance of 0.5 per gillnet lift was below average compared to 13 previous investigations. Mean length of 17.5 inches was large compared to previous investigations. Other fish species sampled included black crappie, freshwater drum, rock bass, shorthead redhorse, silver redhorse, smallmouth bass, tadpole madtom, white perch, white sucker and yellow bullhead. Only rock bass and white sucker were sampled at a rate greater than 1.0 per gillnet lift.
