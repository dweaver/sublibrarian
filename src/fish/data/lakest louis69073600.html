Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/01/1998)

Sand Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 16, which consists of 38 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are large, shallow, and have soft (unmineralized) water. Sand Lake is typical of lakes in this lake class except that it is smaller and clearer than most. Sand Lake classifies as mesotrophic according to Carlson's Trophic Status Index.

Sand Lake was slightly stratified thermally on 08/03/98 and retained good oxygen (more than 5 ppm) to the bottom, where the temperature was two degrees cooler than the surface temperature. The inlet from Rat Lake flows through bogs, beaver dams, and a culvert grate, all of which are barriers to fish movement. The outlet to Little Sand Lake has a concrete dam with provisions for stoplogs which have deteriorated so that no stoplogs can be used; consequently this dam functions as a fixed sill weir. The dam has two drops, 15" and 10", which act as fish barriers. Water levels on Sand Lake vary by an average of 0.5 ft each year.

Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline are mostly sand, with smaller amounts of muck, gravel, and rubble. Aquatic plants are scattered throughout the lake with concentrations in eastern bays, and grow to a depth of 10 ft; the most common plants are bushy pondweed, quillwort, yellow waterlily, variable pondweed, claspingleaf pondweed, wild celery, and floatingleaf burreed.

Most of the shoreline is developed, except for State land in the bog-fringed southwest bay. There is no public access. In 1998 there were two resorts with 16 cabins and six campsites, and 174 private homes and cottages.

Fish sampling in 1998 consisted of 12 gillnet sets, 12 trapnet sets, and shoreline seining. Previous investigations (08/28/50, 08/08/77, 08/09/87 consisted of 6-7 gillnet sets and 4-8 trapnet sets.

The total catch of fish (all species combined) in the gillnets in 1998 of 54 fish per net (37 lb per net) was normal for this lake class and was similar to the average catch of 44 fish per net (41 lb per net) in all three previous nettings on this lake. Lake class 16 is one of the most productive lake classes in northeast Minnesota, in terms of the number and weight of fish in gillnet catches.

The total catch of fish in the trapnets in 1998 of 9 fish per net (10 lb per net) was similar to the average catch of 8 fish per net (6 lb per net) in previous nettings on this lake.

Fish populations in 1998, as in previous investigations on Sand Lake, were dominated by walleye, yellow perch, white sucker, and northern pike. Other species were present in low numbers.

Walleye numbers in 1998 (9 per gillnet, 3 per trapnet) were higher than normal for this lake class and were higher than the average catches of 7 per gillnet and 1 per trapnet in previous nettings on this lake. Walleye sizes in the gillnets in 1998 averaged 14"; the largest was 27". Most of the walleye were ages two and four, from strong 1996 and 1994 year classes. Walleye growth (8" at age two, 15" at age four) was normal for the area.

Yellow perch numbers in 1998 (35 per gillnet) were higher than normal for this lake class and were higher than the average catch of 20 per gillnet in previous nettings on this lake. Perch sizes in 1998 averaged 6"; the largest was 9". Most of the perch were age three, from a strong 1995 year class. Perch growth (5" at age three) was normal for the area.

White sucker numbers in 1998 (6 per gillnet) were normal for this lake class and were lower than the average catch of 9 per gillnet in previous nettings on this lake. Sucker sizes in 1998 averaged 16"; the largest was 22".

Northern pike numbers in 1998 (3 per gillnet) were normal for this lake class and were lower than the average catch of 5 per gillnet in previous nettings on this lake. Pike sizes in 1998 averaged 23"; the largest was 34". Many of the pike were age two, from a strong 1996 year class. Pike growth (20" at age three) was faster than normal for the area.

Bluegill numbers in 1998 (4 per trapnet) were normal for this lake class; bluegill were previously captured only in 1977, when their numbers were 3 per trapnet. Bluegill sizes in 1998 averaged 5"; the largest was 7". Most bluegill were age two, from a strong 1996 year class. Bluegill growth (4" at age two) was faster than normal for this lake class.

Crappie numbers in 1998 (less than one per trapnet) were lower than normal for this lake class and were lower than the average catch of 2 per trapnet in previous nettings on this lake. Crappie sizes in 1998 averaged 8"; the largest was 9". Crappie up to 14" were captured in previous nettings.

Neascus (black spot) was observed on northern pike, walleye, and yellow perch in 1998. Neascus is a common parasite that is native to the area. It cannot infect humans, is often removed by filleting, and is killed at the temperatures used for cooking.
