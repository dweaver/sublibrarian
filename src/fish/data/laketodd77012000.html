* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/26/2006)

Charlotte Lake is a fairly small, deep lake being 148 acres in size with a maximum depth of 84 feet. The lake is located on the south side of the City of Long Prairie and has a good access with a concrete boat ramp along with a fishing pier. Water clarity at the end of June was good with a secchi disk reading of 13.4 feet. The lake has relatively fast drop-offs around the lake which limits the amount of productive shallow water habitat available to fish. Muskgrass was the most abundant submerged vegetation which is indicative of a marl, hard-water lake. Submerged vegetation was found to grow down to a depth of 14 feet with other common species being coontail, flat-stem pondweed, illinois pondweed, and northern milfoil. Cattails are common on the west side of the lake which for the most part has protected the shoreline from development. The city owns a swimming beach on the north side of the lake and also has a park on the east side of the lake. The fishing pier is well used in the openwater season with small sunfish and northern pike being the most common fish caught. The lake management focus has been on largemouth bass, northern pike, and walleye. Charlotte Lake has an abundant northern pike population with the average length in the survey being 19 inches or about 2 pounds. Pike numbers appear to have increased since the last survey in 2000 and are above desired abundance. Having high pike numbers can have a negative affect on the fish community in the lake by reducing survival of walleye fingerlings as well as over predation on the prey base such as yellow perch. Charlotte Lake does have a tullibee population along with a good white sucker population which provide additional prey species for the larger northern pike and walleye. Some of the tullibee were found to be infected with triaenophorus or white grubs which makes them undesirable to fisherman. All of the walleye caught in the summer survey were over 17 inches in length with one up to 30 inches being measured. Locating the fish may be a challenge but an angler has a chance of catching a trophy fish. The population is likely maintained by stocking which usually occurs every other year when the fish are available. The largemouth bass population appears low in abundance when compared to similar type lakes. A couple of trophy size bass over 16 inches were caught in the trapnets but the average that anglers are likely to see would probably be around 12 inches. The bluegill catch was dominated by small to medium size fish. While there were some fish over 7 inches caught, the average size was about 5 inches. Even though the net catches suggest low numbers of black crappies in Charlotte Lake, the species can be difficult to catch with standard lake survey gear so abundance is unknown. Another panfish species in the lake is pumpkinseeds. Like with the bluegills, some pumpkinseeds over 7 inches were measured but the average size was around 5 inches. Yellow perch have historically been present in low numbers in Charlotte Lake. Perch can be an important prey species for many species of fish and help to maintain a balanced bluegill population. It is unknown if reducing pike numbers can help perch numbers rebound, but suspected, as there may also be other environmental factors involved. Other species sampled in the survey included black bullhead, bowfin or dogfish, brown bullhead, green sunfish, hybrid sunfish, rock bass, and yellow bullhead. Yellow bullheads were the most abundant of the three species. Some quality individuals over 12 inches were found in all of the three species. Dogfish appear to abundant and can provide quite an angling experience when caught. Despite the dogfish not being considered a food fish by most people, their usefulness as a predator on small bluegills makes them a good fish to have in a lake. Anglers are encouraged to practice selective harvest and release the larger fish of each species to maintain a balanced fish community and sustain high quality fishing. Neascus or black spot was common on the northern pike and some walleye. Neascus is a trematode or parasite that is usually found in the skin of the fish. The life cycle of the parasite also includes snails and fish-eating birds, such as herons and bitterns. Skinning the fish removes most of the parasites and cooking will kill the rest. Humans cannot be infested. Charlotte Lake's recreational value is high with its good water clarity, swimming beach, park, and fishing pier. Protecting and improving the water quality should be a high priority of the City of Long Prairie and lakeshore owners. Maintaining adequate septics, keeping grass clippings and raked leaves out of the lake, and the common sense use of lawn fertilizer will help to reduce nutrients entering the lake thus slowing algae growth. Buffer strips along inlets and lakeshore property are encouraged to reduce nutrients entering the lake. Overall, the lake appears to have fairly responsible lakeshore owners as shoreline impacts by development were lower than most lakes. The MPCA and Todd County Planning and Zoning have some good lake protection information on their websites. www.pca.state.mn.us/water/lakeprotection.html " Guide to Lake Protection & Management".
