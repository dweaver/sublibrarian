Status of the Fishery (as of 08/09/2004)

The Red Lake River Reservoir presents fishing opportunities in the center of the city of Thief River Falls. The reservoir is created by a dam on the Red Lake River and has two main arms which includes part of the Thief River. This silty bottom reservoir is mostly less than 15 feet deep with sufficient submerged aquatic vegetation to provide good habitat for fish such as northern pike. Fall fingerling stocking of walleye every two out of three years and annual stocking of bluegill sunfish make these species available. Other fish species found here include black crappie, yellow perch, pumpkinseed sunfish, fresh water drum (sheepshead), rock bass, bullheads, and white suckers. Two public boat ramps and a fishing pier in town enable both boaters and shore anglers fishing opportunities. Good fishing can also be found below the dam. Northern pike are the most abundant gamefish in this reservoir due to the suitability of the habitat for natural reproduction. Cattails, hardstem bullrush, and other aquatic vegetation provide good pike spawning and nursery areas. Northern pike sampled in 1998 were a wide range of sizes showing many successful year classes. Most pike were between 15-20" although this year=s sampling caught a 40" specimen. Patient walleye anglers have the opportunity to occasionally catch a larger fish. Walleye caught in test nets were mostly between 17-22". Stocking maintains this fishery which is limited by lack of good spawning habitat and possibly a low abundance of suitable food for juvenile fish. Local stories suggest early ice fishing provides the best walleye fishing. Panfish are available in the reservoir as well. Stocked adult bluegills offer opportunity for anglers of various skill. Bluegills stocked in 1998 averaged 8". Naturally reproducing black crappie populations are known to produce year classes (fish of the same age) that are quite variable in number from year to year. Crappie populations are most likely linked to strong year classes in Red Lake that move down the river. A strong year class of 8-9" fish is now present in the reservoir and should provide even larger specimens in 1999. Red Lake River Reservoir water quality is influenced by the upper watershed and the area around the reservoir. Sediment and agricultural runoff coming from the Thief River and urban run-off of lawn chemicals and from storm sewer outlets have a negative impact on water clarity and quality. Nutrient enrichment (as from fertilizer) from farmland and residences contributes to dense growths of algae at times. When these algal mats die and decay, oxygen content in the reservoir is reduced. Storm sewer outlets contribute trash and urban pollution (such as oil run-off), as well. Water contributed from Thief Lake and Agassiz wildlife refuge can also be lacking in oxygen (anoxic) which has a negative impact on fish populations, especially when water levels are low. Because of these factors, maintenance of the fishery in Red Lake River Reservoir is a challenge. The Red Lake River fishery is dependent on decisions made in the surrounding watershed as well as stocking and other efforts made within the reservoir.

The Red Lake River Reservoir is a fishing venue primarily due to its location rather than its quality. The reservoir=s location in the center of Thief River Falls and the lack of nearby fishing venues creates the need to maintain fishing opportunities in this degraded system. The Thief River enters this reservoir at its head and the sediment load it carries, combined with its agri-chemical load, is most likely the contributor to its rating by MPCA (1996, 305b Report to Congress of the United States) of >not supportive= of swimming and =partially supportive= of aquatic life. The Thief River also rated only partially supportive in terms of oxygen, turbidity, and habitat alteration. The Red Lake River was given similar ratings (except for being supportive of swimming) from its source to the Thief River. However personal observation of the river at its confluence with the Thief indicate it carries less sediment. The heavy sediment loading is most likely due to poor agricultural practices in this area and an unstable stream channel due to channelization. The downstream movement of sediment results in the reservoir acting as a settling pond. This reservoir also has heavy nutrient influx which creates dense beds of aquatic macrophytes. These algal blooms contribute to urban residents desire to remove aquatic vegetation as it collects the algal mats and trash. Multiple use interests for recreation also create pressure for dredging and removing aquatic vegetation in this reservoir. Additionally, The reservoir is vulnerable to anoxic events due to outflow from wildlife management areas (such as Thief Lake SWMA and Aggassiz NWR) which can be confounded by low flows during dry periods. Anoxic events have been recorded under the ice in the reservoir. All these factors make the improvement of fishing resources a challenge. The most numerous naturally producing game fish species found in the reservoir are northern pike. Pike were present across a broad range of age classes, and occasional specimens greater than 30' have been sampled. The greatest strength in this system is the success of the northern pike population. Sampling in 1998 showed the presence of a strong year class of 8-9" black crappies. Red Lake may be the source for these fish, as this coincides with a known strong year class in this lake. Few walleye were sampled, but the most common year class was of 1994. It is unclear whether these are naturally produced fish or survivors from stocking. No stocking was done in 1994. Yearlings and fingerlings were stocked in 1995 at a low rate (698 and 422 fish, respectively). We also found evidence of many snapping turtles due to the quantity of northerns and other fish eaten and knawed in our gillnets. Very little evidence of natural reproduction was found in the reservoir during this survey despite the use of 1/4" trap nets. The recapture of stocked blue gills was low and no young-of-the-year were found. The catch rate of yellow perch was also markedly low. Maintenance stocking of bluegills will continue to be necessary to sustain this fishery. It is unclear how much walleye stocking is contributing to the fishery or whether there are naturally reproduced fish present, possibly as emigrants from Red Lake. Historically, Red Lake was likely an important source of walleye production. However, the current depleted state of Red Lake=s fishery effectively discounts it as a source in the near future. The need for stocking walleye continues in order to maintain a fishery. Increased numbers of fish will be stocked (above the total poundage goals) in years where the number per pound rate is low. Most anecdotal fishing reports heard were of walleye ice fishing and some reports of large northern pike. The reservoir was used for fishing during the survey period, primarily by kids. We saw northern pike being caught. The fishing pier was utilized at times. It may be useful to do some water quality analysis to assess the limitations contributing to the lack of juvenile fish. An agricultural chemical analysis and invertebrate presence/absence assessment could be useful for assessing this system.
