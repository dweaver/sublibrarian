Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/18/2003)

North Twin Lake is a 313-acre lake with a maximum depth of 59 feet. It is located six miles east of Turtle River in east central Beltrami County. There is a United States Forest Service public access on the south side of the lake off county road 22. The lake is entirely within the Chippewa National Forest. Over half of the shoreline is under public ownership. The Minnesota Department of Natural Resources has classified Minnesota's lakes into 43 different lake classes based on physical and chemical characteristics. North Twin Lake is in lake class 23. Other area lakes in the same lake class include Buck, Grant, Swenson, South Twin, Elk, South Long and Walker Brook Lakes. Typically lakes in this class are deep, have little area that is less than 15 feet deep and are clear. If it gets too windy on the bigger surrounding lakes, North Twin Lake offers anglers a refuge from the wind. Anglers have a wide variety of species to fish for in North Twin Lake. DNR fisheries management focuses on walleye, northern pike, bluegill, black crappie and largemouth bass.

North Twin Lake along with Medicine Lake have an experimental regulation for northern pike. Anglers are required to release all northern pike between 22" - 30". The northern pike regulation was implemented in 1988 with a goal of improving the size of pike. Northern pike size appeared to improve after the regulation was implemented. The four assessments prior to the regulation had average weight of pike ranging from 1.5 - 2.0 pounds, while in 1993 and 1998, average weights improved to 2.8 and 2.6 pounds. However, by 2003, the average weight of pike declined again to an average of 1.8 pounds. It is uncertain what may have caused a reversal of the positive gains in pike size. It has been documented that relatively low levels of noncompliance (illegal harvest) with special fishing regulations can nullify the expected gains. Creel survey conducted in 2003 documented that 3 -4% of the northern pike caught in the protected size range were being illegally harvested. Data from this assessment will be included with a variety of other sources such as special spring sampling and angler harvest information to formulate recommendations for the future of this experimental regulation. Public comment is encouraged anytime, but a formal public review period will be announced prior to dropping, extending or modifying the current regulation.

Walleye abundance is good compared to other class 23 lakes. Presently walleye fingerling are stocked every third year. Walleye sampled ranged in length from nine to 25 inches with an average length and weight of 17 inches and 1.8 pounds. Thirty percent of the walleye sampled in 2003 were greater than 20 inches long indicating a quality fishery. The most predominant sizes ranged from 12 - 15 inches and 19 - 22 inches, corresponding to years of fingerling stocking. Walleye in North Twin Lake were exceeding growth rates of other similar lakes.

Few perch were sampled that were greater than nine inches which is the siz usually acceptable to anglers. All perch caught by anglers in the summer creel survey were released and not of an acceptable size. But yellow perch are important since they provide the primary food source for most larger predators. In 2003 perch were sampled at a rate that was the lowest recorded out of eight surveys. DNR personnel will be monitoring this species closely as an indicator of available forage.

Both smallmouth and largemouth bass are present in North Twin Lake. Largemouth bass up to 20 inches and smallmouth bass up to 16 inches were sampled in 2003. They both are reproducing naturally in the lake. Natural undeveloped shorelines with its abundant vegetation and submerged logs are ideal for bass spawning. Protection of the habitat are critical for these species. Anglers caught more largemouth bass than smallmouth bass during the summer creel survey.

Bluegill are present in good numbers in North Twin Lake and is one of the primary species sought and harvested. There has been a downward trend in the size of bluegill over the past three surveys. In 2003 only 33% of the bluegill sampled were greater than six inches long and none were sampled greater than 8.5 inches. Anglers should show some restraint when the bluegills are really biting. Larger bluegill have disappeared in many lakes because of over harvest.

Black crappie are also abundant and ranged in length from 4 to 13 inches. Age 1 and 5 year olds are the two strongest age classes present and made up 79% of the sample. Black crappie tend to be more cyclic in their reproductive success. This leads to some fluctuation in fishing success and average size as individual dominant year classes cycle through.
