Status of the Fishery (as of 07/28/1997)

Lily Lake is part of the Fairy Lake chain north of Sauk Centre best known for its bluegill fishing. Anglers access the lake via the outlet from the township road between Fairy and Lily Lakes. Water clarity was excellent for a lake in central Minnesota and vegetation grew out depths of 17 feet. Angling opportunities in the lake include such species as northern pike, bluegills, black crappies, largemouth bass, pumpkinseed, and yellow bullheads. Northern pike numbers have declined some from the last survey, but continue to high for the lake. Most of the fish sampled were from 20 to 23 inches in length. Yellow perch appear to have disappeared or are extremely low in numbers. Perch are an important prey species for northern pike and walleye. With no perch available, northern pike will use bluegill and black crappies as food. Typically, pike populations with a main prey base of panfish tend to show slower growth. The largemouth bass population appears to be moderate in numbers with an average size of 12 to 14 inches. Bluegill abundance appears normal for the lake type. The average size caught during the survey was 5 to 6 inches, while anglers were finding plenty of fish from 6 to 7 inches. Pumpkinseeds were less numerous and smaller than the bluegills. Black crappies are common and while most are less than nine inches in length, the lake will occasionally give up one over eleven inches. Hybrid sunfish were low for the lake type. Bowfin or dogfish and yellow bullhead numbers were at normal levels. Many of the yellow bullheads were around 12 inches in length with some measuring up to 14 inches. Many of the northern pike and bluegills were infested with neascus. Neascus is a black trematode or parasite. The life cycle goes from snails to fish to maturing in fish-eating bird, such as herons. Humans cannot be infested. Skinning the fish removes most of the parasites and cooking will kill the rest. Lily Lake is a small lake with light development. The major source of water comes from the north through Long Lake. Although agriculture and dairy farming are primary land use practices in the watershed, most of the nutrients appear to be filtered out by Long Lake before reaching Lily Lake. Because of the small size of Lily Lake, land use practices in the immediate watershed around the lake could have a direct impact on water quality. Harvest of small northern pike and release of the larger fish is encouraged to improve quality in the population and a more balanced fish community.
