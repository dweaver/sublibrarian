Fish Stocking Activity

Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/01/2008)

Upper Red Lake is a 120,000 acre lake, 60% (72,000 acres) of which is under the jurisdiction of the Red Lake Band of Chippewa Indians (Red Lake Band). The remaining 40% (48,000 acres) falls under the jurisdiction of the state of Minnesota. A gill net fishery heavily exploited walleye stocks in this lake for many decades. Angling harvest was also substantial when strong year classes were present and recruited to the fishery. The collapse of the Red Lakes walleye population in the mid 1990s has been well documented. Similarly, the cooperative recovery effort that included a total closure on walleye harvest, a short-term walleye fry stocking program, intensified population monitoring, and increased law enforcement efforts has been widely publicized. In 2006, the Red Lakes were re-opened to walleye fishing under a conservative set of harvest regulations. Walleye harvest allocations, as outlined in the harvest management plan (Harvest Plan for Red Lakes Walleye Stocks, Red Lakes Fisheries Technical Committee, March 2006), are based on the proportion of surface water acreage within each jurisdiction. Specific fishing methods and regulations for managing harvest within the allocations are determined individually within each jurisdiction. In state waters of Upper Red Lake, a two-fish possession limit in combination with a 17-26 inch protected slot length limit (only one fish over 26 inches allowed in possession) was initially implemented for walleye. Regulations were relaxed in 2008 to a three-fish possession limit with the same protected slot limit. In addition to special walleye regulations, a special harvest regulation for northern pike was implemented in state waters: a 26-40 inch protected slot length limit (only one fish over 40 inches allowed in possession).

Present Fish Population Status Walleye gill net catch-per-effort (CPE) was 26.3 per gill net, which is slightly below catch rates recorded in the previous five years. It appears that overall walleye density peaked in 2003 and has relaxed a bit as the lake seeks to find its carrying capacity. Walleye from 11 consecutive year classes were sampled with oldest fish representing the 1997 year class (age 11). Approximately 61% of the walleye sample consisted of fish younger than age-5, which are primarily the result of natural reproduction. Thirty percent of the 2008 gill net sample was between 14 and 17 inches. Fish from 11 to 13 inches composed 37% of the gill net sample. This size distribution suggests that fishing and the ability to find "keeper" walleyes should be good for the foreseeable future. The black crappie gill net catch rate was below the long-term median, and it continues to follow a gradual decline from an all-time high recorded in 1996. Over the past 13 years, age analyses indicate that a single strong year class produced in 1995 has dominated the population. To date, none of the younger age groups have been very abundant compared to the 1995 year class. Northern pike CPE was 1.5 fish per gill net in 2008, similar to the long-term median of 1.9 fish/net. The average size of fish caught in gill nets has been stable over the past eight years, with a nice mixture of fish sizes in the sample. Spring trap netting was conducted in 2008 during the northern pike spawning run to better assess the population. Thirty-two percent of fish captured were greater than 30 inches. The largest fish captured was 43 inches with 5% of the sample greater than 40 inches. Our sampling confirmed that this fishery contains large fish and provides anglers with a good chance at landing a trophy-sized fish. There was only one lake whitefish observed in the 2008 gill net sample and none during the 2007 assessment. The abundance of lake whitefish has declined from previously high levels following a significant summer kill in 2001. Although whitefish reproduction has been documented since then, the rate of recruitment appears to be low. Yellow perch CPE was only 3.2 fish per gill net in 2008 as heavy predation on juvenile perch for several years has reduced their numbers. However, approximately 40% of all yellow perch caught in gill nets were between 9 and 10 inches in length, and about 58% were greater than 8 inches long. Yellow perch less than eight inches in length made up the remainder of the sample, representing the first significant recruitment in several years.
