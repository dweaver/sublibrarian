Status of the Fishery (as of 06/30/2003)

Pepin Lake is a small lake on the west side of the City of Swanville with a wide connection to the nearby Swan River. Fish migration is known to occur during spring high water, however, water lever flucuations could limit movement throughout the summer and fall. Frequent rain events in June put the water level in Pepin Lake at record highs and may have had an effect on fish movement and location during the survey. The local sportsmens club maintains the lake access and also supplied a fishing dock to help those without boats to have an angling opportunity. Northern pike, bluegills, black crappies, largemouth bass, and yellow perch are some of the more commonly angled species in Pepin Lake. Anglers reported that catching small northern pike was common which fit with what was seen in the survey where most of the pike were under 20 inches in length. Some larger pike up to 36 inches have been observed. Bluegills are abundant and the size structure of the population appears good despite the high numbers. Many of the fish seen in the survey were over 6 inches with some fish up to almost 9 inches. Black crappies are also fairly abundant but most that were seen in the survey would be considered small by most anglers. Ten-inch crappies were the biggest caught, but most were less than 9 inches in length. Pepin Lake does support a largemouth bass population but no bass were sampled in this survey. Bass can be hard to assess with standard lake survey nets. Spring night electrofishing has proved more effective at catching bass and past surveys have recorded quality size fish up to 19 inches. Yellow perch was one fish species that was absent from the 2003 lake survey but abundant in the previous survey in 1997. Perch can be an important prey species for gamefish and can also have a postive affect on the bluegill population. The population may show a recovery if the northern pike numbers go down. An occasional walleye can also be found in Pepin Lake. Although walleye stocking was discontinued because the lake is better suited to northern pike and panfish, fish are thought to migrate into the lake from the Swan River. Pepin's fertile water and connection to the river provides for a diverse roughfish community including black bullheads, bowfin or dogfish, brown bullheads, white suckers, and yellow bullheads. White suckers were the most numerous of the roughfish species and ranged in size from 7 inches up to 21 inches. The smaller suckers could provide an important food source for the larger northern pike in the lake. Carp have been observed in the shoal waters of Pepin although none were caught during the survey. Water quality in Pepin Lake affects the recreational value of the lake and make-up of the fish community. Good lakeshore and agricultural practices in the watershed are encouraged to improve water quality in the lake. Selective harvest is also encouraged for both angling and spearing to minimize impact on the fish community. Releasing larger northern pike and voluntarily limiting harvest of larger panfish will help sustain the quality and balance of the fish community.
