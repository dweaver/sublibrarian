* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/07/2004)

Bear Head Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 7, which consists of 41 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are large, have irregular shoreline shapes, and have very soft (unmineralized) water. Bear Head Lake is smaller than most of the lakes in this lake class.

Bear Head Lake was thermally stratified on 08/23/2004 with a surface temperature of 62 F and a bottom temperature of 52 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 33 ft, where the temperature was 61 F. Bear Head Lake has no inlets or outlets. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline are mostly rubble and gravel, and there are large amounts of old slab wood on the bottom from lumber mill operations decades ago. Aquatic plants are sparse and grow to a depth of 12 ft. Riparian development, all associated with Bear Head Lake State Park, consists of campsites, a boat ramp and dock at the public access, and one log cabin. Use of the public access requires purchase of a State Park permit.

Fish sampling in the 2004 fish population assessment consisted of 12 gillnet sets and 12 trapnet sets. Thirteen previous fisheries investigations, dating back to 1961, consisted of 3-12 gillnet sets. Five of these previous investigations also used 6-20 trapnet sets.

Fish populations in 2004 were dominated by walleye, white sucker, northern pike, and bluegill sunfish. Black crappie and yellow perch numbers in 2004 were lower than in recent investigations. Largemouth bass are more abundant than indicated by the trapnet and gillnet catches, as bass are known to be "net shy".

Walleye numbers in 2004 (4.8/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 6.4/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes in 2004 averaged 16.3" (1.8 lb), which was in the fourth quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 15.0" in all investigations on this lake. The largest walleye captured in 2004 was 31.0" (12.0 lb). Walleye were well distributed among ages 2-10. Walleye growth was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile) by area standards.

Northern pike numbers in 2004 (3.3/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 1.9/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2004 averaged 20.9" (2.1 lb), which was in the second quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 20.1" in all investigations on this lake. Most pike captured in 2004 (68 percent) were age three, and pike growth was somewhat slower than normal by area standards.

Bluegill numbers in 2004 (11.6/trapnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class. Bluegill were not captured in the initial investigation in 1961 and were first observed in the 1969 investigation. Their numbers increased over time, with the highest catch (38.5/trapnet) occurring in 1999. All bluegill captured in 2004 were ages 3-9, with most (57 percent) age six, from the 1998 year class. Growth of bluegill captured in 2004 was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile) by area standards.

Black crappie numbers in 2004 (2.2/trapnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 8.0/trapnet in four assessments since black crappie were first observed in this lake. Black crappie were not captured in initial investigations on this lake and were first observed in the 1989 gillnet assessment. Black crappie numbers peaked in the trapnets in 1995 (14.2/trapnet) and have declined since then. Some large black crappie (up to 14") were captured in 2004.

Many of the game fish examined in 2004 were infected with neascus. Neascus (black spot) is a common parasite that is native to the area. It cannot infect humans, is often removed by filleting the fish, and is killed at temperatures used to cook fish.
