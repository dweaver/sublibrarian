Status of the Fishery (as of 07/19/2004)

Long Lake is a mesotrophic or moderately fertile bass-panfish lake in central Minnesota. The local township government maintains a concrete ramp and a parking area on the north side of the lake for public use. The lake has moderate development with most homes and one resort located on the north half of the lake. Water clarity remains good for a lake located in this area of the state. Much of the lake, except marsh areas, has a sand bottom. Some of the more common aquatic plants include muskgrass, native water milfoil, and cattail. The lake supports a variety of fish species that attract anglers, including largemouth bass, northern pike, walleye, bluegill, and black crappie. While northern pike abundance appeared to decline since the last survey, numbers continue to be high. Despite the high numbers, some nice fish were seen in the survey. A good portion of the pike were over 24 inches or 4 pounds with some up in the 12 - 13 pound range. A northern pike regulation that requires the release of all pike between 24" and 36" inches with only 1 over 36" allowed in possession was implemented on the lake in 2003, so have a good ruler. The purpose of the regulation is to increase the number of quality fish over 24 inches. Walleye abundance continues to be low despite increased stocking efforts since 1998. None of the walleye in the survey were under 12 inches and just over half of the fish were over 20 inches in length. Long Lake does have some walleye natural reproduction but not at levels needed to maintain a fishable population. Yellow perch numbers also showed a decline from 1998 and are at their lowest level since 1988. A high perch population is desirable as they can be a important food species for both northern pike and walleye. Perch can also be instrumental in helping maintain a well-balanced bluegill population of fast growing, quality individuals. The low walleye and perch numbers are likely due to predation from the high northern pike population. The lake appears to support a moderate abundance of largemouth bass available in a wide range of sizes. While anglers usually see the smaller fish because they are more abundant, trophy size bass up to 20 inches are present. Few black crappies were caught in the summer survey but they sometimes can be difficult to sample with standard lake survey gear. While some crappies up to 13 inches have been documented in past surveys, most have been under 9 inches in length. Bluegill provide a popular fishery on the lake in mid-summer. While most of the bluegills seen in the summer survey were less than 6 inches in length, a spring assessment in 2004 caught a fair number of fish over 7 inches with some up to 9 inches documented. Pumpkinseeds are another sunfish species in the lake but they do tend to be smaller than the bluegills. Some of the roughfish species present in the lake include black bullheads, bowfin or dogfish, brown bullheads, white suckers, and yellow bullheads. Yellow bullheads were the most numerous of the bullhead species and are available in a wide range of sizes. Quality size bullheads were seen in all three of the species with some individuals in the 13" to 15" range. Protecting and improving the water quality in Long Lake should be a high priority of the lake association and landowners around the lake. Preservation of emergent vegetation beds can help water quality and protect shorelines as well as provide valuable fish habitat. Buffer strips along inlets and lakeshore property are encouraged to reduce nutrients entering the water and provide riparian habitat. Maintaining adequate septic, keeping grass clippings and raked leaves out of the lake, and the use of zero phosphorus lawn fertilizer will also help reduce nutrients entering the lake thus slowing algae and excess weed growth. The Todd County Planning and Zoning office in Long Prairie has a good website which provides information for shoreland property owners. (www.co.todd.mn.us/PlanningZoning/planning.htm)
