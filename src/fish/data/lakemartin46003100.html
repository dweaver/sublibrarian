Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/25/2005)

Hall is a 513 acre lake of which 277 acres are within the littoral zone. This means that 277 acres are 15 feet or less. The lake has been dredged and has a maximum depth of 27.0 feet. Hall is part of the Fairmont "chain" of lakes and is within the city of Fairmont. It is a class 24 lake and managed primarily for walleye while yellow perch, crappie, and channel catfish are managed secondarily. The lake is presently stocked every other year with walleye fingerlings at one pound per littoral acre and is due to be stocked again in 2006.

Hall has a decent walleye fishery present according to our sampling in 2005. More fish were sampled in trap nets than in gill nets which may indicate that fish were more easily caught in shallow water. Lengths of walleye caught in our trap nets ranged from 8.7 to 28.8 inches and averaged 15.3 inches with an average weight of 2.1 pounds. Six different ages were observed during the assessment from age 1 to age 11. Most of the fish sampled in 2005 appear to have been the result of either fry or fingerling stockings.

The yellow perch population is similar to other yellow perch populations in lakes like Hall. Lengths of yellow perch caught in our gill nets ranged from 5.9 to 10.5 inches and averaged 7.5 inches. Four different ages were observed during the assessment from age 1 to age 5.

Hall has a good crappie population present which includes both black and white crappies. An obvious difference between the two species is that white crappie have vertical bars on the side of their body. Lengths of crappie sampled in trap nets ranged from 5.0 inches to 11.4 inches and averaged right around 9.0 inches. Both black and white crappies were very healthy.

Hall also has a very good channel catfish population. Lengths of channel catfish caught in our gill nets ranged from 14.8 to 25.2 inches and averaged 19.0 inches. The channel catfish population had an average weight of 2.3 pounds. Four ages of channel catfish from age 5 to age nine were caught in the nets.

The bluegill population appears to be similar to the population that was there in 1997. Lengths of bluegill caught in our trap nets ranged from 6.9 to 8.8 inches and averaged 8.1 inches. Four ages from age 2 to age 5 were observed in our trap nets indicating good natural reproduction.

Other species of fish sampled in 2005 included black bullheads, carp, bigmouth buffalo, freshwater drum, quillback, and white suckers.

To promote and maintain healthy fish populations, pollution and other inputs need to be controlled. Fish habitats are directly affected by water quality. Nutrient, sediment and other waste inputs can drastically alter the biological, chemical, andy physical components of a lake. It is paramount to maintain if not improve the current water quality of Hall Lake through watershed management to preserve this fishery for future generations to enjoy.
