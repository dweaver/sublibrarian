Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/18/2008)

Little Bowstring Lake is a 319 acre lake located 16 miles northeast of Deer River, MN. The lake has a county access on the south shore and low to moderate shoreline development. The 2003 lake management plan indicated walleye were the primary species of management with bluegill and northern pike as secondary species.

The black crappie population has continued to be low to moderate density with good size structure. In 2008, the gill-net catch rate (1.2/net) approached the lake class median while the trap-net catch rate (0.6/net) was near the 1st quartile. The 16 fish sampled in the gill nets and trap nets ranged from 5.0 to 12.1 inches and had a mean length of 10.5 inches. Four year-classes from age 1 to age 6 were sampled. Age-5 and age-6 fish were the most prevalent, representing a combined 69% of the sample. Based on mean back-calculated lengths, growth was similar to statewide averages for all ages and class 31 lakes.

Bluegill trap-net catch rates have been low (ranging from 0 to 7.5/net) in all assessments. The catch in 2008 was the highest on record at 7.5 fish/net; which exceeded the 1st quartile of 6.1 fish/net for lake class 31. Gill-net catch rates for bluegill were also higher than usual. Gill nets sampled distinctly larger bluegill than trap nets. The fish captured with gill nets ranged from 4.1 to 9.5 inches and had a mean length of 8.2 inches while trap net sampled fish ranged from 3.4 to 7.6 inches and had a mean length of 4.8 inches. Although age and growth information was not collected in this assessment, growth was likely good in response to the lake fertility and low density population.

The gill-net catch rates have fluctuated from 1.5 northern pike/net in 1971 to the high of 10.9 northern pike/net in 2008. The four prior assessments captured northern pike from 6.1 to 7.8 fish/gill net. The 2008 catch was near the 3rd quartile of 10.5 fish/net. The sampled fish ranged from 14.6 to 32.4 inches and had a mean length of 21.2 inches. Eight year classes were sampled from age 1 to age 8. Each of the age-classes from two to five represented roughly 20% of the sample, indicating consistent recruitment. Growth was similar to the statewide averages for all ages.

The walleye gill-net catch rate of 11.3 fish/net exceeded the 3rd quartile of 5.0 fish/net. The catch in 2008 was the same as in 2002 and exceeded the management goal of 7.0 fish/gill net. Walleye from 6.8 to 26.5 inches were sampled from gill nets with a mean length of 14.8 inches. Ten year classes were determined by scales and bony structures. The 2007 and 2005 year-classes represented 28 and 40% of the sample, respectively. Based on age analysis, some natural reproduction exists but walleye fry stocking appears to be the primary source of fish driving the fishery. Growth was similar to statewide averages for all ages through age 10.

Yellow perch are an important prey for many predator species. Yellow perch were sampled at a gill-net catch rate of 57.1 fish/net, which exceeded the 3rd quartile of 43.6 fish/net. Historical gill-net catch rates have never been below the lake class median and have been above the 3rd quartile in three assessments. The sample was dominated by small fish, but fish ranged from 5.1 to 10.4 inches with a mean length of 7.0 inches. Age and growth information were not collected in this assessment.

Other species sampled during the population assessment included bowfin, brown bullhead, largemouth bass, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, white sucker, and yellow bullhead.

In order to maintain or improve fish and wildlife populations, water quality and habitat must be protected. People often associate water quality problems with large-scale agricultural, forestry, urban development or industrial practices in the watershed. In reality, the impact of land use decisions on one lake lot may be relatively small, yet, the cumulative impact of those decisions on many lake lots can result in a significant decline in water quality and habitat. For example, removing shoreline and aquatic vegetation, fertilizing lawns, mowing to the edge of the water, installing beach sand blankets, failing septic systems and uncontrolled run-off, all contribute excess nutrients and sediment which degrade water quality and habitat. Understanding these cumulative impacts and taking steps to avoid or minimize them will help to insure our quality fisheries can be enjoyed by future generations.?
