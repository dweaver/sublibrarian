* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/25/2001)

Ball Bluff Lake is a moderately fertile 159 acre lake located in northeastern Aitkin County just south of the town of Jacobson. A small proportion of the lake's surface area makes up shallow water areas less than 15 feet in depth. Consequently, food production and available spawning habitat is limited and fish growth of many fish species is restricted. Land use along the lake margin is primarily mixed hardwoods covering gentle to moderately rolling hills interspersed with lake homes or seasonal cabins along the west and northeast shores, while small areas of marshland exist along the north and south ends of the lake. The lake's fishery is managed primarily for walleye with largemouth bass and bluegill sunfish as the secondary management species.

The evaluation portion of the current management plan called for a population assessment in 2001 to evaluate walleye fry stocking success from 1995-1997 and to monitor other fish populations. During the open water season of 2001, spring night electrofishing followed by summer gill and trap netting were utilized for the evaluation. Walleye abundance was below normal for lakes of this type while the average weight, at 2.9 pounds, was within a normal range. Since 1952, five out of six sampling surveys found below normal walleye abundance. Only the 1993 assessment reported abundance within the normal range. The 4 walleye in 2001 ranged from 18.8 to 22.9 inches in length. The year classes represented in the sample were 1995, 1992, and 1990 and their growth was normal at all ages. According to historical records, walleye stocking occurred in 1995 and 1992 with fry and fingerlings, respectively. It appears that stocking success for the fry stocking period from 1995-1997 was minimal as indicated by the 2 walleye represented during this time. One other walleye was represented during a stocked year (1992). During non-stocked years, a single fish was represented. The presence of a year class during a non-stocked year indicates that some limited natural reproduction has occurred; however, fish represented during stocked years may have resulted from either stocking or natural reproduction. Survival of stocked walleye fingerlings has been historically variable and may be limited by the abundance of predator fish, such as northern pike, and low numbers of forage species for pike, such as yellow perch and tullibee. Spring night electrofishing found low numbers of largemouth bass while trap nets found abundance at a normal level. Bass lengths ranged from 5.2 to 21.8 inches. Bluegill sunfish numbers were found to be at a normal level for this type of lake. Lengths ranged from 3.2 to 8.9 inches with 52% of the sample ranging from 5.5 to 7.0 inches. Bluegill were found to grow slow from ages 2-6. Northern pike numbers were above normal while the average size was on the small side. Pike numbers and size were similar to the 1997 survey. The above normal abundance of northern pike experienced in 1997 and 2001 may have been the result of increased spawning area around the lake margin caused by high water levels flooding shoreline vegetation from the early 1990s to the fall of 2000. Northern pike lengths extended from 10.4 to 34.5 inches with 50% of the sample between 18.0 and 22.0 inches. Black crappie were found at normal abundance levels and their lengths ranged from 7.1 to 12.4 inches. The 2001 crappie catch rate was the highest ever recorded by DNR Fisheries assessments. The sampled fish community was also made up of dog fish, brown bullhead, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, tullibee, white sucker, yellow bullhead, and yellow perch. In addition to the above species, the least darter (Etheostoma microperca), a species of special concern, was sampled during a county biological survey in 2001.

An angler fishing Ball Bluff Lake may encounter an occasional nice sized walleye or largemouth bass, an abundance of smaller northern pike with an occasional memorable sized fish, or a fair number of panfish. Anglers are encouraged to be selective if they keep fish by harvesting the smaller ones for eating and releasing the larger ones. Releasing the larger fish will help maintain or improve the current quality of the fish population and provide future angling opportunities for others who would like to catch a quality sized fish. Shoreline property owners and surrounding land users within the watershed can maintain or improve the lake's water quality by protecting the existing abundance of natural shoreline; re-establishing buffer strips of native plant species along the developed shoreline; updating outdated or failing septic systems; using phosphorus free fertilizer (if needed) on surrounding lawns, gardens, or crop land; utilizing farming practices that reduce run-off; limiting aquatic plant removal or disturbance; and limiting construction of impervious surfaces near the shoreline.
