* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/30/2003)

Buffalo Lake is located in northeastern Murray County near the town of Dovray. Buffalo Lake has a surface area of 124 acres, a maximum depth of 8.5 feet with very sparse stands of aquatic plants along the shoreline. An aeration system was installed in 1994 to help prevent winterkill, and no documented winterkill has occurred since then. Buffalo Lake is currently managed for northern pike, yellow perch and black crappie.

The northern pike population is above average compared to similar lakes. The catch rate of 12.0 in 2003 more than doubled the catch rate of 5.3 sampled in 1998. The northern pike caught in our nets ranged from 20 to 27 inches with an average length of 23. Although it would appear fair spawning habitat is available in a nearby wildlife refuge wetland, natural reproduction has shown limited success. Northern pike fingerlings were stocked in 2001. With good numbers of northern pike swimming in Buffalo Lake, the potential for excellent northern fishing exists in the near future.

The yellow perch population is low compared to similar lakes, as numbers are down substantially from a catch rate of 22.0 in 1998, when anglers reported good perch fishing. Since perch numbers are presently low, stocking will be considered in the future.

Buffalo Lake crappie numbers greatly exceed the average found in similar lakes. Crappie sampled in nets ranged from 5 to 14 inches with an average of 7. Adult crappie were stocked in 1999 after the 1998 assessment sampled only one crappie. Three strong year classes of natural reproduction have resulted from the 1999 stocking. Although the average crappie size is small, good growth rates were recorded and could produce great crappie fishing as early as 2004.

The walleye population is low compared to similar lakes. Walleye ranged in length from 16 to 17.5 inches with an average weight of 1.8 pounds. All walleye sampled were two year old fish, representing a single year class from 2001. Buffalo Lake is not managed for walleye and has never been stocked with walleye by the Department of Natural Resources, thus, the source of these fish is unknown.

Other species of fish sampled during the survey included bigmouth buffalo, common carp, white sucker and fathead minnows.

To promote and maintain healthy fish populations, pollution and other inputs need to be controlled. Fish habitats are directly affected by water quality. Nutrient, sediment and other waste inputs can drastically alter the biological, chemical and physical components of a lake. It is important to maintain if not improve the current water quality of Buffalo Lake through watershed management to preserve this fishery for future generations.
