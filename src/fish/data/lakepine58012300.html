Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

No fish consumption guidelines are available for this lake. For more information, see the "Fish Consumption Advice" pages at the Minnesota Department of Health.


Status of the Fishery (as of 08/06/2007)

Grindstone is a 526 acre lake managed for rainbow, brown, and lake trout and assigned to lake class 23. The lake is extremely deep with adequate oxygen levels to 110 feet. Although Grindstone is managed for trout, it is not a designated trout lake. Therefore, anglers only need a trout stamp if they plan on possessing trout. The two stream trout species (brown and rainbow) are currently maintained through annual yearling stockings. Two thousand five hundred brown trout are stocked each spring. Five thousand rainbow trout are stocked each spring with up to 3,000 more added in the fall when available. Yearling lake trout are now stocked biennially. The lake was netted in August 2007 to assess lake management goals and current stocking protocols. Twenty four gillnet sets were made in three depth stratums: 10-30', 40-70', and 80-120'. Gillnet catch rates reported in this summary are relative to species temperature tolerances. Hence, catch rates for warm-water species are calculated from the 9 shallow sets while the coldwater species are from the 15 sets deeper than 30 feet.

Lake trout abundance, based on the gillnet catch rate of 4.2 was well above the lake class norm, down from the 2002 rate and identical to the 1997 catch. Size structure has climbed some with a mode and mean between 13 and 14 inches and a 1.2 pound average. Measured lake trout ranged from 7.6 to 30.0 inches in length. Lake trout relative weights, a comparison of fish plumpness to a species-specific standard, were in excess of 100 for all length groups. This indicates that the forage base is likely being under utilized. Yearling lake trout were stocked annually from 1996 through 2005 when biennial stocking began.

The rainbow trout catch rate, 0.6 per gillnet, was similar in 2002. Sampled rainbows ranged from 12.1 to 21.4 inches and averaged 1.1 pounds. Nine of the ten rainbows sampled were from recent yearling stockings. The one larger fish was the result of the stocking of 77 surplus hatchery brood fish in 2006. Shore angling for rainbows is popular early in the spring until the warming of surface water pushes fish off shore into deeper water.

Although yearlings were again stocked in May of 2007, the 6 sampled brown trout were from the 130 surplus brood fish stocked in the fall of 2006. These fish averaged 19.5 inches with a mean weight of 3.1 pounds.

Rainbow smelt were first sampled in the lake in 1963. How smelt were introduced into the lake is unknown but the population has become well established. Although they provide a forage base for northern pike and larger trout there may be some adverse effects. The tullibee (cisco) population has declined since the establishment of smelt, which compete for the same forage. Tullibee have not been sampled in Grindstone Lake since the 1992 assessment. It is also believed that smelt compete with young trout for forage. A total of 102 smelt were caught in 2007, more than in any previous sampling. Netted smelt ranged from 3.8 to 9.7 inches.

Northern pike have always maintained normal population levels compared to other lakes in the class. Historically, northerns have been larger than normal. The current netting was no exception with a catch rate of 3.9 northerns per net and an average weight of 3.4 pounds. Northerns ranged from 11.1 to 33.0 inches and have provided a trophy fishery with several fish over 40 inches caught or speared in recent years. Pike weights were very close to the standard until they reached 600 mm (24 inches). At this point weights began to exceed the standard, i.e. relative weight values began to exceed 100. Pike mouth gape (roughly 60 mm for a 600mm northern) allows predation on forage up to 80mm in depth. Rainbow trout depths don't begin to exceed 80mm until they reach 12 inches in length.

Yellow perch abundance has historically been low. The 2007 catch was near the 50th percentile for the lake class, down slightly since 2002. Perch were between 6 and 7 inches, a size unacceptable to most anglers.

Black crappie were again sampled above normal levels. Size has improved some with and 64% of the catch exceeding 8 inches. Bluegill abundance was below the lake class norm. Size structure was not great with relatively few fish over 7 inches available.

Others species present in the lake included greater redhorse, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass, smallmouth bass, yellow bullhead, and white sucker. ?
