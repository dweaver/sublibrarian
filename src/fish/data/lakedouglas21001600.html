Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/17/2006)

Smith Lake is a 575-acre basin located between Alexandria and Osakis. Maximum depth is 30.0 ft. Mean depth is 15.0 ft. Average water transparency during the summer is 5.2 ft. Water clarity is periodically impaired during summer months due to periodic algal blooms stimulated by excessive total phosphorus concentrations and seasonal die-off of curly-leaf pondweed. Aquatic recreational use suitability is classified as "non-supporting". In spite of seasonal impairments, the fish community in Smith Lake is diverse and the lake supports a reasonably good fishing. Habitat attributes are typical of a "bass-panfish" lake. Primary gamefishes include walleye, northern pike, largemouth bass, bluegill, and black crappie.

Smith Lake supports a low-density population of large walleye. Consumptive anglers might be disappointed with a fairly low harvest rate, but anglers seeking larger walleye should not dismiss Smith Lake. Walleye grow fast. Fifty-six percent of the walleye captured in gillnets during the 2006 survey exceeded 20.0 inches in length. Seven percent of those walleye exceeded 25.0 inches. Average weight of walleye netted was 2.9 lbs.

Northern pike catch rates in seven of the previous eight fish surveys were near or exceeded the range of catches considered normal for similar lakes. Catches during the 2006 survey were below the range of expected catches. A net decrease in pike abundance would be desirable, but the scale of population decline introduces uncertainty in accuracy of 2006 catch statistics. In contrast to expectations of reduced population abundance, average pike growth was slower and size distribution of captures was consistent with previous survey findings. This species was probably under-represented in the total catch due to high water temperatures and reduced vulnerability to capture during the mid-July survey. Fishing experiences should be good. Twenty-eight inch pike are fairly common in the 2006 survey. Average weight was 2.5 lbs.

Populations of largemouth bass, bluegill, and black crappie can be qualified as "moderately" abundant. Average size of largemouth bass captured in 2006 was 13.0 inches in length. Fishing quality is reasonably good. Thirty-nine percent of electrofishing captures exceeded 15.0 inches in length. Bluegill and black crappie are abundant, but fishing potential is limited by slow growth and relatively short life span. Eight-year-old bluegill captured in 2006 averaged only 7.0 inches in length. Few bluegill live long enough to grow to 8.0-inches. Likewise, few black crappie grow to 10.0 inches before succumbing to harvest or natural mortality.

Smith Lake is a good choice for young or beginning anglers. They can expect high catch rates of medium-size panfishes. Anglers are encouraged to harvest those small- and medium-size panfishes, particularly bluegill. Excessive abundance of 4.5 - 7.0-inch bluegill constitutes an energy sink. Most are too small to stimulate adequate harvest, but too big to be consumed by most resident predators. Harvest of hammer-handle northern pike may increase survival and abundance of walleye.
