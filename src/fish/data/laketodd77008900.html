Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/23/2007)

Little Birch Lake is a deep (89 feet) lake located between Big Birch Lake and Sylvia Lake on the Todd/Stearns County line. The lake has an abundance of deep water structure and habitat as only 33% of the lake is 15 feet deep or less. Agriculture is more apparent around the main basin of the lake which has most of the shallow water habitats. Mixed forest hillsides and fast drop-offs are characteristic of the long arm of the lake. The majority of the development on the lake is also located along both sides of the arm. Water clarity was average for lakes in this area of the state with a secchi disk reading of 6.7 feet in mid-July. Aquatic vegetation was found to grow to a depth of 16 feet with chara or muskgrass being the most common submergent species. Hardstem bulrush was the most common emergent vegetation species with most beds being found in the shallower main basin. Sand and gravel were the most common shoal or shallow water substrates. Both curly leaf pondweed and eurasian milfoil were documented while doing transects but the abundance rating was rare for both. Anglers and lakeshore owners should be especially diligent about cleaning their boats and trailers when leaving the lake to prevent the spread of the exotics to other lakes in the area. Fishing is a popular pastime on the lake and Little Birch supports a diverse fish community which can provide plenty of angling opportunities. Little Birch is a popular walleye lake year round and a wide range of sizes were observed in the summer survey although most were between 12 to 14 inches. Numbers were down from the last survey and below management goals but remained within the normal range when compared to similar type lakes. Although natural reproduction does contribute some fish to the population, it does not appear enough to meet the desired population goals. Walleye stocking occurs with either fry or fingerlings being stocked every year. We are currently evaluating whether fry or fingerling stocking works better. More northern pike were caught in the 2007 survey than in the last survey which is an undesirable development. Most of the pike caught in the survey were between 19 and 23 inches with some up to 34 inches seen. Anglers are encouraged to harvest the smaller northern pike under 24 inches and release the larger ones to help maintain a balanced fish community and promote good growth rates. Lower northern pike numbers may also increase small walleye survival and allow the low yellow perch and tullibee populations to increase. Higher perch and tullibee populations are desirable as they can be important prey species for both walleye and northern pike. Little Birch Lake supports both smallmouth and largemouth bass populations with the smallmouth bass being much more common. Smallmouth abundance was almost 4 times more than what is normally seen in these types of lakes. Despite the abundant population, fish in the 15 - 16 inch range were common and trophy-size smallmouth bass up to 20 inches were documented in the survey. Largemouth bass numbers appear to normal for this type of lake. Average size seen in the summer survey was about ten inches, however, bass up to 18 inches were observed in a spring assessment done on the lake. An abundance of bulrush and waterlily along the shoreline provides excellent habitat for both species. Black crappies and bluegills are both much sought after species in the lake. Neither of the species appears to be over abundant and some nice size fish of both species can be found. Anglers have reported good winter fishing in the past. The 2007 summer survey used only gillnets to assess the fish community in Little Birch Lake which would not sample the shallow water areas where bluegill typically hang out. A spring assessment was also conducted in 2007 to evaluate the crappies and bluegills. The assessment was not very effective at catching crappies but did provide a good sample of bluegills. Little Birch Lake appears to have a bluegill population that would attract most anglers. Bluegills over 6.5 inches were common in the spring assessment with fish up to nine inches measured. A variety of sizes was found in the black crappie catches between the spring and summer surveys with fish up to 12 inches being observed. One fish that may surprise most anglers on the lake could be the presence of channel catfish. The fish was first documented in the 1999 survey and likely came up the Sauk River system and entered the Little Birch Lake from Sylvia Lake. While fewer catfish were caught in the 2007 survey than in 2003, the fish were in excellent condition and could put up a fight. All of the catfish seen in the summer surveys have been between 19 and 27 inches in length. Anglers have also reported catching catfish. Some other species of fish from the same family found in the lake are bullheads. While yellow bullheads were the only species to be caught in the summer survey some black and brown bullheads were sampled in the spring assessment. The average size of the yellow bullheads was about 12 inches. The presence of yellow bullheads can be an indicator of good water quality as they are more abundant in lakes with clear water as opposed to black bullheads which are more abundant in lakes with turbid water. Carp abundance appears to have decreased since the last survey and numbers do not appear to be at levels detrimental to the lake. Carp have a reputation for rooting up aquatic vegetation and increasing water turbidity. Tullibee or cisco abundance was also down which could mean fewer desirable-sized prey for larger northern pike and walleye which tend to suspend in cooler water which tullibee usually inhabit. Tullibee are a cold water species that sometime has difficulty locating adequate habitat in summer months when the cooler, deeper water becomes depleted of oxygen and the upper layers of water become too warm. Other species sampled in the summer survey that make up part of the fish community include pumpkinseeds, rock bass, shorthead redhorse, and white sucker. Some additional species caught in the spring assessment were bowfin or dogfish, great redhorse, green sunfish, and hybrid sunfish. ?
