Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/04/2007)

East Graham, in Murray County, is one of several lakes that are connected to the West Fork of the Des Moines River. It is a 604-acre lake with a maximum depth of 8 feet. East Graham has a history of low dissolved oxygen in winters as it was opened to liberalized fishing 16 times from 1952 to 1982. A helixor aeration system was installed in 1985 and winterkill has not occurred since then. In 2007, Murray County replaced the helixor system with Power House Ice Eaters. East Graham is managed primarily for walleye and northern pike and secondarily for black crappie and yellow perch. A population assessment was conducted during the week of June 4, 2007 using 3 gill nets and 8 trap nets.

Walleye abundance has rebounded since the 2003 survey. The catch rate of walleye in gill nets was 14.7/set, which was within the expected range. Many (59%) of the fish were 14 inches and greater. Ageing information from bone structures indicated most (75%) of the fish were from a fry stocking in 2004. Previous surveys on East Graham also documented success of fry stocking. Anglers should definitely consider East Graham if they are looking to take a couple of eaters home for a meal.

Northern pike catch rate in East Graham has been relatively consistent over the years. The catch rate in gill nets was 3.7/set in 2007, 4.0/set in 2003, 4.0/set in 1999, and 2.6/set in 1995. The large watershed and a connection to the West Fork of the Des Moines River likely contribute to the consistent northern pike abundance. No northern pike have been stocked in East Graham since 1994. Total lengths in 2007 ranged from 17.6 to 30.0 inches with an average of 22.3 inches. There is real potential to catch some northern pike in the middle of summer if an angler would target the cool water refuge of field tile inputs.

Black crappie abundance was the highest ever documented on East Graham. Total lengths ranged from 7.4 to 13.0 inches with an average of 10.6 inches. Most of the fish (87%) were 10 inches and greater. The take home message is that if an angler wants some potentially good crappie fishing, consider East Graham as your first stop. The numbers are high and the sizes are big.

Yellow perch abundance in East Graham has never been considered "good" over the years. Only 4 yellow perch were sampled in 2007 in gill nets and trap nets combined. Consider some other area lake for yellow perch angling.

Black bullhead abundance equaled the highest recorded on East Graham. Total lengths ranged from 5.1 to 8.5 inches with an average of 6.5 inches. If you want some bullheads in the spring consider some other area lakes for some bigger bullheads.

Other species captured were bigmouth buffalo, channel catfish, common carp, green sunfish, and white suckers.

Efforts to improve the health of the lake should focus on best management practices within the watershed. Improvements and education in land stewardship will often have secondary benefits to the lake in the way of quality and quantity of habitat. Generally, improvements in the quality and quantity of lake habitat will mean direct improvements of the fishery.
