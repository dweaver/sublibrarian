Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

No fish consumption guidelines are available for this lake. For more information, see the "Fish Consumption Advice" pages at the Minnesota Department of Health.


Status of the Fishery (as of 06/23/2005)

A fish resurvey was conducted during late June of 2005 in Monson Lake. Monson is a small sized (152 acres), moderately deep (21 feet maximum depth), and moderately productive lake located in Swift County. Monson Lake State Park is located along the east side of the lake. Residential development is limited to 3 homes and 1 recreational vehicle site along the south, north and west shore areas. The public access is located on the east side in the state park. The surrounding watershed is comprised of mostly agricultural row crops with small areas of woodland, grassland, pasture, and wetland areas. The immediate shoreline area consists mostly of undeveloped woodland (hardwood trees) with small to moderate areas of wetlands, row crops, and grassland. Agricultural row crop runoff (via inlets) is the primary source of nutrient pollution into Monson. Nutrient levels were low to moderate (total phosphorus=0.028 ppm) during 2005 in Monson. There are three small sized inlets (northwest, northeast, and south shores) that flow into Monson. The outlet connects to West Sunburg Lake. Water levels have been high in Monson during recent years. Water clarity was good (secchi= 7.83 feet) during the June 2005 resurvey. Blue-green algae blooms can be common during the mid to late summer months in Monson Lake. Submergent vegetation is dense and diverse with northern milfoil, various pondweed species (flat-stem, curly-leaf, clasping-leaf, leafy, sago pondweed), coontail, water moss, and filamentous algae present in Monson. Emergent vegetation is also abundant and diverse consisting of various bulrush species, cattail species, duckweed species, burreed, arrowhead, and reed canary grass scattered throughout the bay, shoreline, and island areas. Shoalwater substrates consist primarily of sand, detritus, gravel, and silt. Monson Lake is managed primarily for walleye, northern pike, largemouth bass, black crappie, yellow perch, and bluegill. Walleye fingerlings or yearlings are generally stocked into Monson every other year.

Northern pike numbers were moderate in 2005 (4.75 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal range for similar lakes. The northern pike historical average catch rate is 4.08 fish/gillnet for Monson. The 2005 northern pike average size was moderate (2.87 pounds and 23.7 inches) from gillnets. The northern pike historical average weight is 2.55 pounds from gillnets. The 2005 catch rate of quality size (21.0 inches and larger) northern pike was moderate (3.75 fish/gillnet) for Monson. Northern pike growth rates in Monson were below the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-8. The 2000 northern pike year class comprised 30% of the 2005 northern pike resurvey catch. The largest northern pike captured was 32.2 inches from the 2005 assessment.

Black crappie numbers were moderate to abundant in 2005 (3.67 fish/trapnet) compared to the normal range for similar lakes. The black crappie historical average catch rate is 0.90 fish/trapnet for Monson. The 2005 black crappie average size was small (0.14 pounds and 5.4 inches) from trapnets. The black crappie historical average weight is 0.27 pounds from trapnets. Black crappie growth rates in Monson were generally below the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-3. The 2004 and 2003 black crappie year classes comprised 41% and 37% respectively of the 2005 black crappie resurvey catch.

Yellow perch numbers were abundant in 2005 (25.25 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal range for similar lakes. The yellow perch historical average catch rate is 10.18 fish/gillnet for Monson Lake. The 2005 yellow perch average size was small (0.12 pounds and 6.2 inches) from gillnets. The yellow perch historical average weight is 0.27 pounds from gillnets. Yellow perch growth rates in Monson were within the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-4. The 2003 year class comprised 82% of the 2005 yellow perch resurvey catch.

Bluegill numbers were moderate in 2005 (14.11 fish/trapnet) compared to the normal range for similar lakes. The bluegill historical catch rate is 3.94 fish/trapnet for Monson. The 2005 bluegill average size was small to moderate (0.19 pounds and 5.6 inches) from trapnets. The bluegill historical average weight is 0.28 pounds from trapnets. The 2005 catch rate of quality size (6.0 inches and larger) bluegill was moderate to high (6.44 fish/trapnet) in Monson. Bluegill growth rates in Monson Lake were within the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-4, but above the normal ranges for ages 5-8. The 2000 and 2003 bluegill year classes comprised 28% and 32% respectively of the 2005 bluegill resurvey catch. The largest bluegill captured was 9.1 inches from the 2005 assessment.

Walleye numbers were moderate in 2005 (2.00 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal range for similar lakes, but low compared to the Monson historical average catch rate (4.48 fish/gillnet). The 2005 walleye average size was large (3.09 pounds and 19.1 inches) from gillnets. The walleye historical average weight is 1.97 pounds from gillnets. Walleye growth rates for Monson were generally within or above the Spicer Area normal ranges. The 2001 walleye year class comprised 33% of the 2005 walleye resurvey catch. Walleye natural reproduction in Monson is generally infrequent and insignificant based on previous fall and summer surveys. The largest walleye captured was 25.5 inches from the 2005 assessment.

Largemouth bass numbers were moderate in the 2005 spring electrofishing survey (52.94 fish/hour) compared to the Spicer Area average catch rate (47.20 fish/hour). The largemouth bass historical average catch rate is 41.83 fish/hour for Monson. The 2005 largemouth bass average size was large (2.44 pounds and 16.0 inches) from spring electrofishing. The largemouth bass historical average weight and length were 1.92 pounds and 13.8 inches from spring electrofishing for Monson. The 2005 catch rate of preferred size (15.0 inches and larger) largemouth bass was high (39.71 fish/hour) and above the Monson historical average (14.27 fish/hour). Largemouth bass growth rates in Monson Lake were above the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-8. The 1997, 1998, and 1999 year classes comprised 22%, 23%, and 20% respectively of the 2005 total largemouth bass spring electrofishing catch. The largest largemouth bass captured was 18.9 inches from the 2005 spring electrofishing survey.

Black bullhead numbers were moderate in 2005 (39.50 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal ranges for similar lakes. The black bullhead historical average catch rate is 128.10 fish/gillnet for Monson. The 2005 black bullhead average size was large (0.90 pounds and 11.11 inches) from gillnets. The black bullhead historical average weight is 0.67 pounds from gillnets.

Current fish management activities on Monson include monitoring the fish population on a periodic basis, protecting aquatic vegetation through the permit process, participating in local watershed initiatives, and stocking various fish species as warranted. The Monson fishery will be sampled in the 2010 summer for all fish species.
