Status of the Fishery (as of 06/25/2001)

Red Lake is a small, shallow, minimally developed basin located just northwest of the town of Palisade. The majority of the surrounding shoreline is state owned tax forfeit land except for the south end of the lake which is privately owned. A carry-in access for small boats is available through the state land along the southwest shore or permission may be obtained to access the lake from a private landowner. The immediate shoreline surrounding the lake is characterized primarily by deciduous forest followed by pasture land and marshland. Eighty-seven percent of the lakes 83 surface acres consist of water depths at 15 feet or less. Since shallow water depths are characteristic of this basin, this type of lake may experience low oxygen levels, especially during favorable winter conditions, which can cause partial or severe fish winterkills. According to historical DNR Fisheries records, a fish winterkill has never been documented, but inadequate oxygen levels have been reported during previous winter dissolved oxygen monitoring.

In response to several public requests regarding the current status of the fish population, a special assessment was conducted utilizing gill and trap nets during the summer of 2001. When compared to similar lakes; black crappie and dog fish were above normal in abundance; bluegill sunfish, northern pike, and yellow perch were within a normal abundance range; and black, brown, and yellow bullhead and pumpkinseed sunfish were below normal in number. Measured northern pike ranged from 15.1 to 31.9 inches with an average length of 20.8 inches. Crappie lengths extended from 4.5 to 11.5 inches with an average length of 7.7 inches while bluegill sunfish lengths ranged from 3.7 to 8.7 inches with an average length of 6.8 inches. Of the 50 bluegill sampled in trap nets, 50% were at 7.0 inches or greater in length.

An anglers fishing experience on Red Lake may include good numbers of larger sized northern pike, an abundance of smaller black crappie, and good numbers of larger bluegill. Fishing enthusiasts are encouraged to be selective when harvesting fish by keeping the smaller ones for eating and releasing the larger ones. Releasing the larger fish will help maintain or improve the quality of the fish population and provide future angling opportunties for others who would like to experience catching a quality sized fish.
