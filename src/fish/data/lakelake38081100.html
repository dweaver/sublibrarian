Fish Stocking Activity

Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/20/2007)

Fall Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 7, which consists of 41 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are very large, have irregular shoreline shapes, and have very soft (unmineralized) water. Fall Lake is shallower than most of the lakes in this lake class.

Fall Lake ranks as slightly eutrophic according to Carlson's Trophic State Index. The major inlet, the Kawishiwi River, contributes bog-stained water to Fall Lake, while much of the phosphorus likely originates in Shagawa Lake, which for decades prior to the 1970's received poorly treated sewage from the City of Ely. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline of Fall Lake are mostly boulder and rubble. Aquatic plants are sparse and grow to a depth of 4 ft.

Fall Lake was thermally stratified on 08/20/2007, with a surface temperature of 68 F and a bottom temperature of 62 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 22 ft, where the temperature was 66 F. Fall Lake does not stratify in many summers, and retains good levels of oxygen to within a foot or two of the bottom. Water levels often vary as much as 4 ft annually in Fall Lake, due in part to the operation of the Minnesota Power hydroelectric plant on the Kawishiwi River inlet to Fall Lake.

The northeast portion of Fall Lake is in the Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness (BWCAW); motorboats in this part of the lake are limited to 25 horsepower and BWCAW permits are required. Public accesses to Fall Lake are in Winton and at the USFS Fall Lake Campground, which is a major entry point to Newton and Basswood Lakes in the BWCAW.

Fish populations in 2007 were sampled with 14 trapnet sets and 12 gillnet sets. Thirteen previous investigations, dating back to 1959, used 3-21 gillnet sets. Eight of these previous investigations used 4-34 trapnet sets. Shoreline seining was done in four of these investigations.

Fish populations in 2007, as in most previous investigations, were dominated by walleye and northern pike, followed by white sucker and yellow perch. Cisco numbers in 2007 were lower than normal for this lake. Most fish were caught in gillnets (average of 30 fish/net, 27 lb/net). Low numbers of fish, typical for this lake, were caught in trapnets (average of 8 fish/net, 15 lb/net).

Walleye numbers in 2007 (11.9/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were identical to the median catch of 11.9/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes in 2007 averaged 12.2" (0.7 lb), which was in the first quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 12.5" in all investigations on this lake. The largest walleye in 2007, captured in a trapnet, was 22.9". Ages 1-3 walleye were all well represented, comprising 79 percent of the total catch. Walleye have not been stocked in Fall Lake since 1990, and natural reproduction has been sufficient to maintain a walleye population at historic levels for this lake and higher than normal for this lake class. Growth of walleye captured in 2007 was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile) by area standards.

Northern pike numbers in 2007 (4.2/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 2.8/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2007 averaged 19.0" (1.6 lb), which was in the first quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 19.4" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike in 2007, captured in a trapnet, was 37.9". Most of the pike (59 percent) were age 2. Pike growth was normal (in the second or third quartile, depending on the age) by area standards.

Perch numbers in 2007 (5.8/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median catch of 6.1/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Perch sizes in 2007 averaged 9.6" (0.5 lb), which was in the fourth quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 8.6" in all investigations on this lake. The largest perch captured in 2007 was 12.4". Many of the perch (35 percent) were age five, but ages 2-6 were all well represented. Perch growth was faster than normal (in the fourth quartile) by area standards.

Cisco numbers in 2007 (4.6/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 10.7/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Cisco catches have been lower than normal in the last five investigations on this lake, beginning in 1993. Cisco sizes in 2007 averaged 12.2" (0.7 lb), which was in the fourth quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 10.6" in all investigations on this lake. The largest cisco captured in 2007 was 17.7".

Black crappie and bluegill numbers in 2007, as in previous investigations, were low. Some good-sized crappie (12") and bluegill (9") were captured. Smallmouth bass numbers were likely underrepresented in the 2007 investigation, as bass are "net shy" and difficult to catch in standard sampling nets. The largest smallmouth captured in 2007 was 14.8".

Some of the northern pike, walleye and yellow perch examined in 2007 were infected with neascus, and some of the yellow perch were infected with yellow grub. Neascus (black spot) and yellow grub are common parasites that are native to the area. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.

Rusty crayfish were first observed in the Shagawa River, upstream of Fall Lake, in 1984. They were first captured in Fall Lake in the 1986 investigation, and their numbers subsequently increased from 42/gillnet in 1989 to a high of 89/gillnet in 2003, then declined to 35/gillnet in 2007.?
