Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/20/2007)

Clear Lake is a 68-acre lake located near Russell, MN in Lyon County. The lake has a maximum depth of 11 feet, a shoreline length of 1.3 miles and is a class 42 lake. It is managed primarily for yellow perch while walleye, crappie, northern pike, and bluegill are managed secondarily.

The only stocking record we had prior to the 1995 Initial Survey was a 1962 stocking of northern pike fingerlings done via transfer permit. Local interest in the lake inspired the development of a public access and boat ramp, which was completed in 1996. The initial lake survey, conducted in 1995, found a surprisingly diverse fish population, including such sport species as largemouth bass, bluegill, and yellow perch, that had never been officially stocked. Since there is no true inlet or outlet to account for fish immigration, the presence of these species is probably due to unauthorized stocking activity. No common carp or bigmouth buffalo have been sampled in Clear Lake, which is unique in Southwest Minnesota.

Clear Lake shows promise as an angling lake, especially with its proximity to the town of Russell, it being one of relatively few lakes in the immediate area, and its basically favorable physical characteristics. Most of the lake bottom is sand and gravel, the watershed is relatively small, and flooded trees and brush provide some spawning habitat and cover.

Clear Lake has a fairly good yellow perch population. The yellow perch gill net catch rate of 5.00 per net in 2007 compared to 104.5 per net in 2003 and 169.0 per net in 1999. Lengths of yellow perch caught in gill nets ranged from 9.4 to 10.7 inches and averaged 9.8 inches.

The walleye population is also doing well in Clear Lake. The walleye gill net catch rate was 5.5 per set in 2007 compared to 17.0 per set in 2003 and 10.50 per set in 1999. Lengths of walleye caught in gill nets ranged from 10.9 to 26.5 inches with an average length of 18.0 inches. Five different ages of walleye were observed during the assessment of which four age classes coincided with walleye fingerling stockings.

The bluegill population is low in Clear Lake. The bluegill trap net catch rate was 1.3 per set in 2007 compared to 0 per set in 2003 and 17.0per set in 1999. Lengths of bluegill caught in trap nets ranged from 3.5 to 9.5 inches and averaged 7.4 inches.

Clear Lake has a fairly good black crappie population. The black crappie trap net catch rate was 3.5 per net in 2007 compared to 0.3 per net in 2003 and 66.3 per net in 1999. Lengths of black crappie caught in trap nets ranged from 5.2 to 10.0 inches and averaged 8.0 inches.

Clear Lake has a good black bullhead population. The black bullhead trap net catch rate was 37.8 per net in 2007 compared to 150.3 per net in 2003 and 42.7 per net in 1999. Lengths of black bullhead caught in trap nets ranged from 7.6 to 8.8 inches with an average length of 8.1 inches.

Other species sampled during the assessment included largemouth bass and white sucker.

To maintain a healthy fishery in Clear Lake, we need to promote Best Management Practices (BMPs) within the watershed to help reduce nutrients entering the lake. High nutrients and sediments in a lake can cause algae blooms and reduce water clarity.
