Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/12/2004)

Third Crow Wing is located in southeastern Hubbard County, eight miles east of the town of Hubbard. Third Crow Wing has a surface area of 647 acres and a maximum depth of 35 feet. There is a state-owned access located on the northwest shore of the lake. The Crow Wing River flows from Fourth into Third then into Second Crow Wing. Boating between the lakes is possible with a small craft. Third Crow Wing is a popular lake for panfishing, as well as supporting good populations of northern pike, walleye, and largemouth bass.

The Minnesota Department of Natural Resources (DNR) has classified Minnesota's lakes into 43 different types bases on physical and chemical characteristics. Third Crow Wing is in lake class 25. Other area lakes in this same classification include: Belle Taine, West Crooked, Upper and Lower Ojibway, Eagle, Island, Big and Little Mantrap, Little Sand, and Spider.

Panfish are highly sought after by anglers on Third Crow Wing. Both bluegill and pumpkinseed were sampled in moderate numbers, within the range "typical" for this lake class. Anglers will find good numbers of bluegill and pumpkinseed in the 6-8 inch size range. The number of larger bluegill sampled (greater than 8.0 inches) was lower than past surveys. Black crappies numbers were down slightly when compared to past surveys, however, gillnet and trapnet catch rates were still within the range "typical" for this lake class. Both bluegill and black crappie exhibited good growth rates, similar to other class 25 lakes.

Walleye were sampled at their lowest abundance (2.2 walleye/gillnet) for all surveys dating back to 1956. The 2004 walleye catch rate of 2.2 walleye/gillnet is well below the current management goal of 6.0 walleye/gillnet. Walleye abundance was much higher in the 1990's, when walleye gillnet catch rates ranged from 10.2 walleye/net (1994) to 6.6 walleye/net (1999). Fingerling stocking by the DNR comprises a large proportion of the walleye population. Age analysis showed that the majority of the walleye sampled corresponded to stocked years with limited contribution from natural reproduction. It is hoped that through aggressive walleye fingerling stocking and with a little help from Mother Nature, walleye abundance in Third Crow Wing will increase. Third Crow Wing is currently stocked with walleye fingerlings during odd years. Important walleye and northern pike forage species such as yellow perch and tullibee (cisco) were sampled in good numbers. The yellow perch in Third Crow Wing are generally not an acceptable size for angling. Walleye exhibited good growth rates when compared to other class 25 lakes.

Northern pike abundance (8.1 pike/gillnet) was at the upper end of the range "typical" for this lake class. Northern pike abundance was also very high in the late 1990's, while earlier surveys had pike numbers at low to moderate levels. Size of northern pike was good when compared to other area lakes. Sampled northern pike had an average length and weight of 24.7 inches and 3.6 pounds. Northern pike growth rates were fast when compared to other class 25 lakes.

Third Crow Wing supports a decent largemouth bass fishery. Largemouth bass were sampled in low to moderate numbers (19.5 bass/hour) when compared to other lakes in the Crow Wing chain, such as Fifth and Sixth Crow Wing. The number of largemouth bass sampled over 15.0 inches was low compared to other area lakes. Good water quality, submerged and emergent aquatic vegetation cover, and spawning substrate provide excellent habitat for largemouth bass in Third Crow Wing.

Other species sampled included high numbers of white sucker and moderate numbers of shorthead redhorse and bowfin. Brown bullhead, yellow bullhead, rock bass, and carp were sampled in low numbers.
