Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/24/2002)

Little Cut Foot Sioux Lake is located 15 miles northwest of Deer River, MN in the Mississippi River watershed. The lake has a surface area of 660 acres and a maximum depth of 20 feet. Little Cut Foot Sioux is in lake class 35. Other area lakes in this class are Lawrence and Pughole Lakes. Little Cut Foot Sioux is a fairly productive lake that occasionally experiences winterkill. The lake is within the Chippewa National Forest and the Leech Lake Indian Reservation. The US Forest Service maintains an earthen ramp boat access on the southwest corner of the lake. Access is also available by boat through a channel from Cut Foot Sioux Lake.

Little Cut Foot Sioux is connected to Cut Foot Sioux and Winnibigoshish Lakes, and the abundance of many fish species fluctuates due to seasonal migrations within the system. Little Cut Foot experiences a large migration of walleye during spring. The Minnesota DNR operates a walleye egg-taking operation on Little Cut Foot Sioux each spring, with a percentage of the collected walleye eggs returned to the lake as fry. Walleye gill-net catch rates during June assessments have been variable, and are likely affected by early summer weather patterns which influence the migration of walleye back out to Cut Foot Sioux and Winnibigoshish. The walleye gill-net catch was 2.3/net in 2002, which is slightly above the lake class average. The fish ranged from 7.1 to 24.4 inches, with a mean length of 15.1 inches. Scale analysis identified fish from ages 1-6 and 8, with ages 3 and 4 making up 67% of the sample. Growth was considered average.

Northern pike were moderately abundant in the 2002 assessment. The gill-net catch rate of 8.7/net was slightly above the lake class average. Sampled fish ranged from 9.4 to 35.5 inches, with a mean length of 19.8 inches. Age and growth analysis indicated consistent natural reproduction, and growth was similar to the statewide average for all ages examined.

As in previous assessments, bluegill remained low in abundance but had a good average size. In 2002, the bluegill trap-net catch was below the lake class normal range at 1.9/net. Trap-net sampled bluegill ranged from 7.5 to 10.1 inches with a mean length of 8.3 inches. Ages 3-7 were identified from scale analysis, with no dominant year class. Growth was above the lake class average for all ages.

The black crappie population has typically had moderate numbers and size structure. In 2002, the trap-net catch for black crappie was 1.8/net, similar to the lake class average of 1.7/net. Fish in the sample ranged from 3.5 to 11.8 inches, with a mean length of 8.8 inches.

Yellow perch abundance has been above the normal range in four of the last five assessments. In 2002, the yellow perch gill-net catch rate was 38.8/net and size structure was moderate. Gill-net sampled perch ranged from 5.0 to 10.8 inches with a mean length of 7.9 inches.

Gill-net catch rates for tullibee have been variable, but have been above the lake class normal range in every assessment. In 2002, the gill-net catch rate was 5.8/net, down from 32.8/net in the 1995 assessment. The high but variable abundance of tullibee in Little Cut Foot Sioux Lake, relative to other class 35 lakes, is likely due to the connection to Cut Foot Sioux and Winnibigoshish Lakes.

Other species sampled in the latest assessment included: black, yellow and brown bullhead, bowfin, hybrid sunfish, pumpkinseed sunfish, rock bass and white sucker.

In order to maintain or improve fish and wildlife populations, water quality and habitat must be protected. People often associate water quality problems with large-scale agricultural, forestry, urban development or industrial practices in the watershed. In reality, the impact of land use decisions on one lake lot may seem relatively small, yet the combined effects of many lakeshore owners "fixing up" their property can result in a significant decline in water quality and habitat. For example, removing shoreline and aquatic vegetation, installing sand beach blankets, fertilizing lawns, mowing to the waters edge, uncontrolled run-off and failing septic systems all contribute excess nutrients and sediment which degrade water quality and habitat. Understanding these cumulative impacts and taking steps to avoid or minimize them will help to insure our quality fisheries can be enjoyed by future generations. Refer to http://www.shorelandmanagement.org/ for more information.
