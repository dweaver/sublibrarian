Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/16/2007)

St. James Lake is a 252-acre lake located within the city limits of St. James in Watonwan County. It has a history of dredging and therefore has some deep holes that maintain a maximum depth of around 16 feet. Historically, St. James Lake had good water clarity, abundant aquatic vegetation, and a respectable largemouth bass/bluegill fishery prior to 1995. However by 1999, St. James Lake had poor water clarity, was devoid of aquatic vegetation, and had a less than respectable fishery for sportfish. This change was likely a result of low dissolved oxygen during winter due to snow cover and an ineffective aeration system. With support from the City of St. James, the lake was reclaimed (killed-off) in the fall of 2001. At the time, there were a few concerned citizens and sportsmen not convinced the lake needed to be reclaimed. During the reclamation, it was soon obvious common carp, black bullheads, and black crappie had dominated the fishery. By the spring of 2002, water clarity and aquatic vegetation returned to foster a fabulous return of the fish community. Since the city campground is now often full, it would be difficult to find a citizen now that would say the project was not a success. A population assessment was conducted during the week of July 16, 2007.

The largemouth bass electrofishing catch rate was 128 bass/hour in 2007, 104 bass/hour in 2005, and 394 bass per hour in 2003. Total lengths of fish ranged from 6.5 to 15.6 inches with an average of 10.6 inches. All year classes of bass were sampled, but 2-year old fish (fish naturally reproduced in 2005) dominated the population. Most of these fish were 9-11 inches long. The largemouth bass condition indicated the fish are likely finding plenty to eat, as they are plump and healthy. St. James is a unique fishery as good bass lakes are not numerous in the Windom area. Unlike many of our other area lakes, St. James now has aquatic vegetation that provides good cover for refuge and natural reproduction. It is this in-lake structure that provides bass anglers areas to pinpoint to put fish in the boat.

The bluegill catch rate in trap nets was nearly twice the expected upper normal range. Total lengths ranged from 3.1 to 8.2 inches with an average of 5.8 inches. Five-year classes of bluegill were sampled with most of the fish being 2 (5 inches), 3 (6.8 inches), and 4 (7.4 inches) years old. Growth rates of bluegill were excellent compared to several other upper midwest state averages.

Despite the fact that walleye are not a primary species of management, good numbers of walleye were sampled. The catch rate for walleye was nearly twice the expected range and they averaged 17.2 inches in total length. Many (69%) of the walleye sampled were from the yearlings (510 pounds) stocked in 2002.

It would not be a surprise to catch some northerns while fishing for bass on St. James. Total lengths ranged from 18.4 to 29.7 inches with a mean of 23.9 inches. The condition of these fish was as expected, as they were plump and healthy.

Yellow perch numbers are as expected. Total lengths range from 5.6 to 11.3 inches with an average of 7.5 inches. The condition index (plumpness) of these fish was lower than expected, but yellow perch and bluegills often compete for similar resources. Given the large bluegill population, the condition of the perch was not surprising.

The black crappie catch rate in trap nets was 10.7, which was within the expected range. Total lengths ranged from 5.5 to 11.7 inches with an average of 6.5 inches. Smaller fish dominated the crappie fishery. Despite this, crappie condition index was good and there will be some good "keepers" in a year or two.

The reclamation of St. James Lake was done because a significant proportion of the fishery biomass was black bullhead and common carp. Although no common carp have been sampled post reclamation, black bullheads were been sampled in 2005 (1) and 2007 (3). The presence of black bullheads was likely a result of an illegal stocking or they entered the lake via an anglers bait bucket. Due to the presence of a sizable predator population (walleye, largemouth bass, and northern pike), the presence of black bullheads is not an indication that they will begin to dominate the fishery as in the past. However, it does raise concern that common carp might find their way into St. James via bait buckets.

No other species were sampled.

Efforts to improve the health of the lake should focus on best management practices within the watershed. Improvements and education in land stewardship will often have secondary benefits to the lake in the way of quality and quantity of habitat. Generally, improvements in the quality and quantity of lake habitat will mean direct improvements of the fishery.
