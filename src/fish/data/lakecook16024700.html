Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 09/04/2007)

The single lake trout caught in this assessment was taken in a deep-set gill net. The four previous assessments used the same number of deep gill net sets (three), and had taken 5 lake trout in 2004, 3 lake trout in 1998, and 0 lake trout in 1996 and 1994. This fish was 13 years old, so was of the 1994 year class. Fish of that year class had been fin clipped and stocked as yearlings in 1995. This fish had no clipped fins, so had been naturally produced. It had grown slowly.

All splake caught were of the 2006 year class and had been stocked as yearlings in spring 2007, which was the first splake stocking since 1979. So these were age 1, in their second year of growth, and had spent one growing season in the lake. Comparisons to other lakes will have to wait until more stocking occurs. Yearling splake are no longer available for use in this lake; plans currently call for stocking yearling brook trout in their place, beginning in 2009.

The combined deep and shallow catch of rainbow trout (0.50 fish/set) was similar to catches observed in 2004 and 1998, but appeared to have been lower than catches in 1994 and 1996. The 2007 catch was below the normal range for catches of this species in fall assessments in area stream trout lakes. However, most stream trout lakes in the area have fewer fish species than does Birch Lake, and are stocked more frequently, so rainbow trout could be expected to be less abundant in this lake. The average weight of a rainbow trout in the combined deep and shallow catch (2.14 lb) compared favorably with values in previous population assessments: 2.66 lb in 2004, 1.32 lb in 1998, 0.87 lb in 1996, and 1.23 lb in 1994. The three rainbow trout caught in this assessment were age 2, and had been stocked as yearlings in 2006. Growth had been fast. These fish were over three inches longer than the average for their age in this area.

The source of the single walleye taken in this assessment is unknown. The DNR has not stocked this species since the lake was reclaimed in 1969. This is the first assessment since then to collect walleye. The 3-year-old fish had grown at a rate somewhat slower than average for the lake class in this area.

The smallmouth bass catch in shallow-set gill nets matched the catch in the 2004 assessment, which was the first assessment to have collected the species. The catch exceeded the 75th percentile (1.50 fish/set) for shallow sets in the area. The average weight for bass caught in the 2007 assessment was double that of the 2004 assessment, but was still fairly low compared to average lengths of fish taken in similar lakes in the area. Five age classes were represented in the combined catch from shallow-set gill nets and 0.25-inch-mesh trap nets: ages 1, 2, 3, 4, and 6. Growth of the two age-2 fish had been fast, and the remaining fish had grown faster than average for the lake class and area.

White sucker abundance was less than usual for this lake, but was within the normal range for population assessments in this area when this type of sampling has been used. The average weight for white sucker was high for this lake, and in comparison to average weights for suckers taken in similar lakes in the area.

No minnows were caught by 0.25-in-mesh trap nets in this assessment. This gear caught no minnows in the 2004 assessment, but had taken many when used in previous assessments. The decline in minnow numbers corresponds with the appearance of smallmouth bass in the lake. ?
