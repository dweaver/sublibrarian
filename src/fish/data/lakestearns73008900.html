Status of the Fishery (as of 08/05/2003)

The Sauk River Chain of Lakes is located in south central Minnesota, near the city of Richmond. Because of the construction of a dam on the Sauk River at Cold Spring, boaters can access nearly 3,000 acres by water. The Sauk River drains a large portion of central Minnesota before flowing through the chain of lakes. Combined with drainage from the Long Lake sub-watershed to the south, the total watershed of the chain is nearly 600,000 acres. Although non-point sources (agricultural run-off) continue to be a concern, some major contributors or nutrients (such as municipal wastewater discharge) were greatly reduced in the 1980's and 1990's. Efforts are being made to address existing sources, but water quality in most of the lakes remains in the hypereutrophic range (very nutrient-rich).

The entire Sauk River Chain was re-surveyed in 2003. Netting results from the river portion of the lake chain (lakes downstream of Cedar Island Lake - Zumwalde, Great Northern, Krays, Knaus, Schneider, and Bolfing) were similar, and were combined for this summary.

The lakes are managed primarily for northern pike and largemouth bass, although walleye, black crappie, bluegill, and channel catfish are also popular with anglers. The catch rate of northern pike in 2003 was within the range expected for lakes similar to the Sauk River Chain, and similar to the 1995 survey. Since 1980, catch rates of northern pike in these lakes have been relatively stable. Mean weight of fish sampled in 2003 (3.1 pounds) was similar to 1995.

The walleye population is currently supported by stocking of fry by the lake association. Nearby Cedar Island and Horseshoe Lakes are also stocked with fry by the DNR, and some migration among lakes is likely. Fry stocking in 2001 on Horseshoe and Cedar Island Lakes appeared very successful (based on fall electrofishing), and the catch throughout the Sauk River Chain was dominated by this year class. Catch rates of walleye in the river portion of the chain were similar to those reported in Horseshoe and Cedar Island. As with these other Sauk River lakes, catch rates were down from 1995. Mean weight of walleye sampled in 2003 was 1.8 pounds.

Yellow perch catch rates declined sharply from the 1995 survey to the 2003 survey, in all of the lakes in the Sauk River Chain. Mean weight (0.14 pounds) was similar to 1995. Prior to the 2003 survey, catch rates of yellow perch were significantly higher on these lakes.

Largemouth bass were assessed by electrofishing. Portions of Bolfing, Knaus, Krays and Great Northern were sampled. Catch rates were similar to other lakes in this part of the state. The average length of bass was 12 inches, and the largest was 18 inches. A few smallmouth bass were sampled during summer in these lakes, but not by electrofishing.

Channel catfish were introduced into the chain in the 1980's, and have become well established. In the 1985 and 1990 surveys, catfish were sampled at relatively low rates in the river portion of the chain. The population appeared to expand in the early 1990's. Schneider Lake has been an exception to this trend, as the catch rate has remained low. Schneider Lake has clearer water, more submerged plant growth, less river-like, and is probably less favorable for channel catfish. Mean weight of catfish sampled in the river portion of the chain was 1.8 pounds, lower than Horseshoe (2.7 pounds) or Cedar Island (2.1 pounds). Most of the small catfish (less than 12 inches) sampled in 2003 were found in these smaller, more riverine lakes.

Catch rates of black crappie were lower than in 1995 in all of the Sauk River chain of lakes, although still favorable. Mean weight increased to 0.41 pounds. Both catch rate and average size was higher in these lakes than in Horseshoe or Cedar Island. As with channel catfish, catch rates of crappie have generally been lower in Schneider than the other lakes. Catch rates of bluegill in 2003 were similar to 1995, while mean weight increased (from 0.21 to 0.34 pounds), and is now very favorable.

Other species sampled during the 2003 survey included bullhead, redhorse, white sucker, carp, and several minnow species.
