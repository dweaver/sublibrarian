Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/06/2005)

Dry Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 4, which consists of 47 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are small, deep, bowl-shaped, and have clear and soft (unmineralized) water. Dry Lake was thermally stratified on 6/6/2005 with a surface temperature of 66 F and a bottom temperature of 41 F. Good oxygen for trout (more than 5 ppm) was retained to a depth of 22 ft, where the temperature was 45 F. Surface temperatures in midsummer are warmer and 5 ppm oxygen is retained to a depth of about 20 ft, where the temperature is in the mid 50's F.

The inlet to Dry Lake is from High Lake. There is an old rock crib filter barrier, built to limit fish movement, at the outlet of High Lake. Brook trout were observed spawning in the inlet from High Lake in the mid 1980's. The outlet, to Bass Lake, has a waterfall that prevents upstream fish movement from Bass Lake. There is also a filter barrier in the outlet to Bass Lake to limit fish movement out of Dry Lake. Dry Lake is connected to adjacent Little Dry Lake by a channel four feet deep, through which fish can move. Recent trout stocking in Dry Lake consisted of brown trout yearlings in 1989, 1990, 1992, and every third year beginning in 1994. Splake were stocked in 1993 and two of every three years beginning in 1995.

This trout population assessment was done early in the summer while surface temperatures were still cool, and like thirteen previous trout assessments done on Dry Lake since 1973, consisted of low numbers of gillnets.

The trout catch in 2005 consisted of four brown trout and three splake. The brown trout consisted of three 13-14" fish that had probably been stocked as yearlings in 2003, and one 18" fish that may have been stocked as a yearling in 2000. If true, this represents fairly slow growth for brown trout. Two of the splake were 16" and one was 26.5". The larger splake was alive and was not weighed before it was released. The stomachs of three brown trout and two splake that were examined were empty.

The trout catch by weight of 7.7 lb/gillnet in 2005 was higher than the median trout catch of 1.6 lb/gillnet in all trout assessments on this lake and was higher than the median catch of 3.9 lb/gillnet in recent assessments of 25 stream trout lakes (excluding mine pit lakes) in the Tower Fisheries Management Area.

Also captured in the 2005 assessment were high numbers of white sucker (16.0/gillnet) and cisco (13.5/gillnet). The sucker averaged 10" and the largest was 17", while the cisco averaged 12" and the largest was 13".
