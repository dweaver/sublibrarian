Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/08/2005)

Round Lake, near the town of Round Lake in Jackson County, is a 1,024-acre lake with a maximum depth of 9 feet. Round Lake has a shoreline development index (SDI) of 1.2 (SDI of 1.0 is a perfect circle), which means Round Lake is well-named. Establishing aquatic vegetation has been difficult given the lakes morphology, fish community, and landscape use. Field tiling is a common practice on the landscape in this agricultural area, which creates a direct line for nutrient input. Round Lake has an aeration system in operation during the winter months and has not experienced a partial winterkill since 1979, according to the DNR records. Round Lake is managed primarily for walleye and secondarily for yellow perch, black crappie, and channel catfish. A population assessment was conducted during the week of August 8 of 2005. Two gill nets and 10 trap nets were used to assess the fish community composition.

At the time of the survey, there was an unprecedented walleye population in Round Lake. An average statewide walleye catch rate during a lake survey would be around 15 walleye/gill net set. Round Lake had a catch rate of nearly 153 walleye/gill net set. It is no doubt the highest catch rate the Windom Area Lakes have experienced and quite possibly the highest catch rate statewide for a management lake. It should be noted that this phenomenal catch rate was a result of walleye fry and not fingerling stockings. Most of the fish are in the 10-13 inch range with another group of fish in the 14-20 inch range. The average fish length was just over 12 inches. The average angler has a great chance to catch some walleye but reasonable harvest should be considered in order to continue this great fishery.

The channel catfish catch rate was not as high as we have seen in other years. Despite the lower catch rate, the average sized catfish was nearly 18 inches. According to our stocking records, channel catfish have not been stocked since 1981. However, it is possible that channel catfish could be using the cavities in the spawning reef for reproduction. During the warm summer months, this could be a destination for all of those catfish anglers with a good chance at catching a few.

Yellow perch numbers have slightly rebounded since the last survey. The catch rate for perch was nearly 10/set, which was average for this type of lake. The average length was nearly 10 inches which is a "keeper" for most anglers targeting perch. Most of the fish are in the 9-11 inch range.

Another panfish that could provide some table fare and at the same time provide some good action is black crappie. The catch rate for black crappie matched a near record high in Round Lake. Most of the crappie are in the 5-6 inch and 9-10 inch ranges. The average length crappie was almost 8 inches while the largest fish measured was almost 14 inches. When things slow up walleye fishing, it is possible to have some good action catching panfish, with an occasional "keeper". The Round Lake panfish populations are likely benefiting with a large predator base, that eats the smaller panfish, which allows the escapees to grow faster and reach larger lengths.

Despite the fact that northern pike are not managed for in Round Lake, there are some real "dandies" swimming around. Although the catch rate was not real high, the average sized northern was almost 32 inches.

Black bullhead numbers in both gill nets and trap nets were considerably lower than average for the Windom Area. The walleye population may be keeping the bullhead numbers down. Despite the few bullheads in Round Lake, there are several fisheries not far from Round Lake that would provide some great fishing for bullheads. If bullheads are your target, consider fishing Indian, Okabena, Loon, Pearl, Ocheda, Iowa, Illinois, and Bella Lakes.

Other species captured in the 2005 survey either by trap net and gill net were bigmouth buffalo, common carp, freshwater drum, white sucker, and white bass.

To promote and maintain healthy fish populations, pollution and other inputs need to be controlled. Fish habitats are directly affected by water quality. Nutrient, sediment and other waste inputs can drastically alter the biological, chemical, and physical components of a lake. It is paramount to maintain if not improve the current water quality of Round Lake through watershed management to preserve this fishery for future generations to enjoy.
