Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/25/2003)

Birch Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 7, which consists of 41 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are large, have irregular shoreline shapes, and have soft (unmineralized) water, Birch Lake is the impoundment of old river beds. Compared to most Class 7 lakes, Birch Lake is larger, shallower, has steeper shoreline dropoffs, and has an extremely irregular shoreline shape.

Birch Lake was very slightly thermally stratified on 08/25/2003 with a surface temperature of 75 F and a bottom temperature of 72 F. Dissolved oxygen was at saturation at the surface (7.5 ppm), and remained near saturation (5.9 ppm) at the bottom. The major inlet (South Kawishiwi River) and the outlet (Birch River, also known as South Kawishiwi River) are both in the northeast corner of the lake. There is a dam at the outlet and dam operation results in winter drawdown of about 4 ft. Shoal water substrates on Birch Lake are mostly boulder and ledgerock, with lesser amounts of sand. Aquatic vegetation grows to a depth of 5 ft and is sparse, with small clusters in protected bays; waterlilies, various pondweeds, and floatingleaf burreed are the most common plants. Birch Lake ranks as mesotrophic-to-eutrophic according to Carlson's Trophic State Index.

Birch Lake has been stocked with walleye fry every second or third year since 1982. There is a special regulation for northern pike which began on May 10, 2003: a 24-36" protected slot with one over 36" allowed in possession. The 2003 fish population assessment consisted of 15 standard gillnet sets to evaluate northern pike and walleye populations. Trapnets were not set because they were not necessary for this evaluation. Twelve previous gillnettings on this lake dating back to 1954 consisted of 5-15 gillnets set in mid-to-late August.

Fish populations in 2003, as in previous investigations, were dominated by walleye, followed by northern pike, cisco, sucker, and perch. The total catch of fish (all species combined) in the gillnets in 2003 of 31 fish/net (29 lb/net) was at the median for this lake class and was near the median for all investigations on this lake.

Walleye numbers in 2003 (12.5/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 8.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye were evenly spread throughout the lake. Walleye sizes in 2003 averaged 12.4" (0.9 lb), which was near the median for this lake class and was similar to the average length of 12.9" in all investigations on this lake. The largest walleye captured in 2003 was 29.1" (6.6 lb). The number of walleye of quality size (16" or larger) in 2003 of 1.7/gillnet was higher than the median catch of 1.4/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. There does not appear to be any trend in the number of quality size walleye present in Birch Lake over time, although no data is available for the initial survey in 1954 because the lengths of fish in the gillnets and trapnets were combined. Walleye scales were collected for age and growth analysis in 2003 and were considered to be moderately difficult to read, but it appeared that eleven different year classes of walleye were captured in the gillnets. Many of the walleye were young: age two (37 percent) and age one (24 percent). Walleye growth was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile) by area standards.

Northern pike numbers in 2003 (1.9/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were slightly lower than the median catch of 2.3/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2003 averaged 21.9" (2.3 lb), which was in the second quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 22.4" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike captured in 2003 was 26.7". The number of northern pike in the protected slot range (24-36") in 2003 of 0.4/gillnet was lower than the median catch of 0.7/gillnet in all investigations on this lake (no data is available for the initial survey in 1954 because the lengths of fish in the gillnets and trapnets was combined). Catches of 24-36" pike were consistently lower than the median in the three most recent investigations on this lake, beginning in 1997. Pike scales were collected for age and growth analysis in 2003 and were considered to be moderately difficult to read, but it appeared that only five year classes of pike were captured. Most pike (48 percent) were age two. Pike growth was faster than normal (in the third or fourth quartile, depending on the age) by area standards.

Cisco numbers in 2003 (7.1/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were near the median catch of 7.2/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Cisco sizes in 2003 averaged 10.4" (0.6 lb), which was in the fourth quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 9.8" in all investigations on this lake. The largest cisco in 2003 was 17.6".

White sucker numbers in 2003 (4.9/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were at the median catch for all investigations on this lake. Sucker sizes in 2003 averaged 15.7" (1.7 lb), which was at the median for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 14.0" in all investigations on this lake.

Yellow perch numbers in 2003 (3.3/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 4.7/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Perch sizes in 2003 averaged 7.7" (0.2 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 7.3" in all investigations on this lake. The largest perch captured in 2003 was 12.2". Perch scales were difficult to read, but six year classes appeared to be present and growth appeared to be somewhat faster than normal (in the third quartile) by area standards.

Smallmouth bass were first observed in Birch Lake in the 1997 investigation. While smallmouth numbers have been low, with a median catch of 0.1/gillnet, their numbers in the gillnet catch probably underestimate their true abundance because smallmouth are known to be "net shy". Smallmouth sizes increased from an average of 5.3" in 1997, to 13.5" in 2000, and to 15.7" in 2003. The largest smallmouth captured in 2003 was 15.9". Smallmouth scales were easy to read, and growth was faster than normal (in the fourth quartile) by area standards.

Many of the game fish examined in 2003 were infected with neascus. Some of the yellow perch also had yellow grub, and some of the cisco had triaenophorus larvae in their muscle tissue. Neascus (black spot), yellow grub, and triaenophorus are all common parasites that are native to the area. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.

Five rusty crayfish were captured in Gillnet 1, at the northeast tip of Birch Lake in 2003. This is the first appearance of this species in this lake, and if experience is a guide, we can expect their numbers to increase over time with negative consequences for native crayfish and aquatic plants.
