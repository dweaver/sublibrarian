Status of the Fishery (as of 08/07/2000)

Beatrice Lake is a small, heavily developed lake located four miles northwest of Side Lake. Due to the small size of the lake, it may be vulnerable to over-harvest. The lake has a concrete public access and state campground on the north side of the lake. The lake management plan indicates black crappie as the primary species of management with bluegill and northern pike as secondary species.

Black crappie are difficult to sample during the summer because they often suspend in mid-lake areas where our nets are not set. Despite these sampling challenges, the 2000 catch rates appeared to be average when compared to previous assessments and lakes that are similar to Beatrice. Black crappie from the trap nets ranged from 3.9 to 9.8 inches and had an average length of 4.9 inches. Nearly 87% of the fish sampled were one year old. Growth was above statewide averages for all year classes.

Bluegill numbers were similar to the last two assessments but low when compared to other similar lakes. The population has never been real abundant which is likely a result of the lakes habitat and low productivity. Bluegill ranged from 3.4 to 9.6 inches with an average length of 5.6 inches. After aging the scales, it was determined that fish from two to eight years old were present. Bluegill growth in Beatrice tended to be better than lakes with similar characteristics.

Northern pike numbers have always been fairly low. Generally it's better to have low to moderate numbers of fish with better size distribution than to have a high population with only small fish. The 2000 net catches were about average for Beatrice Lake. Northern pike ranged from 15.4 to 31.4 inches and averaged 23.2 inches. Six year classes were identified by aging fish scales ranging from one to six years old. Northern pike averaged 24.6 inches in four years of growth which is above the statewide average of 20.5 inches.

Since 1969, walleye numbers have ranged from 0.0 to 4.0 fish/net. In 2000, the catch was 1.2 fish/net which is average for lakes similar to Beatrice. Walleye have not been stocked since 1991. Natural reproduction accounted for all the fish sampled with fish from one to five years old aged. The fish ranged from 11.7 to 19.5 inches and had an average length of 16.3 inches. Walleye averaged 14.9 inches after three years of growth which was above the statewide average of 12.3 inches. In a lake like Beatrice, walleye will never be a dominant species due its habitat and productivity characteristics.

Yellow perch catch rates declined from a high of 46.0 fish/net in 1969 to a low of 1.0 fish/net in 1986 and have since increased to 4.5 fish/net in 2000. The 2000 catch rates were above average when compared to other lake similar to Beatrice. Fish ranged 5.9 to 8.3 inches and averaged 6.6 inches. Two to four year old fish were sampled in 2000. Growth was similar to statewide averages for all ages. The yellow perch population is probably more important to the fish community as a food item than to anglers for a recreational species.

Largemouth bass have never been sampled with spring electrofishing which is the most effective tool for catching bass. However, seven fish were sampled in the nets that ranged from 5.0 to 16.3 inches with an average length of 8.2 inches. Three year classes were sampled with three age-1, two age-2, and one age-4 fish. Low sample size precludes any meaningful discussion on growth.

To promote and maintain healthy fish populations, pollution needs to be controlled. Fish habitat is directly affected by water quality. Nutrients, sediments and other waste can drastically alter the chemical and physical characteristics of a lake. People often associate water quality problems in a lake with agricultural, forestry or industrial practices within the watershed. Although these can be areas of concern, lakeshore property owners may also contribute to water quality problems. Some examples include putting in sand beaches, removing shoreline trees and vegetation, fertilizing lawns, and mowing their lawns down to the lakeshore. Proper land use practices by everyone will help insure that this fishery can be enjoyed by future generations.
