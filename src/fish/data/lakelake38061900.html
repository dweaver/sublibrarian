Status of the Fishery (as of 06/16/2005)

Newfound Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 3, which consists of 72 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are small, deep, have irregular shoreline shapes, and have very clear and soft (unmineralized) water. Newfound Lake is larger and shallower than most of the lakes in this lake class. Newfound Lake is a mesotrophic lake.

Newfound Lake was thermally stratified on 08/22/2005 with a surface temperature of 66 F and a bottom temperature of 50 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 24 ft, where the temperature was 65 F. The major inlet to Newfound Lake is from Moose Lake and the outlet is to Sucker Lake; both are navigable and have no barriers to fish movement. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline of Newfound Lake are mostly rubble, gravel, boulder, and ledge rock. Aquatic plants are sparse and grow to a depth of 10 ft.

Newfound Lake is entirely within the Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness (BWCAW) and is on the most heavily traveled route in the BWCAW. There are 14 campsites maintained by the United States Forest Service (USFS). Access is by boat from Moose Lake or Sucker Lake, and by portage to smaller nearby lakes. Motorboat use is restricted to 25 horsepower maximum, permits from the USFS are required and numbers are limited by quota. Snowmobiles are not allowed. Overnight camping permits for both motor and no-motor use are also required and are limited by quota. Day use paddle-only permits are required and are not limited by quota.

Fish sampling in the 2005 fish population assessment was done with 9 gillnets and 12 trapnets. Ten previous fisheries investigations, dating back to 1972, used 5-9 gillnets. Trapnets (6-12 nets) were used in three of these previous investigations.

Fish populations in 2005, as in previous investigations, were dominated by northern pike, walleye, cisco, and white sucker. In contrast to adjacent Moose Lake, bluegill populations in Newfound Lake have not increased in recent investigations. Non-native crayfish populations in Newfound Lake are fairly high.

Northern pike numbers in 2005 (2.2/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were identical to the median catch of 2.2/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2005 averaged 23.2" (3.7 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 23.5" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike caught in 2005 was 34.2". Pike scales were difficult to read, but it appeared that pike growth was somewhat slower than normal by area standards (in the first or second quartile, depending on the age).

Walleye numbers in 2005 (9.6/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 6.2/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes in 2005 averaged 18.8" (2.8 lb), which was in the fourth quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 15.9" in all investigations on this lake. The largest walleye caught in 2005 was 29.4". Many of the walleye were age four, from the 2001 year-class. Walleye growth was faster than normal (in the fourth quartile) by area standards.

Sucker numbers in 2005 (3.2/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median catch of 5.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake.

Cisco numbers in 2005 (50.6/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median catch of 22.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Cisco sizes in 2005 averaged 9.1"; the largest was 14.9".

Smallmouth numbers in the gillnets in 2005 (0.7/gillnet) were at the median for this lake class, while smallmouth numbers in the trapnets (5.6/trapnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class. Smallmouth sizes in 2005 averaged 14.2" in the gillnets and 8.9" in the trapnets. The largest smallmouth was 19.2". Smallmouth and largemouth bass numbers are often underrepresented by gillnet and trapnet catches, as they are "net shy" and difficult to catch in standard sampling nets.

Bluegill numbers have increased in recent years in adjacent Moose Lake and were quite abundant (29.2/trapnet) in 2005. In contrast, bluegill numbers in Newfound Lake have been low, with a median catch of 1.0/trapnet over the long term.

Neascus was found on many of the game fish examined in 2005. Bass tapeworm was found in some of the smallmouth bass, triaenophorus was found in many of the cisco, and yellow grub was found on some of the yellow perch. All of these parasites are native to the area and are common. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.

Non-native crayfish were first observed in Newfound Lake in the 1989 investigation, when they were caught entangled in gillnets. Their numbers in 2005 (52 Orconectes rusticus/gillnet and 24 O. propinquus/gillnet) were fairly high and typical of catches in recent investigations on Newfound Lake. Abundant populations of O. rusticus (rusty crayfish) have been linked to the loss of aquatic vegetation and the decline of bluegill populations in some lakes. These non-native crayfish are also present in Moose, Sucker, Birch, and Basswood Lakes.
