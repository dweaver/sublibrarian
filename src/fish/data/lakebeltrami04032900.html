* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/25/2001)

Balm Lake is a 512-acre lake with a maximum depth of 33 feet. The lake is located 15 miles north of Solway in western Beltrami County. There is a public access on the north end of the lake off of state aid hwy. 32. The Minnesota Department of Natural Resources has classified Minnesota's lakes into 43 different lake classes based on physical and chemical characteristics. Balm Lake is in lake class 25. Typically lakes in this class are deep and clear with hard water. Other area lakes in the same lake class include Beltrami, Big Buzzle, Gilstad, Big Moose, Movil, and Pimushe Lakes. The lake is managed for bluegill, walleye and largemouth bass.

Walleye abundance in the 2001 assessment was above what you would typically find in a class 25 lake but similar to what was found in past assessments. Walleye sampled were mostly older fish with 95% aged at five years or older. This tendency for older fish has been common in earlier assessments as well. Although this might indicate poor reproduction, ten different year classes were sampled. Walleye from both stocked and nonstocked years were sampled indicating that natural reproduction and stocking are both contributing to the fishery. As expected with a population dominated by older fish, there are some quality sized fish available to anglers. Fish ranged in length from 15.2 to 28.0 inches with an average length and weight of 21.0 inches and 3.3 pounds.

Northern pike abundance was normal for this lake type, but size is better than average. Pike ranged in length from 20 to 37 inches with an average length and weight of 27 inches and 4.6 pounds.

Yellow perch abundance is well above what you would normally find in a class 25 lake. Their presence and abundance as a forage species probably contributes to the quality size structure of the walleye and northern pike populations. In addition to providing an excellent forage supply for the larger predators, perch in Balm Lake grow large enough to be of interest to anglers. Fish ranged in length from 5 to 11 inches and had an average length of 7 inches. Yellow perch up until nine years old were averaging an inch a year in growth. As perch became older, they averaged less than one inch a year. The oldest yellow perch aged was 14 years and was 11.3 inches long.

White sucker, also present in Balm Lake, is another fish that is used as food for predator fish. It is important for the larger predator species to have access to larger prey species to maintain good growth rates.

Anglers can expect to have much success angling for bluegill. Abundance is well above averages for this lake class. The average size is small but fish over seven inches do exist. Pumpkinseed sunfish are also very abundant and their size is very similar to the bluegill. If you are not particular about size, it should be relatively easy to catch a meal from this lake.

Black crappie are also abundant and can furnish anglers recreational opportunity. Crappie ranged in length from 5 to 10.5 inches. Most crappie are small with most fish between 7 to 8 inches. These fish were four years old when sampled and should provide some good fishing over the next few years.

Riparian development in the form of new lake homes continues to occur on Balm Lake. Shoreline development can negatively affect water quality and can impact aquatic habitat necessary for fish at various life stages. Undeveloped shoreline is some of the most sensitive and important shoreline for maintaining water quality and fish habitat. Shorelines and shallow water areas with their aquatic plants, help protect the lake from erosion and absorb nutrients and pollutants as well as provide excellent places for wildlife and fish to feed, hide and raise their young. Protection of the larger watershed that drains into Balm Lake is also needed to maintain water quality. Based on the increasing value of lakeshore in this area, there will be a demand to develop the remaining lakeshore on Balm Lake since most of the lakeshore is privately owned. While future shoreline development may be inevitable, there are ways to minimize disturbances to sensitive aquatic habitats. More specific information on protecting or restoring shorelines and watersheds is available through DNR Fisheries offices.
