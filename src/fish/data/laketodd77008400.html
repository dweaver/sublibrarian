Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/06/2007)

Big Birch Lake consists of two connected basins that cover 2,108 acres straddling the Todd/Stearns County line. The lake has a maximum depth of 81 feet in the lower basin and 65 feet in the upper basin. A variety of fishing hot spots can be found around the lake such as islands, bays, sunken islands, sunken bars, vegetation beds, and rock piles which provide a multitude of angling experiences. Water clarity was moderate to poor for the time of year which may have been the results of lower water levels and a hot summer. Aquatic vegetation was found to grow to a depth of 19 feet with the most common submergent species being muskgrass, coontail, bushy pondweed, and sago pondweed. Marl and sand were the most common shallow water substrates around the lake. A state forest campground with a boat access and fishing pier is located on the southwest side of the lake. The Big Birch Lake Association has taken a very active role in trying to improve water qualtiy in the lake. They have encouraged local agricultural producers and lakeshore property owners, both with money and information, to use best management practices. The lake supports a variety of gamefish that can provide an opportunity to catch quality size fish. The primary management focus is on walleye and northern pike. Walleye fry or fingerlings are stocked every year to sustain the fishery and some natural reproduction has been documented as well. Abundance showed a slight improvement from the last survey and numbers continue to be within the normal range for similar type lakes. A wide range of sizes, between 8 to 28 inches, were seen in the summer survey and walleye up to 30 inches were documented in a spring trapnet assessment. Fishing reports have been good for much of the year for walleye. Northern pike abundance showed a significant decrease since the last survey in 2003 but continues to be above management goals for the lake. Average size of the pike was about 2 pounds with a fair number of fish over 24 inches. An experimental regulation implemented in 1996 that required the release of all northern pike over 24 inches was modified in 2006 to a protected slot between 24 and 36 inches and a possession limit of 3 with one over 36 inches allowed. While few pike over 36 inches have been observed during sampling efforts, the survey results and angler reports suggest an increase in the number of medium size fish. It is hoped that having more large northern pike in the population wil help to keep total pike numbers down and growth good. This typically leads to a healthier fish community with a good balance of predators and prey. Another desired outcome of the regulation is to provide an opportunity to anglers to catch large pike in excess of 10 pounds; a feat becoming increasingly difficult in Minnesota. Compliance with the regulation is important to achieve improvements in both northern pike and walleye populations. Largemouth and smallmouth bass are both abundant in Big Birch Lake. Trophy-size bass up to 20 inches have been reported by anglers, but average size of both species in the survey was between 11 and 14 inches. Panfish species in the lake include black crappies, bluegills, pumpkinseeds, hybrid sunfish, and rock bass. Gillnets were the only sampling method to be used in this survey since gamefish were the primary targets, but trapnets used this spring to evaluate the northern pike population caught good numbers of panfish. While some quality size crappies up to 13 inches and bluegills up to 9 inches were observed in the gillnet survey, the spring trapnets sampled crappies up to 15 inches and bluegills up to 10 inches in length. Anglers have also reported catching nice size crappies and bluegills. Abundance of three prey species, white sucker, yellow perch, and tullibee (cisco), which gamefish feed on, were low with the perch and tullibee being at their lowest levels ever. Perch numbers have fluctuated from survey to survey which may be the result of high pike numbers and the walleye stocking. High numbers of perch in a range of sizes are desirable to provide a prey base for all predator species to maintain good growth rates. Tullibee and sucker numbers have shown declines since 1995, however, growth rates of predators are still decent. Other fish species netted during the survey included bowfin (dogfish), brown bullheads, common carp, shorthead redhorse, and yellow bullheads. Yellow bullheads were the more numerous of two bulhead species which may indicate good water quality as they are more abundant in lakes with clear water. Carp do not appear to be at levels detrimental to the lake habitats.?
