Status of the Fishery (as of 06/20/2005)

Moose Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 7, which consists of 41 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are very large, have very irregular shoreline shapes, and have soft (unmineralized) water. Moose Lake is deeper and has clearer water than most of the lakes in this lake class. Moose Lake is a mesotrophic lake.

Moose Lake was thermally stratified on 08/22/2005 with a surface temperature of 66 F and a bottom temperature of 48 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 50 ft, where the temperature was 48 F. Moose Lake has six small inlets, five of which originate in small nearby lakes. The outlet to Newfound Lake is 5 ft deep and easily navigable. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline of Moose Lake are mostly boulder, rubble, gravel, and ledgerock. Aquatic plants are sparse and grow to a depth of 12 ft.

There is a public access with a concrete boat ramp and a canoe landing. There are about 60 homes and cottages, and about 10 resorts, canoe outfitters, and camps, with numerous motel units, bunkhouses, and campsites. All of the private development, which comprises about 30 percent of the shoreline, is on the southwestern shore of the lake. The northeastern half of Moose Lake is in the Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness (BWCAW), which is administered by the US Forest Service. Moose Lake is the largest single entry point to the BWCAW and has five water-access campsites. There are winter and summer use restrictions on the BWCAW portion of Moose Lake: quotas and permits for motorboats and overnight campers, day use permits for day visitors, 25 horsepower maximum motor size for motorboats, and a ban on snowmobile use in the winter.

Fish sampling in the 2005 fish population assessment consisted of 12 gillnet sets and 12 trapnet sets. Eleven previous fisheries investigations dating back to 1972 each used 6-12 gillnets. Four of these previous investigations used 5-12 trapnets. Shoreline seining to evaluate natural reproduction was done in the 2000 investigation. Shoreline seining and small mesh trapnetting was done in 1967-68 and in 1971-72 in an effort to evaluate walleye fry stocking.

Fish populations in 2005, as in most previous investigations, were dominated by walleye, northern pike, white sucker, and cisco. Also captured in 2005 were high numbers of bluegill sunfish.

Walleye numbers in 2005 (4.8/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median walleye catch of 4.1/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes in 2005 averaged 16.4" (2.0 lb), which was in the fourth quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 15.0" in all investigations on this lake. The largest walleye was 30.6". Most walleye captured in 2005 were ages 2-4. Walleye growth was somewhat faster than normal (in the third quartile) by area standards.

Northern pike numbers in 2005 (7.3/gillnet) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median pike catch of 4.4/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2005 averaged 21.6" (2.4 lb), which was in the second quartile for this lake class and was smaller than the average size of 23.3" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike was 38.5". Most pike captured in 2005 were ages 3-5. Pike growth was somewhat slower than normal by area standards (in the second quartile).

Cisco and sucker numbers in 2005 were lower than normal for this lake class and lower than the long term medians for this lake. Cisco sizes in 2005 ranged from 6" to 14".

Bluegill were first captured in shoreline seines in 1968-72, but were not captured in standard trapnets in the first fisheries lake survey in 1972. Their numbers in subsequent investigations increased from 1.2/trapnet in 1993 to 4.4/trapnet in 1995, 6.8/trapnet in 2000, and to 29.2/trapnet in 2005. The trapnetting dates were standardized in 1995 to mid-June, so the recent increase in bluegill numbers appears to be real and not due to changes in sampling dates. Increases in bluegill numbers have been observed in a number of area lakes in recent years, perhaps due to favorable conditions caused by longer and warmer summers. Bluegill sizes in 2005 averaged 6.1"; the largest (caught in a gillnet) was 8.4". Bluegill recruitment was erratic, with most bluegill in the 2005 trapnet catch being age four or seven. Bluegill growth was somewhat slower than normal by area standards (in the second quartile).

Non-native crayfish were first observed in Moose Lake in the 1991 investigation, when they were caught entangled in gillnets. Typical of recent crayfish catches in Moose Lake, the 2005 catch consisted of a native species (Orconectes virilis - 0.2/gillnet), and two non-native species: O. propinquus (5.8/gillnet) and O. rusticus (8.5/gillnet). These numbers are relatively low; abundant populations of rusty crayfish (more than 100/gillnet) have decimated aquatic vegetation in some area lakes and have been implicated in the decline of sunfish populations. These non-native species are also found in Newfound, Sucker, Birch, and Basswood lakes.

Neascus (black spot) was found on many of the game fish examined in 2005. Bass tapeworm was found in most of the smallmouth bass examined internally. Yellow grub was found on one of the yellow perch. All of these parasites are native to the area and are common. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.
