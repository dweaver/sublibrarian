Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/20/2000)

Bella Lake is a reservoir located in southern Nobles County. During an average year, the lake has a surface area of 182 acres and a maximum depth of 14 feet; however, the lake serves as an aquifer replenishment reservoir for municipal water supplies and fluctuates substantially. In 1996, a management plan was developed and included an objective to establish a self-sustaining northern pike population with secondary populations of yellow perch and black crappie. Although the primary management species in Bella Lake has been northern pike, no pike were captured in the gill nets and only one 22.5 inch individual was captured in the trap nets. In 1994, pike were captured at a rate of 1 fish/gill net and 0.56 fish/trap net. Northern pike spawning habitat can be good in the channel and marshes that connect Bella Lake with Ocheda Lake; however, low water levels have created barriers to these habitats.

Although walleye have never been stocked into Bella Lake, it is believed that they likely migrated from Ocheda Lake during high water periods. Walleye were captured at a rate of 7.0 fish/gill net and 1.67 fish/trap net. Both of these catch rates were higher than average for similar lakes in southwestern Minnesota. In 1994, walleye were caught at a rate of 1 fish/gill net and none were captured in the trap nets. Captured walleye ranged from 10.5 to 23.0 inches. Age and growth analyses indicated that Bella Lake walleye were growing rather slowly, with age 2, 4 and 6 fish reaching average total lengths of 8.50, 15.19, and 19.74 inches, respectively. These growth rates are less than typical for lakes in southwestern Minnesota, where age 2, 4, and 6 walleyes usually attain lengths of 11-12, 16-17, and 20-21 inches, respectively. The slow growth rates, combined with the presence of age-7 walleye and a high proportion of fish between 15 and 20 inches, is typical of a population experiencing limited exploitation. The condition of walleye in Bella Lake was slightly less than desired, meaning the average walleye was not as plump as preferred. No young-of-year walleye were captured in the natural reproduction seining; however, the consistent age-structure and frequency of disconnection with other walleye waters, indicates that natural reproduction has been occurring.

Secondary management species in Bella Lake are yellow perch and black crappie. Yellow perch were caught in gill nets at a rate of 1.0 fish/net, a value that is less than most similar lakes. However, in the trap nets, perch catch rates were 3.33 fish/net, which is similar to slightly above similar lakes. The yellow perch size structure was good, with a number of fish exceeding 8 inches; however, growth and condition were quite poor. The age and growth assessment revealed that age 2 and 3 perch in Bella Lake averaged 5.92 and 7.38 inches. Several other area lakes maintain perch populations that can reach 7 inches by age 2 and surpass 9 inches by age 3. These poor growth rates are likely attributable to the poor condition of yellow perch in Bella Lake. All captured yellow perch were extremely thin.

Black crappie adults were stocked in 1995 to help establish a population; however, few black crappies have been captured since. In 2000, the catch rate on black crappie was 3.56 fish/trap net, a value that falls short of the average for lakes in this classification. The black crappies ranged in total length from 6 to 13 inches, included 3 year classes, and were in good condition. Although black crappies were introduced to the lake, white crappies have become more abundant. The white crappies were captured at a rate of 47.56 fish/trap net, a value that greatly exceeds most lakes in southern Minnesota. The white crappie size structure was also very good, with high proportions exceeding 10 inches and several also exceeding 12 inches. The body condition of these white crappie was extremely good. The average lengths at age 2 were quite different for black and white crappie in Bella Lake, with black crappies attaining a mean length of 5.76 inches and white crappies reaching 6.78 inches. The difference was less notable by ages 3 and 4, with black crappies reaching 8.38 and 10.34 inches and white crappies measuring 9.11 and 10.60 inches, respectively.

Yellow and black bullhead species were present during the 2000 survey and caught at rates of 2.11 and 74.0 fish/net, respectively. These catch rates are similar to other lakes in this classification. The abundance of yellow bullheads has increased since the 1994 survey; however, their condition has declined. The yellow bullhead total lengths ranged from 6 to 11 inches. Yellow bullheads have a tendency to not overpopulate and the size structure typically remains good. Black bullhead abundance has also increased slightly since 1994. Black bullheads ranged in length from 4.5 to 10.5 inches; however, nearly all of these fish were <8 inches and body condition was low to moderate.

Common carp were caught at a rate of 58.33 fish/gill net and 9.67 fish/ trap net. Both of these rates greatly exceeded most lakes in this class. With a slight increase in density over the past 6 years, the common carp size structure has also declined. Carp ranged in length from 7.5 to 24.0 inches; however, a large proportion of these carp were <18 inches. Another strong year class of carp was produced in 2000, with numerous age-0 specimens sampled during the natural reproduction assessment. With a greater number of small carp feeding in this system, the likelihood of decreasing water clarity and vegetation becomes a greater concern and competition for benthic food resources becomes probable.

The lake has maintained a relatively constant bigmouth buffalo population. The 2000 gill net catch rate was 6.0 fish/net and is similar to other lakes in the classification. Buffalo condition and size structure were also comparable between 1994 and 2000, with fish widely dispersed between 11 and 28 inches in length. Other species captured during the 2000 assessment included (total number in parentheses) channel catfish (3), bluegill (16), pumpkinseed (1), and white sucker (3) and numerous fathead minnows. The channel catfish were caught at a rate of 1 fish/gill net. The bluegill were captured at a rate of 1.78 fish/ trap net and ranged in total length from 5 to 8 inches. Several year classes of bluegill were present, indicating that some successful reproduction had likely occurred.

The management plan objective of two pike per gill net set was not achieved. In fact, northern pike were absent from the 2000 gill net assessment. The predator population in the lake is certainly not without a good predator species, as the walleye abundance has greatly increased. Secondary management of yellow perch and black crappies have also met with several obstacles, including low black crappie density and extremely poor yellow perch condition. Yellow perch tend to rely on benthic food resources; however, the relatively high abundance of small common carp and bullheads, has likely reduced food availability and decreased water clarity. The panfish population, though, has been bolstered with a couple of strong year classes of white crappies. White crappie density is somewhat high; however, the growth and condition of this population is excellent, indicating that there is a sufficient food supply and environmental condition are within tolerable parameters. Additionally, water clarity in Bella Lake has been historically poor and white crappies have been known to succeed in turbid reservoirs where black crappies have failed.

Bella Lake has relatively good walleye, northern pike, crappie, yellow perch and bluegill habitats during average to high water years. The walleye appear to be succeeding in the lake with little assistance; however, to increase the potential predator base, an occasional fingerling stocking could be completed. When water levels are low, bluegill and northern pike cover is unavailable; however, pike should continue to be managed for the years when water levels are high. Although black crappies were introduced and have been the target of management efforts, white crappies have faired better and provided a quality population. The yellow perch population is in relatively poor condition; however, perch populations can highly vary from year to year.
