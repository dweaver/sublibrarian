Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/16/2007)

Burntside Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 2, which consists of 15 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are very large and deep, have irregular shoreline shapes, and have clear and soft (unmineralized) water. Burntside Lake is smaller and has clearer water than most of the lakes in this lake class.

Burntside Lake was thermally stratified on 07/16/2007 with surface temperatures of 69 F and bottom temperatures of 43 F. Good oxygen for trout (more than 5 ppm) was retained to the bottom in each of the three major basins (North Arm, East End, West End) of this lake. The water quality in 2007 was similar to previous investigations on this lake that showed Burntside Lake was oligotrophic with excellent water quality and low water fertility.

Fish populations in 2007 were investigated with stratified shallow and deep gillnets, and with deep smelt gillnets. Twelve of each type of gillnet were set, but one of each type was disturbed by the public, leaving eleven of each net type with usable data. Trapnets were not used to sample shallow water fish populations in 2007 due to staffing constraints and because previous trapnetting in this lake captured low numbers of fish of the same species and in the same proportions as the gillnet catch.

SHALLOW WATER FISH POPULATIONS

The shallow gillnets were set in and above the thermocline at an average depth of 9 ft for the shallow end of the net and an average depth of 20 ft for the deep end of the net. On average, the water temperature at both the shallow and the deep ends of the nets was 68 F, although some nets tailed off into 30 ft deep water where the temperature was 57 F. Fifteen previous shallow water investigations were conducted on Burntside Lake dating back to 1950, with 10-19 shallow gillnets used in each investigation. Shallow water fish populations in 2007, as in previous investigations on this lake, were dominated by walleye, northern pike, smallmouth bass, and rock bass. Fish catch quartiles have not been developed for stratified gillnets, so no comparison can be made with other Lake Class 2 lakes.

Walleye have been stocked in Burntside Lake since 1911, and walleye fingerlings have been stocked every-other-year in Burntside Lake since 1988. Walleye numbers in 2007 (4.2/gillnet) were lower than in recent investigations on this lake, but were similar to the median catch of 4.4/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes in 2007 averaged 18.0" (2.9 lb), which was larger than the average size of 15.7" in all investigations on this lake. All but two of the walleye captured in 2007 appeared to be from fingerling-stocked year classes. The two walleye that appeared to be from a non-stocked year class, both age six, were the same size as the age seven fish, indicating their assigned ages may have been in error and that they may have been from a stocked year. Scales of older walleye in Burntside Lake are difficult to read, and inconsistencies such as this sometimes occur. Except for the age one walleye that were stocked as fingerlings the previous fall, the older walleye were very plump, with lots of visceral fat, and growth was very fast (in the fourth quartile, by area standards). Thirty of 42 walleye stomachs examined were empty, eight contained smelt, three contained unidentified fish remains, and one contained a sculpin.

Northern pike numbers in 2007 (2.7/gillnet) were higher than the median catch of 1.7/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2007 averaged 23.9" (3.5 lb), which was similar to the average size of 23.6" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike captured in 2007 (caught in a deep gillnet) was 33.5". Pike scales were difficult to read, resulting in conflicting data on age and growth. Twenty two of 28 pike stomachs examined were empty, the remainder contained fish including yellow perch, smallmouth bass, rock bass, and smelt.

Smallmouth bass numbers in 2007 (1.7/gillnet) were higher than the median catch of 1.4/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Smallmouth sizes in 2007 averaged 12.2" (1.2 lb), which was similar to the average size of 11.7" in all investigations on this lake. The largest smallmouth was 18.7". Four of six smallmouth stomachs examined were empty, and two contained crayfish.

Some of the northern pike, smallmouth bass, and walleye captured in 2007 were infected with neascus and yellow grub. Some of the smallmouth bass were also infected with bass tapeworm larvae in their viscera. Neascus (black spot), yellow grub, and bass tapeworm are all common parasites that are native to the area. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.

SMELT POPULATION

Rainbow smelt were first observed in Burntside Lake in 1970 and a variety of nets were used to catch smelt through the mid 1980's. Regular smelt assessment gillnetting began in 1987 at standard locations (two nets in each of the three basins on Burntside Lake: North Arm, East End, West End), with nets that were 6 ft x 100 ft x 1/2" bar mesh multifilament nylon, and 6 ft x 150 ft or 200 ft x 3/8" bar mesh multifilament nylon. Standard smelt nets (200 ft x 6 ft, with one 100 ft panel of 3/8" bar mesh multifilament nylon and one 100 ft panel of 1/2" bar mesh multifilament nylon) were introduced in 1992 and previous 3/8" bar mesh net catches were converted to 100 ft equivalents for purposes of comparison with the new nets. Two mid-depth smelt nets were added to each basin in 1998 to ensure that the entire water column from the thermocline (at about 30 ft) to very deep water was sampled.

These smelt gillnet investigations showed that smelt catches averaged 130/standard smelt net from 1987 through 1995. Smelt numbers declined from 1996 through 2005, averaging 35/standard smelt net, but then rebounded to 128/smelt net in 2006 and 140/standard smelt net in 2007.

In most investigations on Burntside Lake, smelt numbers were highest in the East End and lowest in the North Arm. In 2006 and 2007, however, smelt numbers were evenly distributed in each of the three basins of Burntside Lake. As in most recent investigations on this lake, most of the smelt captured in 2007 (99 percent) were caught in the 3/8" bar mesh. Smelt sizes in 2005 averaged 4.9"; the largest was 8.2".

Also captured in the smelt gillnets in 2007 were 11 slimy sculpin and three lake trout. One of the lake trout (5.9") had no fin clips, indicating it was produced naturally. The two larger trout (12.2", right ventral clip; 19.5", left ventral clip) had been stocked as yearlings in 2004 and 2000, respectively.

OTHER COLD WATER FISH POPULATIONS

Lake trout have been stocked into Burntside Lake since 1916. Since 1988 about 70,000 Gillis Lake strain lake trout yearlings have been stocked in Burntside Lake every other year. Since 1984 all stocked lake trout have been fin-clipped for later identification, to determine their survival and growth, and to monitor for the presence of unclipped trout that were produced by natural reproduction.

Lake trout numbers in 2007 (2.2/gillnet) were identical to the median catch of 2.2/gillnet in all deep water investigations on this lake. By weight, the lake trout catch in 2007 of 2.3 lb/gillnet was lower than the median catch of 3.2 lb/gillnet in all deep water investigations on this lake. Lake trout sizes in 2007 averaged 13.2" (1.0 lb), which was smaller than the average size of 14.5" in all investigations on this lake. The largest lake trout captured in 2007 was 23.2". Nine of 22 lake trout stomachs examined in 2007 were empty, eight contained smelt, four contained slimy sculpins, and one contained invertebrate remains. A few of the lake trout had anchor worms attached to them. Anchor worms are common parasites of fish and do not harm humans.

Of the 26 lake trout captured in the standard deep gillnets and the smelt nets in 2005, 12 were fin clipped, indicating they had been stocked. The 14 unclipped lake trout were aged by sectioning their otoliths, but the otoliths often broke before the sectioning was completed and the confidence of the reader was low for some fish.

Of the fourteen unclipped lake trout, one trout (5.9") was age 1+ from the 2006 year class, eight trout averaging 12.1" (range: 11.3-13.2") were age 3+ from the 2004 year class, three trout averaging 17.6" (range: 16.4-19.5") were age 4+ from the 2003 year class, and two trout were age 5 or older (19.5", 21.9"). Growth of the age 1-3 trout was typical for this lake, but growth of the age 4 and 5+ trout was substantially faster than typical, indicating that there may have been a problem with the ageing as mentioned in the previous paragraph.

The presence of young, unclipped trout indicates that natural recruitment of lake trout is occurring. The proportion of naturally produced lake trout in the deep gillnet and smelt gillnet catches has increased in recent investigations, from 11 percent (3 of 27) in 2001, to 23 percent (6 of 26) in 2003, 33 percent (9 of 27) in 2005, and 54 percent (14 of 26) in 2007.

Ten of the 12 fin-clipped lake trout had clips identifying year classes that could reasonably be assigned to the fish based on their lengths. Seven trout were age 2+ from the 2005 year class stocked in 2006 and averaged 9.1" (range: 7.9-11.0"). Three trout were age 4+ from the 2003 year class stocked in 2004 and averaged 13.9" (range: 12.2-15.3"). Two larger trout (19.5", 23.2") had clips that did not reasonably match with their size.

Considering both stocked and naturally produced lake trout in Burntside Lake, age 1+ and 2+ lake trout growth rates were typical for lake trout lakes in the Tower Fisheries Management Area, while age 3+ and older growth rates were faster than normal. The faster growth of older trout may be due to the abundant smelt population available as forage.

The lake whitefish population in 2007 continued a trend of nonexistent recruitment, resulting in declining numbers and increasing sizes. Whitefish numbers in Burntside Lake averaged 9.5/deep gillnet from 1950 through 1981, declined to an average of 5.2/deep gillnet through 1985, then gradually declined to the 2007 catch of 1.3/gillnet. Whitefish sizes gradually increased during this time, from an average of 14.1" in the first two investigations, to an average of 24.5" in 2007. No whitefish under 22" were caught in 2007.

Cisco have apparently been extirpated from Burntside Lake. An average of 4.1 cisco/deep gillnet were captured from 1950 through 1986. Cisco numbers then gradually declined, and no cisco have been captured in the last five deep water gillnet assessments.

We feel that the unintentional introduction of smelt has resulted in the nearly total failure of natural recruitment of walleye, lake whitefish, and cisco in Burntside Lake. Lake trout natural reproduction was also poor and has only recently showed some signs of rebounding. These are all cold water spawning species that do not nest or otherwise protect their eggs or young following spawning. The literature has many examples of similar recruitment failures for cold water spawning fish in lakes stocked with smelt (e.g. Evans and Loftus, 1987. Colonization of Inland Lakes in the Great Lakes Region by Rainbow Smelt. International Symposium of Stocks Assessment and Yield Prediction. Canadian Journal of Fisheries and Aquatic Sciences. Vol. 44, Supplement 2:249-266). The mechanisms by which smelt affect recruitment of other species may be direct (by preying on eggs or young fish) or indirect (by competing with these species for forage, particularly invertebrate forage). ?
