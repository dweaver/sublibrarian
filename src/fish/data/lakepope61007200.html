Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/10/2006)

Lake Gilchrist is a shallow, 321-acre basin located in southeast Pope County. Maximum depth is 24.0 ft. The East Branch of the Chippewa River runs through the basin. Water quality and habitat conditions are variable in response to a large watershed area, river flow, and prevailing climatic conditions. Lake level has varied as much as 5.5 ft between dry and wet weather cycles. Water transparency in recent years has averaged 5.0 ft during summer months. An abundant and diverse aquatic plant community helps sustain reasonably good water clarity. One plant species is an undesired aquatic exotic species. Eurasian watermilfoil was discovered in the lake in 1996. This invasive plant has expanded in density and coverage to nuisance levels. Even though habitat conditions can be highly variable, and occasionally limiting to sight-feeding and nesting fishes, Lake Gilchrist is very productive and supports a diverse fishery. Fishing attributes can be generally described as "moderate densities of medium-size gamefishes".

Walleye are well suited to riverine influences on the lake. Fingerlings are stocked in alternate years to supplement natural recruitment and increase population abundance. Gillnet catches can't be qualified as "abundant", but survey catch rates have been within the range of "normal" for similar lakes. Walleye grow fast and are fairly long-lived, thus fishing quality in terms of average size caught is very good. Sixty-seven percent of walleye in the capture sample exceeding 20.0 inches in length.

Northern pike are abundant and growth is above average, yet few large pike are caught. Average weight of pike captured in the 2006 survey was 2.3 lbs. The largest captured measured 29.7 inches. Most pike captured in surveys have been young fish less than five-years-old. Habitat conditions likely limit survival and longevity. Either older pike move out of the lake or don't live long enough to maximize growth potential.

Members of the sunfish family, specifically largemouth bass, bluegill, and black crappie, also face less than optimal habitat conditions and reproductive success in any given year can be quite variable. Such variability or limitations to abundance are not necessarily bad for anglers. Population densities are not excessive and subsequently, growth rate and size distribution are good. Sixty-seven percent of largemouth bass captured in 2006 survey efforts exceeded 15.0 inches in length. The largest fish sampled measured 19.0 inches. Similarly, bluegill cannot be described as "abundant" but they grow comparatively fast in Lake Gilchrist. The average length of bluegill captured during the 2006 survey was 6.4 inches. Despite fairly heavy harvest, particularly during winter months, nine percent of bluegill measured in 2006 exceeded 8.0 inches in length. Poor sampling efficiency of black crappie undermines certainty of survey catch statistics and tends to under-represent actual population abundance. Based on feedback from anglers, abundance and fishing success can be variable among years, but true to population attributes of other sunfishes, crappie commonly exceed 10.0 inches and are a valued component of the overall fishery.

Anglers must help contain the spread of Eurasian watermilfoil in west-central Minnesota. Lake Gilchrist holds the only know infestation of this undesirable exotic plant within Pope County. Please be responsible in preventing transfer to other lakes. Remove all aquatic vegetation from your boat and trailer when leaving Lake Gilchrist. ?
