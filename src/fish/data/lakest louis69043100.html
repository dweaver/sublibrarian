Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

No fish consumption guidelines are available for this lake. For more information, see the "Fish Consumption Advice" pages at the Minnesota Department of Health.


Status of the Fishery (as of 05/22/2007)

Cedar Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 13, which consists of 84 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are small, shallow, and have very soft (unmineralized) water. Cedar Lake is smaller and deeper than most of the lakes in this lake class.

Cedar Lake was thermally stratified on 5/22/2007 with a surface temperature of 57 F and a bottom temperature of 39 F. Adequate oxygen for trout (more than 5 ppm) was present to a depth of 21 ft, where the temperature was 41 F. Previous investigations have shown that in midsummer, 5 ppm oxygen is retained to a depth of about 16 ft, where the temperature is about 62 F.

Cedar Lake has no inlets, and the outlet to the St. Louis River flows through marshes and has steep gradients that prevent upstream fish movement. There is a grate to prevent downstream fish movement where the outlet leaves the lake and crosses under Co. Rd. 341. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline are muck (53 percent), sand (22 percent), and silt (25 percent). There is marsh and bog along the entire shoreline. Aquatic plants grow to a depth of 10 ft; the most common plants are yellow waterlily, water milfoil, and filamentous algae.

Cedar Lake has been poisoned four times to remove non-trout fish species, in 1961, 1966, 1979, and 1989. Rainbow trout have been stocked annually since 1961, except for years in which the lake was poisoned. Trout population assessments have been conducted 15 times with 1-2 gillnet sets, with 2-9 standard trapnet sets (during 13 of the assessments) and with small mesh trapnets (during 12 of the assessments). The first three assessments, through 1977, were done in mid-to-late summer. All subsequent assessments were done in late May or early June.

The catch of trout in 2007 of 2.0/gillnet was lower than the median catch of 3.2/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. The catch of trout on this lake in past assessments has been quite variable, ranging from 0/gillnet to 20/gillnet depending on the number of stocked fall fingerlings that survived their first winter. Two of the four trout caught in the 2007 gillnets were from the previous fall's stocking. The remaining trout were 12"-14".

By weight, the 2007 trout catch of 1.1 lb/gillnet was lower than the median catch of 4.0 lb/gillnet in all investigations on this lake and was lower than the median catch of trout (all species combined) of 3.7 lb/gillnet in recent investigations of 25 stream trout lakes in the Tower Fisheries Management Area.

Two of the trout captured in 2007 had insect remains in their stomachs; one trout stomach was empty, and one contained fish remains.

Sunfish were eradicated from Cedar Lake during the 1989 poisoning and were absent in assessments through 2001. Fair numbers of bluegill sunfish (10.8/standard trapnet) were captured in the 2004 assessment, and very high numbers of bluegill sunfish (130/standard trapnet and 424/small mesh trapnet) were captured in the 2007 assessment. Sunfish sizes in the standard trapnets averaged 6.8" and the largest was 8.0".

Also captured in the small mesh trapnets in 2007 were fairly high numbers of fathead minnows (110/net) and redbelly dace (60/net). A few golden shiner were captured in 2007. A few white sucker had been captured in the 1995, 2001, and 2004 assessments, but were not observed in 2007.?
