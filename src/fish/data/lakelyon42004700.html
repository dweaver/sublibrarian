Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/19/2006)

Lake Yankton is a 387-acre lake located near the town of Balaton, MN in Lyon County. Yankton is managed primarily for walleye and secondarily for northern pike, crappie, and yellow perch. Yankton was re-surveyed during the week of June 19, 2006 using gill nets, trap nets, and seine hauls.

The walleye catch rate in gill nets was 10.3/set, which was within the expected range. Walleye averaged nearly 16 inches in length with most of the fish ranging from 14 to 25 inches. Most of the walleye were plump and growth was quite good. Walleye fry stocking has been successful as many of the fish captured came from fry stocking. Most walleye anglers would be happy with this population and would be happy with the sizes being caught.

Northern pike numbers were down from the previous survey, however that was the highest catch rate ever recorded on Yankton. Most of the fish were quite healthy and plump and averaged just over 27 inches in length. Consistent northern pike abundance is more likely in lakes where quality wetland habitat exists. During those hot summer months, target any tiles running as northerns will be seeking out these locations for feeding and thermal refuge.

Panfish abundance appears to be fairly high in Yankton with crappie numbers dominating the fishery. Yellow perch numbers were within the expected range, with most of the fish averaging just over 7 inches in total length. As with many of the Windom Area lakes within the past few years, crappie numbers are quite high. A majority of the fish are black crappie and averaged 9 inches in length. The white crappie abundance was higher than expected and averaged over 10 inches in length. The surprise this year on Yankton was the numbers of good-sized bluegills. In fact, the 2006 survey captured the most bluegills than any previous survey on Lake Yankton. Not only was their abundance relatively high, but many of the bluegills were in the 8.5-9.0 inch range. This may indicate more aquatic vegetation than in previous years.

Black bullhead numbers are as low as they have ever been in Yankton since surveys have been done by DNR. While the abundance seems to be low compared to historical catch rates, the catch rates are still within the normal range. Most of these fish were in the 5.0-5.5 inch and 9.0-10.0 inch range.

Other species captured were bigmouth buffalo, brown bullhead, common carp, orangespotted sunfish, white sucker, and fathead minnows.

Efforts to improve the health of the lake should focus on best management practices within the watershed. Improvements and education in land stewardship will often have secondary benefits to the lake in the way of quality and quantity of habitat. Generally, improvements in the quality and quantity of lake habitat will mean direct improvements of the fishery.
