Status of the Fishery (as of 07/07/2005)

Colby Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 11, which consists of 49 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are small and have a lot of shallow shoreline area. Colby Lake is larger, has a more irregular shoreline shape, and has darker (more bog-stained) and softer (less mineralized) water than most of the lakes in this lake class. Colby Lake ranks as mesotrophic, according to the amount of chlorophyll and phosphorus in the water.

Colby Lake is used as a water source for the Laskin Energy Center coal powered electrical plant owned by Minnesota Power. The power plant intake is on the main part of the lake and has a fish screen. The outlet is on the other side of a long point, in the channel where the Partridge River exits the lake. Colby Lake was also used as a water source by the Erie Mining Company (later LTV) taconite plant to the north. Adjacent Whitewater Lake was impounded in the 1950's by Erie Mining Company, to be used as a back up water source for Colby Lake when water levels in the Partridge River were low. In operation, water would flow through a channel from Colby Lake to Whitewater Lake during the spring runoff, then be pumped back into Colby Lake when needed. This system was never used as much as expected, as 30+ foot fluctuations in the water level of Whitewater Lake were anticipated, but never realized. Water levels on Colby Lake had annual fluctuations averaging 3.3 ft from 1999 through 2001, and were similar to water levels recorded from 1939 through 1980.

Colby Lake was thermally stratified on 7/11/2005 with a surface temperature of 81 F and a bottom temperature of 55 F in the central portion of the lake. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 22 ft, where the temperature was 57 F. Very high surface temperatures were recorded near the power plant outlet (101 F) and further upstream (100 F) near Benchmark 2. The summer of 2005 was very dry, with very low flows in the Partridge River when oxygen and temperature profiles were done on 7/11/2005. It is likely that some of the warm power plant effluent was cycling back around to the main lake basin towards the power plant intake, as there was hardly any flow in the Partridge River at the outlet. It should be noted that the inlet and outlet stream flows were measured on 7/15/2005, after a rainstorm the previous day had replenished water levels somewhat.

Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline of Colby Lake are mostly boulder and rubble, with lower amounts of gravel, sand, and silt. Aquatic plants grow to a depth of 6 ft and are moderate in abundance. Water lilies, potamogetons, water shield, and algae are the most common plants. Curly-leaf pondweed, a non-native plant, was found in several locations on the west end of the lake. Also found, while shoreline seining along the southeast shoreline, was the empty shell of a Chinese mystery snail.

Colby Lake has a public access with a concrete boat ramp on the south shore. There is a public park and swimming beach to the northeast of the public access. There were 20 private homes or cabins on Colby Lake in 2005, up from six in 1985 and three in 1968. The private dwellings on Colby Lake are along the southeast and west shores; most of the shoreline (about 75 percent) is owned by Minnesota Power, much of this shoreline was acquired by Minnesota Power from the bankrupt LTV Taconite operation around 2003.

The 2005 fisheries lake survey was conducted in mid July and used nine gillnets, nine trapnets, and shoreline seining to sample fish populations. Previous fisheries investigations on Colby Lake were two fisheries lake surveys (1968, 1985) and seven fish population assessments; each of these investigations used 3-12 gillnets and 4-10 trapnets. Shoreline seining was done during the two previous surveys.

These investigations show that fish populations have generally been low, but that bluegill sunfish catches have recently increased. Fish populations in 2005 were dominated by bluegill sunfish, northern pike, black crappie, channel catfish, white sucker, and yellow perch. Largemouth bass may be more abundant than indicated in this survey, as bass are known to be "net-shy" and difficult to catch in standard sampling nets.

Bluegill catches in the first seven investigations on Colby Lake, from 1968 through 1995, averaged 4.6/trapnet; all of these investigations were done in late August or early September when bluegill are less likely to be caught in trapnets. Trapnetting was moved to early July in 2000 and 2005, and the bluegill catches in these investigations (24.8/trapnet and 26.7/trapnet, respectively) were in the fourth quartile for this lake class. We do not know if the increase in bluegill catches on Colby Lake mean the population has increased or is just a result of changing the sampling dates to early in the summer. We have observed increases in bluegill populations in many area lakes in recent years, irrespective of trapnetting dates.

Bluegill sizes in 2005 averaged 6.6" (0.22 lb), which was near the median for this lake class. The largest bluegill captured in 2005 was 8.6". Many of the bluegill (44 percent) were age four, from the 2001 year class. Growth of bluegill captured in 2005 was somewhat faster than normal (in the third quartile) by area standards.

Northern pike numbers in 2005 (5.4/gillnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were about twice as high as the highest previous pike catch (2.9/gillnet in 1989). Pike sizes in 2005 averaged 23.3" (2.7 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 19.3" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike captured in 2005 was 37.6". Pike scales were difficult to read, but pike appeared to be well distributed among ages 3-6 and pike growth appeared to be in the normal range by area standards.

Black crappie numbers in 2005 (8.9/trapnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were higher than the median crappie catch of 2.8/trapnet in all investigations on this lake. Crappie sizes in 2005 averaged 7.5" (0.23 lb), which was in the second quartile for this lake class and was similar to the average crappie size of 7.6" in all investigations on this lake. The largest crappie captured in 2005 was 9.6". Most of the crappie were ages 3-5. Growth of crappie captured in 2005 was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile) by area standards.

Channel catfish numbers in 2005 (4.4/trapnet) were much higher than the median catch of 0.2/trapnet in all investigations on this lake. The reason for the increase in the trapnet catch of catfish in 2005 is not known. Catfish sizes in 2005 averaged 17.2" (1.6 lb); the largest catfish was 22.8".

Yellow perch numbers in 2005 (4.2/gillnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median perch catch of 4.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Perch sizes in 2005 averaged 6.1" (0.09 lb), which was in the first quartile for this lake class. Perch were the most commonly caught species in the shoreline seines in 2005.

Many of the game fish examined in 2005 were infected with neascus (black spot). Many of the perch were also infected with yellow grub. Black spot and yellow grub are common parasites that are native to the area. They cannot infect people, are often removed by filleting fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.
