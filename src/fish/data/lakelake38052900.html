Status of the Fishery (as of 07/25/2005)

Snowbank Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 1, which consists of 34 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are large, deep, have irregular shoreline shapes, and have clear and soft (unmineralized) water. Snowbank Lake is larger than many of the lakes in this lake class.

Snowbank Lake was thermally stratified on 07/25/2005 and retained 4.9 ppm oxygen to the bottom at 100 ft, where the temperature was 46 F. The major inlet, from Parent Lake, has a large sucker spawning run each spring. The outlet is to Boot Lake. Bottom substrates along the shoreline of Snowbank Lake are mostly rubble, boulder, and ledgerock. The shallow south bay (Pickeral Bay) has a mucky bottom. Aquatic plants are sparse and grow to a depth of 15 ft; floatingleaf pondweed is the most common plant.

There is a public access with a concrete boat ramp and a canoe landing. Snowbank Lake is a popular entry point to the Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness (BWCAW), which is administered by the United States Forest Service (USFS). The northeast half of Snowbank Lake is in the BWCAW, where overnight camping permits and outboard motor permits are required and there is a 25 horsepower outboard motor limit. Snowmobiles and power augers are not allowed in the BWCAW portion of Snowbank Lake. Day use paddle only self written permits (on forms provided by the USFS) are required in the BWCAW during summer months. From October 1 through April 30 BWCAW, self written permits on forms provided by the USFS are required. Questions about BWCAW permits should be directed to the USFS. There are two resorts on Snowbank Lake (Smitty's and Wilderness Bay) with about 20 cabins and there are about 25 private homes or cottages (many of which are on USFS leased land on the southwest corner of the lake). A third resort (Jackpine Lodge) was purchased in 2004 and is currently being divided into private cabins.

There has been some confusion about the size of Snowbank Lake. The DNR lake contour map, made in 1949, lists the surface area at 3,303 acres. Kevin Zytkovicz (DNR Division of Ecological Services) examined GIS data in 2005 and found that the rectified lake outline of Snowbank Lake was 4,654 acres minus 380 acres of island area, resulting in a lake surface area of 4,273 acres.

This cold-water fish population assessment consisted of 12 deep gillnets (set in water colder than 55 F), in depths of 42-100 ft. Eight previous cold water investigations, dating back to 1950, consisted of 4-12 deep gillnet sets. Net catches from research studies from 1969 through 1979 and in 1994 are not used for comparison in this discussion because non-standard sampling methods were used. Lake class quartiles for cold water gillnets have not been calculated and are not presented in this report.

The total catch of fish (all species combined) in the deep gillnets in 2005 of 18 fish/net (17 lb/net) was similar to the median catch of 16 fish/net (17 lb/net) in all cold water investigations on this lake. More burbot were captured in 2005 than in most previous investigations.

Lake trout numbers in 2005 (2.2/deep gillnet) were at the median for all cold water investigations on this lake. Lake trout sizes in 2005 averaged 16.9", which was slightly smaller than the average size of 17.7" in all investigations on this lake. The largest lake trout captured in 2005 was 29.7". Most of the lake trout were age three (9 fish), four (3 fish) or five (6 fish), indicating good natural reproduction in recent years. Only two mature lake trout (both age nine) were caught. Growth of lake trout captured in 2005 was faster than average for this lake and for the area. Lake trout stomachs were empty (8) or contained cisco (5), yellow perch (3), sculpin (3), unidentified fish (2), or mayflies (1).

Burbot numbers in 2006 (4.0/deep gillnet) were higher than the median catch of 2.9/deep gillnet in all cold water investigations on this lake. Burbot numbers in the last four investigations (from 1994 through 2005) were higher than in previous investigations. Burbot sizes in 2005 averaged 17.7", which was smaller than the average size of 18.2" in all investigations on this lake. The largest burbot captured in 2005 was 22.2".

Cisco numbers in 2005 (9.8/deep gillnet) were at the median for all cold water investigations on this lake. Cisco sizes in 2005 averaged 7.9", which was similar to the average size of 7.7" in all investigations on this lake. The largest cisco captured in 2005 was 12.2".
