Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/09/2004)

North Big Pine Lake is a fertile, 387 acre lake located in Pine and Aitkin counties. The lake averages 14 feet with a 22 foot maximum. Water clarity is generally good with a light bog stain, though algae blooms are not uncommon. Lake users can access North Big Pine through the public access on the north end of the lake or from South Big Pine lake. While natural reproduction does occur, due to limited spawning habitat, walleye fingerlings are stocked biennially.

Anglers on North Big Pine can expect moderate fishing pressure. Walleye abundance is typical for the lake type but both size structure and catch rate were lower in 2004 than in the recent assessments. Despite past evidence of natural reproduction, scale aging attributed all sampled walleye to stocking years. Northern pike numbers are below normal for the lake type but quality sized northern pike dominanted the netting with most fish over 24 inches. Largemouth bass numbers are very good with an increase in quality. Bass averaged 14.2 inches and ranged from 11.1 to 18.2 inches. Yellow perch are far less abundant than five years ago, but remain small, functioning primarily as a forage species. Bluegill and black crappie numbers are more than adequate to warrant angling, but size may be an issure with some anglers. The trapnet sample found few crappies greater than 9 inches and few bluegill larger than 7 inches.
