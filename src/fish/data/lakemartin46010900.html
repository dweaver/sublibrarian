Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 09/02/2008)

Fox Lake, near the town of Sherburn, is a 1,041-acre lake with a maximum depth of 18 feet. Fox Lake has a low watershed to lake ratio of 4 to 1, which indicates a smaller drainage area when compared to the surface acres of the lake. Fox Lake is managed primarily for walleye and muskellunge and secondarily for channel catfish, white crappie, and black crappie. The annual population assessment was conducted during the week of September 2, 2008.

Historically, walleye catch rates have not changed much in over 8 years despite regular walleye fingerling stockings. The regular stockings were equivalent to stocking 20 walleye fingerlings/acre/year with little return to the survey. Total lengths of walleye sampled ranged from 8.4 to 26.4 inches and averaged 16.8 inches. Six year classes of walleye were sampled in 2008 with most correlating back to fingerling stockings in 2004, 2005, and 2006. Nearly half the walleye sampled in 2008 were 14 inches and larger. Although numbers were higher in the early 90s, walleye can still be caught with regularity as reported in the open water creel survey on Fox Lake in 2008.

A total of 4 muskellunge were sampled in trap nets and gill nets combined in 2008. Muskellunge sampling has been extremely challenging over the years. Typically we sample very few muskies each year during the annual assessment. In the last 8 assessments we have sampled a total of 26 muskies in trap nets and gill nets combined. Although muskie sampling can be difficult and sample sizes are typically small, the sizes of fish captured indicate the presence of legal sized fish. The creel survey during the open water period of 2008 showed that 25% of the anglers fishing Fox Lake were targeting muskellunge. The creel survey estimated that 203 muskellunge were captured and only 8 were harvested. Nearly 96% of the muskellunge captured were released. The estimated average size muskie caught was just over 37 inches. Muskellunge (520 fish) will continue to be stocked in even years through 2010. In the spring of 2009, we are planning to conduct another special assessment to get better estimates of muskie size structure and abundance.

The channel catfish catch rate in gill nets was 4.5 fish/set, which was down from the 2007 assessment of 11.3 fish/set. Historically catch rates have ranged from 0.6 to 15.2 fish/set. Total lengths of channel catfish ranged from 18.6 to 25.4 inches with a mean of 21.4 inches. The average total length channel catfish sampled in gill nets has increased 8.6 inches during a 6-year time frame. During a mid-summer evening, there is a good chance of catching some nice sized channel catfish.

The black crappie catch rate in trap nets was 5.4 fish/set, which was within the expected range (1.8 to 21.2 fish/set). Based on trap net catch rates, black crappie numbers have not changed since last year. Total lengths of black crappie in trap nets ranged from 3.2 to 11.8 inches and averaged 9.3 inches. It would appear that white crappie outnumber black crappie in Fox Lake by at least 2 to 1. Total lengths and average sizes are similar between the two species. Either species can provide some good action. Since 2004, both species have showed an increase of 2 inches in average length.

Compared to last year, yellow perch numbers are nearly the same. Total lengths ranged from 6.5 to 8.9 inches and averaged 7.4 inches. Most of the fish sampled were in the 6.5 to 8.0 inch range. It is possible to catch a few perch while targeting the seemingly abundant crappie in Fox Lake.

Freshwater drum, or sheephead, numbers continue to be relatively high. Since 1981, sheephead numbers continue to be nearly the same but average size is increasing. This may suggest that predators, such as walleyes or muskellunge, are foraging on the smaller freshwater drum and thereby shifting the size structure toward larger fish. Total lengths ranged from 11.0 to 19.1 inches and averaged 12.9 inches.

Black bullhead numbers in Fox Lake continue to decline. A total of 10 black bullhead were sampled in gill nets and trap nets combined in 2008. Since 1999, catch rates during annual assessments have revealed a declining trend in abundance. However, all of the bullheads sampled in 2008 were at least 10 inches in total length. Natural reproduction is either very limited and/or heavy predation on smaller bullheads limit their abundance. It is possible to catch a bullhead on Fox Lake but there are plenty of lakes nearby with better abundance, such as Big Twin Lake.

Other species captured were bigmouth buffalo, bluegill, common carp, golden shiner, largemouth bass, and yellow bullhead.

Efforts to improve the health of the lake should focus on best management practices within the watershed. Improvements and education in land stewardship will often have secondary benefits to the lake in the way of quality and quantity of habitat. Generally, improvements in the quality and quantity of lake habitat will mean direct improvements of the fishery.
