Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/18/2007)

Johnson Lake is in Ecological Lake Class 12, which consists of 90 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are small, shallow, have irregular shoreline shapes, and have turbid and soft (unmineralized) water. Johnson Lake is larger and more irregularly shaped than most of the lakes in this lake class.

Johnson Lake was slightly thermally stratified on 06/18/2007 with a surface temperature of 71 F and a bottom temperature of 62 F. Adequate oxygen for fish (more than 2 ppm) was retained to a depth of 15 ft, where the temperature was 62 F. Lake bottom substrates along the shoreline of Johnson Lake are mostly boulder, sand, and silt. Aquatic plants grow to a depth of 4 ft; the most common plants are sedges and sweetgale along the shoreline, and floatingleaf pondweed. The inlet from Mud Lake has beaver dams that limit fish movement. The outlet to One Pine Lake has no fish barriers at high water levels.

Fish sampling in this fish population assessment was done with six gillnets and nine trapnets. Eight previous fisheries investigations dating back to 1965 used 2-9 gillnets. Seven of these previous investigations used 7-15 trapnets. Shoreline seining for small fish was done during three of these previous investigations.

The total catch of fish (all species combined) in the gillnets in 2007 of 19.6 fish/net (15.1 lb/net) was in the first quartile for this lake class and was similar to the median catch of 13.5 fish/net (12.5 lb/net) in all investigations on this lake. The gillnet catch was dominated by northern pike, white sucker, and yellow perch.

The total catch of fish in the trapnets in 2007 of 13.6 fish/net (14.7 lb/net) was similar to the median catch of 14.0 fish/net (14.7 lb/net) in all investigations on this lake. The trapnet catch was dominated by northern pike, bluegill sunfish, black crappie, and white sucker.

Northern pike numbers in 2007 (3.5/gillnet) were near the median for this lake class and were similar to the median pike catch of 3.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Pike sizes in 2007 averaged 20.6" (2.0 lb), which was in the third quartile for this lake class and was larger than the average size of 19.2" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike in 2007 (caught in a trapnet) was 32.4". Most pike captured in 2007 were age three, four, and five. Pike growth was slower than normal (in the first or second quartile, depending on the age) by area standards.

Bluegill sunfish numbers in 2007 (5.8/trapnet) were in the third quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median bluegill catch of 4.9/trapnet in all investigations on this lake. Bluegill sizes in 2007 averaged 5.5" (0.15 lb), which was near the median for this lake class and was similar to the average size of 5.9" in all investigations on this lake. The largest bluegill in 2007 (caught in a gillnet) was 8.5". Most bluegill captured in 2007 were ages two, five, and six. Bluegill growth was somewhat slower than normal (in the second quartile) by area standards.

Black crappie numbers in 2007 (1.8/trapnet) were in the second quartile for this lake class and were lower than the median crappie catch of 5.6/trapnet in all investigations on this lake. Crappie sizes in 2007 averaged 8.3" (0.4 lb) which was near the median for this lake class and was identical to the average size of 8.3" in all investigations on this lake. The largest crappie caught in 2007 was 11.9". Crappie growth was slower than normal (in the first quartile) by area standards.

Walleye fingerlings have been stocked every third year in Johnson Lake since 1986. Walleye numbers in 2007 (1.5/gillnet) were in the first quartile for this lake class and were similar to the median walleye catch of 2.0/gillnet in all investigations on this lake. Walleye sizes in 2007 averaged 13.2" (0.9 lb), which was in the second quartile for this lake class and was smaller than the average size of 14.9" in all investigations on this lake. The largest walleye in 2007 (caught in a trapnet) was 26.1". All but one of the walleye captured in 2007 were from years in which fingerlings had been stocked. Walleye growth was normal by area standards.

Largemouth bass may be more abundant in Johnson Lake than indicated by the low catches in the trapnets and gillnets. Bass are "net-shy" and difficult to catch in standard sampling nets.

A few of the game fish examined in 2007 were infected with neascus. A few of the yellow perch were also infected with yellow grub. Neascus (black spot) and yellow grub are common parasites that are native to the area. They cannot infect humans, are often removed by filleting fish, and are killed at temperatures used to cook fish.?
