Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/17/2006)

Green Prairie Lake is a shallow, 178 acre lake with a maximum depth of 22 feet. Water clarity was fair with a secchi disk reading of 8.7 feet in the middle of July. Sand was the most common shoal water substrate with abundant vegetation growth out to a depth of 11 feet. The most common submerged plant species were coontail, flat-stem pondweed, largeleaf pondweed, and wild celery. The City of Little Falls also has a swimming beach on the south shore of the lake. The management goals for the lake focus on largemouth bass, northern pike, walleye, and bluegills. Walleye fingerlings are stocked in odd numbered years when available. Several fish populations in the lake displayed some significant changes since the 2000 survey. The most notable for fishermen was a decrease in walleye abundance. Yellow perch abundance also appeared to drop while yellow bullhead numbers went up. Even though walleye numbers went down, the catch rate was within the normal range when compared to similar type lakes. The walleye captured in the survey were between 13 and 25 inches with an average size of about 18.5 inches or about 2.4 pounds. Anglers reported that the best walleye fishing has typically been in the spring and early ice-up. Fishermen on the lake during the survey reported catching or hearing of an occasional large walleye, some up to 8 pounds. The lake has little structure so anglers should concentrate efforts along the weedlines. Northern pike numbers stayed about same as in the last survey and even though the catch rate fell within the normal range for this type of lake it was above the desired management goal. A wide range of sizes was seen in the survey, from 15 to 31 inches in length. Average size of the pike was about 21 inches or 2.3 pounds. Anglers should consider keeping northern pike less than 24 inches as a harvestable surplus of small fish exists. Reducing the population of small pike could be beneficial to the entire fish community. Walleye management in particular would benefit from lower pike numbers. The drop in perch abundance may be result of the high pike numbers as the perch can be an important prey species for northern pike and walleye. Perch can also be instrumental in helping maintain a well balanced bluegill population with fast growing, quality individuals. One factor that may make the possession of northern pike undesirable to fishermen is that many of the fish had neascus or black spot. Neascus is a trematode or parasite that usually resides in the skin of the fish. The life cycle of the parasite includes snails and fish-eating birds, such as herons and bitterns. Filleting the fish will remove most of the parasites and cooking will kill the rest. Humans can not be infested. A panfish assessement targetting bluegills was conducted on the lake in early June of 2006. Panfish assessments will usually show a more accurate picture of the size structure of the adults in the panfish populations than summer nets will. Average size of the bluegill in the spring assessment was almost six inches with individuals over 8 inches being measured. The few black crappies that were caught in the spring assessment and summer survey were not that impressive. The biggest crappie caught was just over 10 inches with the rest being under 9 inches in length. Anglers reported catching small black crappies in the summer while bigger ones are more common in the winter along with some nice bluegills. Neither of the netting efforts in 2006 produced much for a largemouth bass catch. Spring electrofishing which is usually conducted to evaluate the bass in a lake was not done. The last survey in 2000 indicated that Green Prairie Fish Lake has an abundant bass population and anglers also reported catching lots of bass this year. Green Prairie Fish Lake has shown in the past that the lake can produce trophy size fish up to 20 inches although bass under 12 inches are more common. Yellow perch also provide some angling action but the presence of both neascus and yellow grub make most of the fish undesirable to anglers. All three species of bullhead, black, brown, and yellow, were sampled in the survey with yellow bullheads being the most abundant. Quality size bullheads over 12 inches were present in all three species. Other species in the fish community include bowfin or dogfish, pumpkinseed, and white sucker. The spring assessment also caught some green sunfish and hybrid sunfish. Improving the water quality in the lake should be a high priority of the lakeshore owners. Buffer strips along the developed and undeveloped inlets and lakeshore property are encouraged to reduce nutrients entering the lake through run-off. Maintaining adequate septics, keeping grass clippings and raked leaves out of the water along with the common sense use of lawn fertilizer will reduce additional nitrogen and phosphorus from entering the water thus slowing algae and excess weed growth. The Minnesota Pollution Control Agency has a good website that provides information for shoreline property owners.
