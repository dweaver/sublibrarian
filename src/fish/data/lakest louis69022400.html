Fish Consumption Guidelines

Whitefish from this lake contain dioxin, a chemical that may cause cancer in humans. Meat and dairy products also contain dioxin. At this time, MDH does not provide meal advice based on dioxin in fish. Although mercury levels in whitefish are low, you can reduce your exposure to dioxin by choosing to eat a different species and following the consumption guidelines for that species.

These fish consumption guidelines help people make choices about which fish to eat and how often. Following the guidelines enables people to reduce their exposure to contaminants while still enjoying the many benefits from fish.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/12/2004)

Lac La Croix is in Ecological Lake Class 2, which consists of 15 lakes in northeast Minnesota that are very large, have very irregular shoreline shapes, and have very soft (unmineralized) water. With a surface area of 34,070 acres, maximum depth of 168 ft, littoral area of about 25 percent, total alkalinity of 12-16 ppm, Secchi water clarity of 8 ft on the east end and 15 ft on the west end, and a Shoreline Development Factor of about 7.1, Lac La Croix is very typical of lakes in this lake class.

Lac La Croix was thermally stratified on 07/12/2004 with surface temperatures about 73 F and bottom temperatures about 43 F. On the west end of the lake, 7.0 ppm oxygen was retained to a depth of 100 ft (the length of the sampling probe cable) over a bottom depth of 120 ft at WQ-1 and 9.0 ppm oxygen was retained to a depth of 100 ft over a bottom depth of 106 ft at WQ-2. On the east end of the lake, 6.9 ppm oxygen was retained to the bottom depth of 130 ft at WQ-3, and 7.5 ppm oxygen was retained to the bottom depth of 120 ft. On both ends of the lake, the depth at which the water temperature was good for trout (55 F) was about 35 ft.

Lac La Croix is a very rocky lake with little aquatic vegetation present. The major inlets are the Basswood River and the Maligne River, entering at several points along the east end of the lake, and the outlets are the Namakan River and the Loon River, exiting on the north and west ends of the lake, respectively.

This fish population assessment consisted of 12 shallow gillnets set in depths of 8-26 ft where water temperatures were warmer than 55 F, 29 deep gillnets set in depths of 43-124 ft where the water temperatures were colder than 55 F, and 8 smelt nets set in depths of 54-100 ft. The shallow and deep gillnets used were standard Minnesota survey gillnets 6' x 250' with five 50' panels, one panel each of 0.75", 1.0", 1.25", 1.5", and 2.0" bar mesh twisted nylon, graduated from smallest to largest mesh. The smelt nets were 6' x 200' with two 100' panels, one panel each of 0.375" and 0.5" bar mesh twisted nylon. Approximately equal numbers of nets were set on each end of the lake, with the large shallow central basin between Twentyfive Island and Twentyseven Island being the dividing line between the east and west ends.

Because of the stratified sampling (separate deep and shallow gillnets) used in this assessment, the CPUE quartiles developed for standard gillnet sampling for this lake class cannot be used as a basis for comparison and are omitted from this report. Standard sampling consists of gillnets set from shallow to deeper water, with the entire net set in oxygenated water, but with variable amounts of net sampling shallow (warm) and deep (cold) water. In general, warm water is probably sampled more intensively than cold water with standard sampling because the species of interest are warm water game fish. With stratified sampling, cold water is often sampled more intensively than warm water because the species of interest is usually lake trout.

Previous Minnesota DNR Investigations were conducted in 1963 (shallow nets only - cooperatively with Ontario MNR), in 1984 (shallow and deep gillnets - Minnesota DNR only), and in 1994 (shallow, deep, and smelt gillnets, cooperatively with Ontario MNR).

SHALLOW GILLNETS:

For Lac La Croix as a whole, the total catch of fish (all species combined) in the shallow gillnets in 2004 of 12 fish/net (27 lb/net) was similar to catches in 1984, 1994, and 1999, but was lower than the catch in 1963. More walleye and white sucker were caught in 1963 than in later investigations:

Shallow water fish populations in 2004 were very similar on the east end of the lake in 2004 (13 fish/net, 26 lb/net) and on the west end of the lake (12 fish/net, 28 lb/net). In general, gillnet catches of shallow water fish on both ends of the lake were dominated by walleye and northern pike.

For Lac La Croix as a whole, walleye catches appeared to gradually decline, from a high of 14/net in 1963 to a low of 5.5/net in 2004. Net locations and depths were not recorded in the 1963 investigation, so we cannot separate catches from the east and west ends of the lake in that investigation.

Walleye numbers in the east end of the lake in 2004 (5.8/net) were similar to the median catch of 6.5/net in all four shallow water investigations in which east end catches were kept separate, beginning in 1984. East end walleye sizes in 2004 averaged 15.5", which was larger than the average size of 13.7" in all investigations on the east end of this lake. The largest walleye on the east end was 24.4" (all fish lengths in this report are total lengths). Walleye aged two through seven were captured in 2004 with no particular year class being dominant. Walleye growth was slower than normal (in the first or second quartile, depending on the age) by area standards. Twenty nine of 33 walleye stomachs examined were empty, the remaining stomachs contained cisco (2) or yellow perch (2). No smelt were found in walleye stomachs on the east end of the lake.

Walleye numbers in the west end of the lake in 2004 (5.2/net) were lower than previous catches of 8.3/net in 1984, 17.3/net in 1994, and 7.0/net in 1999. West end walleye sizes in 2004 averaged 15.8", which was larger than the average size of 14.6" in all investigations on the west end of this lake. The largest walleye on the west end was 23.7". Most of the walleye were age two (20 %) and age three (42 %). Walleye growth was somewhat faster than normal (in the third quartile) by area standards. Twenty five of 30 walleye stomachs examined were empty, the remaining stomachs contained smelt (5).

The proportion of walleye greater than 500 mm fork length is used by Ontario as an indicator of fish size quality. Using the conversion Total Length = Fork Length x 1.06 and converting to English units results in a quality size of 20.7". Gillnet catches of northern pike in Lac La Croix have been remarkably consistent over time, ranging from 3.1gillnet in 1984 to 4.6/net in 1963. The 2004 pike catch of 3.7/net falls in the middle of this historic range. Similar numbers of pike were captured in the east and west ends of the lake in 2004 as in previous investigations. Pike sizes in 2004 averaged 25" (3.6 lbs) and were similar in the east and west basins, but were somewhat larger than the average size of 23" in all investigations on this lake. The largest pike, caught on the east end, was 39". Pike scales were difficult to read, but pike from ages two through seven were present and pike growth appeared to be faster than normal by area standards.

In summary, shallow water fish populations in 2004 were dominated by walleye and northern pike:

Walleye numbers have been stable on the east end of the lake over time, but have declined on the west end, and in 2004 similar numbers of walleye were captured on each end of the lake. Walleye sizes historically have been somewhat larger on the west end of the lake, but in 2004 walleye sizes were similar on each end of the lake. The average age of walleye captured on the west end of the lake was younger than on the east end of the lake. Walleye growth was faster on the west end of the lake, and some walleye stomachs contained smelt. Male walleye matured earlier on the west end of the lake.

Pike numbers have been quite consistent over time and have been similar on each end of the lake. The average size of northern pike in 2004 and previous investigations was somewhat larger on the west end of the lake. Growth of pike captured in 2004 appeared to be similar on each end of the lake, although pike scales were difficult to read.

DEEP GILLNETS:

For Lac La Croix as a whole, the total catch of fish (all species combined) in the deep gillnets in 2004 of 19 fish/net was lower than catches in 1984, but was similar to catches in 1994 and 1999:

Date of netting 08/06/84 07/11/94 07/12/99 07/12/04 Number of gillnets 9 29 28 29 Number of fish/net 39 24 19 19 Pounds of fish/net 28 20 24 22

Deep water fish populations were higher on the east end of the lake in 2004 (29 fish/net, 26 lb/net) than on the west end (7 fish/net, 14 lb/net), due mostly to higher numbers of cisco on the east end. In general, deep water fish populations on the east end were dominated by cisco, lake whitefish, and lake trout, while fish populations on the west end were dominated by smelt (reported in the following section), lake whitefish, and lake trout.

Cisco numbers on the east end of the lake in 2004 (25.9/net) were similar to previous catches on this end of the lake, which ranged from 22.6/net to 25.9/net. East end cisco sizes in 2004 averaged 9.4"; the largest was 18.2".

Cisco numbers on the west end of the lake in 2004 (1.2/net) were similar to the catch of 0.8/net in 1994 and 0.9/net in 1999, but were much lower than the catch of 35.3/net in 1984. West end cisco sizes in 2004 averaged 8.8"; the largest was 14.1".

Lake whitefish numbers on the east end of the lake in 2004 (1.6/net) were lower than previous catches, which ranged from 4.0/net to 7.6/net. East end whitefish sizes in 2004 averaged 16.6"; the largest was 23.4".

Lake whitefish numbers on the west end of the lake in 2004 (3.5/net) were similar to previous catches, which ranged from 2.2/net to 3.5/net. West end whitefish sizes in 2004 averaged 15.9"; the largest was 23.3".

Lake trout numbers on the east end of the lake in 2004 (1.1/net) were similar to previous catches, which ranged from 0.7/net to 1.4/net. East end trout sizes in 2004 averaged 29.6"; the largest was 37.6". Historically, most trout captured in the east end of the lake have been 20" or larger, the exception being in 1999 when 10 of 21 trout were smaller than 20". Five of six trout stomachs examined in 2004 contained cisco, and one stomach was empty. Ages of the eight lake trout killed from the east end of the lake in 2004 ranged from 6 to 23, based on otolith readings.

Lake trout numbers on the west end of the lake (0.7/net) were lower than previous catches of 2.7/net in 1984 and 3.0/net in 1994, but were identical to the catch of 0.7/net in 1999. West end trout sizes in 2004 averaged 25.2"; the largest was 35.0". In contrast to assessments in 1984 and 1994 when many of the captured trout were under 20", only one of nine trout captured on the west end in 1999 was less than 20" and no trout in 2004 was under 20". Three of five trout stomachs examined in 2004 were empty, while two stomachs contained smelt. Ages of the five trout killed from the west end of the lake in 2004 were 6 (two trout) and 7 (three trout). Although sample sizes were small and there was little age overlap, it appears that trout from the west end of the lake were growing faster than on the east end.

Burbot numbers have declined in each end of the lake over time, and their numbers in 2004 were quite low: 0.1/net on the east end and 0.7 on the west end.

DEEP SMELT GILLNETS:

Smelt were not captured or observed in game fish stomachs during the 1984 assessment. Smelt were first captured in Lac La Croix in 1989, south of Snow Bay, by a University of North Dakota crew specifically looking for smelt.

Smelt nets set in 2004, as in 1999 and 1994, captured smelt in the west end of the lake as far east as net 56, about one mile southwest of Twentyfive Island. Smelt numbers in the five westernmost smelt nets in 2004 (including net 56, the westernmost of the eastern smelt nets), averaged 20.0/net, which was lower than the 1999 average catch of 73.8/net and the 1994 average catch of 38.5/net in these same five nets. As in 1994 and 1999, all but a few of the smelt captured in 2004 were in the 0.375" bar mesh, except that the easternmost net had about half of the smelt in the 0.5" mesh. Smelt sizes in 2004 averaged 4.9"; the largest was 6.7".

Smelt were found in the stomach of a lake trout in the bay west of Fish Stake Narrows in 2004. Previously, the farthest east smelt had been observed in Minnesota waters of Lac La Croix were in a lake trout stomach four miles south of Twentyfive Island in 1999.

East end cisco numbers in the 2004 smelt nets (57.2/net) were similar to the 1999 catch of 60.8/net, and were higher than the 1994 catch of 16.5/net. Most cisco were in the 0.5" bar mesh, and east end cisco sizes in the smelt nets averaged 5.7".

West end cisco numbers in the 2004 smelt nets (3.5/net) were similar to the 1999 catch of 4.2/net and were higher than in 1994, when no cisco were captured in the west end. All the cisco in 2004 were in the 0.5" bar mesh, and west end cisco sizes in the smelt nets averaged 5.5".

In summary, deep water fish populations are dominated by cisco, whitefish, and lake trout in the east end of the lake, and by smelt, whitefish, and lake trout in the west end of the lake. Smelt and cisco populations overlap in the vicinity of Twentyfive Island. Smelt were observed as far east as the bay west of Fish Stake Narrows, which is the farthest eastern observance to date. Lake trout catches have declined in the west end of the lake and were similar to east end catches in 1999 and 2004. Few small lake trout (less than 20") were captured in 2004, and all lake trout were age six years old or older. Lake trout on the west end are eating smelt and growing faster. The apparent lack of recruitment of cisco (and perhaps lake trout) in the west end of the lake and the faster growth of walleye and lake trout in the west end of the lake is consistent with the responses to smelt introductions reported elsewhere.
