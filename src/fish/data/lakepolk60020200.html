Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/24/2006)

The fish community in this small (352 acre), Polk County lake has been impacted by both drought and high water conditions during the past. As indicated by historical net catches, the low water conditions of the late 1980's and the early 1990's negatively impacted the fish community in Lake Sarah more than the recent high water levels.

In 2006, test nets revealed good catches of northern pike, walleye, and black crappie. Pike, crappie, and bluegill catch rates were at their historic highs for Lake Sarah. Age and growth analysis of these species revealed signs of heavy fishing pressure. Very few fish older than three or four years were found and growth rates for all three species were good. Fortunately for anglers, the relatively rapid growth rates by gamefish and panfish in Lake Sarah have helped to sustain the fishery.

Currently, there is a walleye stocking study in progress in this lake comparing walleye fry versus walleye fingerlings. This study will continue for at least one more survey rotation (5 years) to complete the evaluation. Early indications are that fry stocking works very well in Lake Sarah since 60% of sampled walleyes were attributed to the 2004 year class (the first fry stocking year). Even with the large number of young walleyes, the average walleye size was still quite acceptable at 15.2 inches and 1.4 pounds.

Bluegills were present in moderate numbers compared to other similar lakes, but historic high numbers compared to past reports from this lake. Like several other fish species in Lake Sarah, the bluegill population was comprised of mostly young fish. Although some fish over eight inches were found, small fish were much more prevalent in 2006.

As a small, fertile lake, Sarah is vulnerable to development related impacts. In the past 15 years, the number of shoreline homes and cabins has quadrupled from 36 to 144. Unchecked development of the shoreline and surrounding watershed can increase the rate of eutrophication (aging) and tip the balance toward foul smelling algal blooms and a fish community dominated by black bullheads. The loss of fish habitat that often results from shoreline development is a concern. Clearing of bulrush, cattails, and other aquatic plants frequently occurs. Sand is often placed on the shoreline. These practices negatively affect water quality and fish populations, two of the things most important to lakeshore owners.

Landowners are encouraged to practice shoreland best management practices to maintain property values, water quality, and fish populations. Two excellent sources are available to help in landscaping a shoreline in the least damaging, most beneficial way. "Lakescaping for Wildlife and Water Quality" and "Restore Your Shore" are available through the Minnesota Bookstore. ?
