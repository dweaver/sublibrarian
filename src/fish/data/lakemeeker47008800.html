Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/16/2008)

A resurvey was conducted at Richardson Lake in 2008 to evaluate the lake's physical, chemical, biological characteristics. A total of 10 different species of fish were sampled.

Richardson's lakeshed (5,084 acres) was dominated by row crop agriculture. The lakeshed to lake ratio was 45:1. Roughly 2/3 of the shoreline was undeveloped, with forest being the dominant coverage. Aquatic vegetation was sparse and with little diversity (6 varieties). The two species of submergent vegetation present, curlyleaf pondweed and sago pondweed, were both classified as rare. Sand and silt were the only shoalwater substrates classified as abundant. Dissolved oxygen dropped below 2 mg/l at 24 foot on 6/16/08. Water clarity was fair, with a secchi disk reading of 5.0 feet in mid June.

Gill nets sampled 19 walleye for a catch rate of 3.2/net, which was within the normal range for this type of lake. The 2008 catch rate was the highest ever recorded at Richardson Lake. The 2003 catch rate was 0.3/net. Walleye were 12.4 to 21.1 inches long averaging 16.8 inches. Walleye were 2 to 5 years old with 3 year-classes present. Approximately 5% of the gill netted fish (1 fish) came from non-stocked years, which likely indicated that the stockings were successful. Growth was fast. The latest stocking regime (1 pound of fingerlings every-other-year) was initiated in 2004.

Gill nets sampled 13 northern pike for a catch rate of 2.2/net, which was within the normal range for this type of lake. The 2003 catch rate was 9.3/net. Northern pike were 18.2 to 31.6 inches long averaging 27.0 inches. Northern pike were 1 to 4 years old with 4 year-classes present. Growth was fast.

Gill nets sampled 39 yellow perch for a catch rate of 6.5/net, which was within the normal range for lakes of this type. The 2003 catch rate was 25.0/net. The latest three catch rates indicated a downward trend in the number of fish caught per net. Yellow perch were 5.6 to 8.7 inches long averaging 6.7 inches. Yellow perch were 2 to 4 years old with 3 year-classes present.

Spring night-time electrofishing sampled 18 largemouth bass for a catch rate of 22.4/hr of electrofisher on-time. The 2003 catch rate was 62.6/hr. Catch rates (n=6) from 1991 to 2008 ranged from 2.4 to 64.6/hr, averaging 31.1/hr. The latest three catch rates indicated a downward trend for the number of fish caught per hour. Largemouth bass were 9.5 to 16.7 inches long averaging 11.3 inches. Largemouth bass were 3 to 6 years old with 2 year-classes present. Age 3 fish accounted for 89% of the electrofishing sample. The mean length at third annulus formation was 10.9 inches. A poor size structure and few year-classes indicated a marginal largemouth bass population. A lack of aquatic vegetation is likely limiting largemouth bass recruitment at Richardson Lake.

Gill nets sampled 8 carp for a catch rate of 1.3/net, which was withing the normal range for this type of lake. The 2003 catch rate was 5.5/net. Carp were 20.1 to 23.6 inches long averaging 21.7 inches. The trap net catch rate was 0.2/net. Trap net catch rates have been steadily declining since the all time high catch in 1999.

Black bullhead were not sampled in 2008. A total of 2 yellow bullhead were trap netted for a catch rate of 0.2/net.

Trap nets sampled 1,099 black crappie for a catch rate of 122.1/net, which was above the normal range for this type of lake. The 2008 catch rate was the highest ever recorded at Richardson Lake. The 2003 catch rate was 24.0/net. Black crappie were 6.3 to 10.9 inches long averaging 7.4 inches. Approximately 16% of the trap net catch was 8 inches or longer. Gill nets sampled 732 black crappie for a catch rate of 122.0/net, with fish lengths ranging from 5.2 to 11.2 inches. Black crappie were 2 to 5 years old with 4 year-classes present. The majority of the fish appeared to be from the 2005 year-class (age 3). Growth was slow. Black crappie have a history of producing sporadic strong year-classes at Richardson Lake and the 2005 age-class should provide plenty of angling opportunity.

Trap nets sampled 603 white crappie for a catch rate of 67.0/net, which was above the normal range for this lake type. The 2003 catch rate was 3.0/net. White crappie were 6.5 to 12.4 inches long averaging 8.3 inches. Approximately 64% of the trap net catch was 8 inches or longer, while 9% were 9 inches or longer. Gill nets sampled 72 white crappie for a catch rate of 12.0/net, with fish lengths ranging from 4.1 to 9.5 inches. White crappie were 1 to 6 years old with 6 year-classes present. The majority of the fish appeared to be from the 2005 year-class (age 3). On average, white crappie grew faster than black crappie did for every year-class (ages1-5). White crappie have a history of producing strong year-classes and the 2005 age-class should provide good angling opportunities.

Trap nets sampled 146 bluegill for a catch rate of 16.2/net, which was within the normal range for lakes of this type. The 2003 catch rate was 13.3/net. Bluegill were 4.2 to 8.2 inches long averaging 6.3 inches. Approximately 66% of the trap net catch was 6 inches or longer, while 23% were 7 inches or longer and 8% reached the 8 inch mark. Bluegill were 2 to 6 years old with 5 year-classes present. Growth was fast.

Shoreline seining sampled young-of-the-year: largemouth bass (69). Bluntnose minnow (17) were also seined.?
