Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/21/2006)

Lewis Lake is a 220-acre basin managed primarily for walleye and northern pike. Minnesota lakes managed for fishing are grouped into lake classes based on physical, chemical, and biological similarities. Lewis Lake is assigned to lake class 29. In order to compare the present fish population to others in the lake class and to evaluate management goals, a population assessment was conducted on Lewis Lake in August 2006.

The lake's management plan calls for walleye fingerling stocking (148 pounds) on alternate years. Long-range management goals are to sustain a moderate walleye population with a target catch rate of 2.5 per gillnet. The long-range goal for northern pike is more trophy oriented with a target weight of 4 pounds. Factors limiting the fishery include a low yellow perch forage base and limited spawning habitat for walleye and northern pike.

As in the two previous investigations, the yellow perch gillnet catch was low compared to other similar lakes. Only two perch were caught in the six gill net sets. Yellow perch are an important forage base for both walleye and northern pike. Spawning habitat didn't appear limiting with both brush and vegetation present in shallow water areas.

The walleye gillnet catch, 1.2/set, was a slight decrease from the last two samplings and below both the management goal and lake class 25th percentile. Sampled walleye were smaller than in 2001, ranging in length from 13.2 to 22.9 inches with a 15.6-inch and 1.4 pound mean. Walleye averaged 2.5 pounds in 2001. Otolith and scale aging found a dominant 2003-year class. These 3-year-old fish can be attributed to the 2004 yearling stocking. Other year classes present were 1995, 1996, 2001, and 2002. All year classes could be associated to a fall stocking. Walleye growth was typical of the statewide average.

Northern pike were sampled at a rate of 4.8 per gillnet. The catch rate was above the 25th percentile for the lake class but down from the record high of 10.5 per gillnet in 2001. The lake historically has had a low northern population dominated by large fish. While the catch didn't meet the management goal for size, the 3.5-pound mean was an improvement over the previous average of 2 pounds. Captured northerns ranged from 18.6 to 35 inches with a 22.9-inch average. Scale aging indicated that the 2002 and 2003 year classes (3 and 4 year old fish) accounted for 69% of the catch. Growth was normal compared to the statewide rate.

Black crappie were present at normal levels in the trap nets, while the gill net catch was high (10.3/set). The combined catch averaged 7.1 inches and 0.2 pounds. Means were 8.7 inches and 0.4 pounds in 2001. Trap netted crappies were noticeably larger than those from the gill nets and similar in size to the combined catch from 2001. The length frequency of gill netted crappies was bimodal at 5 and 9-inch intervals. Although crappie were not aged, it is apparent that a large year class is poised to enter the fishery in the coming two or three years.

The trap net catch rate for bluegill was 14 per set, which falls in the normal range for the lake class. Bluegill remain small. The largest bluegill sampled was 6.7 inches with more than 70% of the catch less than 6 inches.

Largemouth bass were sampled at a rate of 71.9 per hour during night electrofishing. Lengths ranged from 6.9 to 19.1 inches with a 13.6-inch and 1.6 pound average. Seventy percent of the bass catch exceeded 12 inches while 31 percent were over 15 inches. The previous two assessments found fewer and smaller bass. The 2000 year class was the most abundant, representing more the a fourth of the catch. These six-year-old fish averaged 13.1 inches. Bass growth was a little slower than the state wide average.

Although the 2006 assessment found northern and walleye both below their management goals, a quality largemouth bass and northern pike fishery is present. Walleye add a bonus aspect for anglers while crappie-angling opportunities should increase in the next 2 to 3 years. ?
