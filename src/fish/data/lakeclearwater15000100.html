* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/05/2006)

Big LaSalle Lake is 221-acre lake with a maximum depth of 48 feet located in South-eastern Clearwater County. A public access was developed by the county on the north west side of the lake off of township road 108. The Minnesota Department of Natural Resources has classified Minnesota lakes into 43 different lake classes based on physical and chemical characteristics. Big LaSalle Lake is in lake class 31.

Historically, natural reproduction of walleye supported this population at moderate levels, typical for this lake class. Walleye stocking was initiated in 1991 and stocking rates were increased in 1999 to improve abundance. Current data suggests walleye stocking efforts have been successful in increasing walleye numbers. As part of any stocking plan, La Salle Lake will be monitored and walleye stocking levels will be evaluated in the future to ensure increased walleye stocking efforts are not negatively impacting the fish community.

In 2006, northern pike abundance was typical of other class 31 lakes. Pike ranged in length from 13.0 - 33.5 inches with a mean length and weight of 22.7 inches and 2.9 pounds. Eight different year classes were identified in the sample.

Yellow perch are the most important forage fish for predators such as northern pike and walleye. Few perch were larger than nine inches. White sucker and tullibee (cisco) were also sampled in 2006.

A total of 34 black crappie were sampled in 2006. Five different year classes were identified in the sample, an indication of fairly consistent reproduction. The 2003 (age 3) year class made up 65% of the sample.

Bluegill abundance was typical of other class 31 lakes. In 2006, bluegill ranged in length from 3.3 - 7.6 inches. This was the first assessment on Big LaSalle that no bluegill longer than eight inches was sampled. This may be an indication of the affects of selective harvest on the larger size bluegill.

Other species sampled include largemouth bass, pumpkinseed, rock bass, yellow bullhead and brown bullhead. ?
