Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/14/2004)

Fulda Lake is a 179-acre aerated lake with a maximum depth of 6.5 feet. In spite of being aerated, Fulda has historically had a fish community that was more tolerant of low dissolved oxygen such as yellow perch, black bullheads, and crappies. In 1990-91 Fulda experienced a severe winterkill caused in part by extremely high phosphorus levels from within the sediment. During the winter of 2000-2001 Fulda Lake experienced another significant winterkill that eliminated most game fish. Due to the morphometry of the lake and the connection to the Heron Lake Watershed, spring movement of fish species, such as common carp, bigmouth buffalo, white suckers, and black bullheads, is not uncommon and complicates fisheries management. Following the winterkill of 2000-2001, common carp and white suckers likely repopulated Fulda from the connection to the Graham lakes. Fulda is managed primarily for walleye and secondarily for channel catfish and black crappie. Currently the management plan calls for stocking walleye fingerlings 2 out 3 years at one pound per littoral acre. A population assessment was conducted the week of June 14, 2004 using 2 gill nets and 9 trap nets.

Walleyes sampled in 2004 were rather low in abundance and of small size. Total lengths in trap nets ranged from 7.4 to 13.4 inches and averaged 9.3 inches. Most of the fish sampled were in relatively poor condition. All of the fish sampled corresponded to DNR stockings, which indicated natural reproduction is limited. It is likely that the winterkill of 2000-2001 established a fish community that will be challenging and time consuming to change. Despite the fact that not many walleye were sampled, it is hopeful that stockings sustains a fishery and that better days are ahead.

The most abundant gamefish in Fulda Lake is black crappie. Total lengths ranged from 4.4 to 9.1 inches and averaged 7.2 inches. Although average numbers of black crappie were sampled, the fish are small but in good condition. Because a winterkill check in the spring of 2001 failed to sample any black crappie, adults were re-introduced in 2001, 2002, and 2004 in an effort to establish a naturally reproducing population. It appears that the broodstock are naturally reproducing and will likely start to turn the fish community in a positive direction.

No channel catfish were sampled in the 2004 survey. Channel catfish yearlings were re-introduced in the fall of 2002 as a top-level predator on black bullheads. Typically, channel catfish grow quite slowly in our shallow pothole prairie lakes and would not have been sampled by the 2004 survey.

Unfortunately black bullhead and common carp were the most numerous fish species sampled in gill nets and trap nets during the 2004 survey. Most of the carp sampled were from 12 to 17 inch range and likely the first year class produced following the winterkill of 2000-2001.

A total of twelve species of fish were sampled, including bluegill, green sunfish, hybrid sufish, northern pike, orangespotted sunfish, white crappie, white sucker, and yellow bullhead.

To promote and maintain healthy fish populations, pollution and other inputs need to be controlled. Fish habitats are directly affected by water quality. Nutrient, sediment and other waste inputs can drastically alter the biological, chemical, and physical components of a lake. It is paramount to maintain if not improve the current water quality of Lake Fulda through watershed management to preserve this fishery for future generations to enjoy.
