Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 08/26/2003)

East Lost Lake is a 501-acre mesotrophic (moderately fertile) lake located in central Otter Tail County. The Otter Tail River runs through East Lost Lake. East Lost Lake is approximately three river miles downstream of Otter Tail Lake and is immediately downstream of Deer Lake. The immediate watershed is composed of agricultural land interspersed with hardwood woodlots. East Lost Lake has a maximum depth of 36 feet; however, 65% of the lake is less than fifteen feet in depth. Shallow water substrates consist primarily of sand and gravel. The secchi disk reading at the time of this survey was 10.2 feet. Previous secchi disk readings have ranged from 6.2 to 9.5 feet. East Lost Lake is included in lake class 32 of the MNDNR lake classification scheme. An unimproved, carry-in public access is located on the west end of East Lost Lake off of County Road 45. East Lost can also be accessed from the concrete public access located at the east end of adjacent Deer Lake. Shoreline development is increasing, and approximately 50% of the shoreline is presently developed. Homes and cabins compose the majority of the development. Large stands of hardstem bulrush, common cattail, and wild rice are located at various locations around the lake. Emergent aquatic plants such as bulrush, common cattail and wild rice provide valuable fish and wildlife habitat, and are critical for maintaining good water quality. They protect shorelines and lake bottoms from erosion, and can actually absorb and breakdown polluting chemicals. Emergent plants provide spawning areas for fish species such as northern pike, largemouth bass and panfish. They also serve as an important nursery area for all species of fish. Because of their important ecological value, emergent plants may not be removed without a DNR permit. East Lost Lake can be ecologically classified as a bass-panfish type of lake and this is reflected in the assemblage of the fish community. Walleye, northern pike, largemouth bass, and bluegill are the dominant fish species in the fish community of East Lost Lake. The walleye test-net catch rate exceeded the upper limit of the normal range for class 32 lakes. Walleye fry are stocked on a biennial basis; however, age data indicate that natural reproduction is also contributing to the population. Walleye ranged in length from 8.2 to 22.9 inches with an average length and weight of 16.6 inches and 1.6 pounds. Walleye attain an average length of 15.0 inches at five years of age. The northern pike test-net catch rate has historically remained at a moderate level. Age data indicate that pike reproduction is consistently good. Northern pike ranged in length from 13.2 to 22.8 inches with an average length and weight of 17.0 inches and 1.0 pound. Growth rates are slow with an average length of 17.0 inches at four years of age. Data from a spring electrofishing assessment indicated that a balanced largemouth bass population with consistently good reproduction exists. Bass ranged in length from 7.1 to 20.0 inches with an average length and weight of 13.2 inches and 1.5 pounds. Bass growth is good with an average length of 13.1 inches at four years of age. Bluegill abundance has historically fluctuated. The test-net catch rate in this survey is within the normal range for lake class 32. Age data indicate that bluegill reproduction is inconsistent which is the most likely reason for the fluctuations in abundance. Bluegill size structure is good with forty-two percent of the sample exceeding 7.0 inches in length. Bluegill growth is moderate with an average length of 7.2 inches at seven years of age. Anglers can maintain the quality of angling by practicing selective harvest. Selective harvest encourages the release of medium to large-size fish while allowing the harvest of more abundant smaller fish for table fare. Releasing the medium to large fish will ensure that the lake will have enough spawning age fish on an annual basis and will provide anglers with more opportunities to catch large fish in the future.
