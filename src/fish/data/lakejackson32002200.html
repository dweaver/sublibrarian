Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 07/09/2007)

Clear Lake, near the town of Jackson, is a 415-acre, class 43 lake at the top of the Iowa Great Lakes Watershed. It has had a history of low dissolved oxygen during winter as it has been opened to liberalized fishing nine times since 1949. A helixor aeration system was installed in 1977 and the lake has not been opened to liberalized fishing since, with the exception of 1979 due to an aeration malfunction. Despite its name, Clear Lake has had a history of heavy algae blooms. The duration and frequency of algae blooms is somewhat surprising given the lakes location within the watershed. Typically, lakes near the top of watersheds have lower amounts of surface water runoff, which translates to lower nutrient inputs. Clear Lake has been surveyed every two years since 1997. Fisheries employees, during the surveys, have been concerned about the catchability of fish in nets because of the heavy algae blooms. The nets often become covered in algae, which may influence catchability of fish. Clear Lake is managed primarily for walleye and secondarily for yellow perch and northern pike. A population assessment was conducted during the week of July 9, 2007 using three gill nets and eight trap nets.

Walleye abundance was within the expected range, but has not change since the 2005 survey. The lake management goal strives for a catch rate of 12 walleye/net or higher, which has not happened since 1999. Historically, catch rates have ranged from 1.7 (2003) to 18/net set (1990). Total lengths of walleye captured ranged from 12.4 to 23.3 with an average of 15.7 inches in 2007. The condition of walleye was good, which indicated the fish were finding plenty of forage. Growth of walleye is good as an Age 2 fish was 14.7 inches and an Age 5 fish was 22.4 inches in length. Walleye fry (1000/littoral acre) or fingerlings (up to 2 lbs/littoral acre) are to be stocked every other year. Although there is plenty of forage in Clear Lake and it may be difficult to get them to bite, the walleye that are caught could be "keepers".

Yellow perch numbers were within the expected range (2.7-25 fish/set). The yellow perch catch rate in gill nets was 16.7/set with an average total length of 8.8 inches. Based on the lengths of the fish that were sampled, it is likely that all of the fish were from 1-year class of fish. The condition of the perch sampled indicated the fish were finding plenty of forage. Although the numbers of perch are good enough to produce some descent fishing, it is likely the fishing could be "spotty" as there is plenty of forage in Clear Lake.

Due to the large forage base (small freshwater drum and fatheads), northern pike fingerlings were stocked in 2004, 2005, and 2007. Despite 3 years of stocking, the abundance of northern pike was not within the expected range in 2007. Total lengths of northern pike sampled ranged from 25.2 to 30.1 inches with a mean of 27.6 inches. Although the numbers are not great, the northerns that could be caught are respectable.

The black bullhead catch rate in both gill nets and trap nets was lower than expected. Total lengths of bullheads in gill nets ranged from 8.5 to 12.6 inches with an average of 11.0 inches. Although netting indicated the numbers are down, the sizes of bullheads are desirable for anglers.

The freshwater drum catch rate was the highest recorded catch rate in Clear Lake since 1999, when they were first sampled. Catch rates have ranged from 1.4 to 16.7/gill net with an average length of 11.6 inches.

The channel catfish catch rate has also never been higher. Historically, catch rates have ranged from 0.1 (1993) to 3.3/set (2007). It is likely these fish entered Clear Lake through the outlet from Loon Lake during the high water of 1993. Total lengths ranged from 10.9 to 19.4 inches with a mean of 15.6 inches. There is the possibility of getting into a few of these good-sized fish during those hot summer days of summer.

Efforts to improve the health of the lake should focus on best management practices within the watershed. Improvements and education in land stewardship will often have secondary benefits to the lake in the way of quality and quantity of habitat. Generally, improvements in the quality and quantity of lake habitat will mean direct improvements of the fishery.
