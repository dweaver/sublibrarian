Privately Stocked Fish
* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Fish Consumption Guidelines

No fish consumption guidelines are available for this lake. For more information, see the "Fish Consumption Advice" pages at the Minnesota Department of Health.


Status of the Fishery (as of 07/07/2008)

A resurvey was conducted on Camp Lake in July of 2008. Camp is a small (203 acres), moderately deep (26 feet maximum), and productive lake located in Swift County. Camp is a popular fishing lake for local residents. Water quality and clarity are variable in Camp. Water clarity was good (secchi=7.0 feet) during mid July of 2008. Total phosphorus levels were low to moderate (0.029 ppm) in June of 2008. Mid summer dissolved oxygen levels were low (< 1.00 ppm) at depths greater than 18 feet. High water conditions have resulted in flooding of the public access, excess erosion of the shorelines, and poor water quality due to runoff in Camp during previous years. Water levels were normal in 2008 with no flooding of the public access road or parking lot. Aquatic vegetation (various pondweed species, duckweed species, water moss, bulrush, coontail, northern milfoil, filamentous algae) was common to abundant within the lake during 2008. The north bay had high densities of submergent vegetation and filamentous algae in 2008. Shoalwater substrates were composed of mostly sand, silt, and rubble. There are numerous small inlets that drain water primarily from agricultural row crop fields into Camp Lake. These intermittently flowing inlets were dry or not flowing during the July 2008 resurvey of Camp Lake. The immediate watershed is varied with hardwoods, wetlands, agricultural row crops, turkey barns, feedlots, grasslands, and minimal number (16) of residential homes. A large turkey barn along the southwest shore attracts large numbers of gulls that use Camp Lake. Birds act as an intermediate host for various fish parasites like neascus (black spot) that are prevalent on several fish species (northern pike, yellow perch, walleye) in Camp Lake. Fish eating birds such as white pelicans and double crested cormorants can also be numerous on Camp. The DNR Spicer Fisheries Office manages Camp primarily for largemouth bass and walleye. Camp Lake is currently a study lake for a DNR Research project on walleye stocking.

Black crappie numbers were low in 2008 (0.00 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal ranges for Camp (25th=0.00, 50th=0.25, 75th=7.67 fish/gillnet) and similar lakes. The black crappie historical average catch rate is 3.58 fish/gillnet. Low numbers (0.33 fish/trapnet) of black crappie were captured in the 2008 trapnets. The 2008 black crappie average size was moderate (0.77 pounds and 9.17 inches) from trapnets. The black crappie historical average weight is 0.27 pounds from trapnets.

Bluegill numbers were abundant in 2008 (108.00 fish/trapnet) compared to the normal ranges for Camp (25th=11.00, 50th=19.33, 75th=30.22 fish/trapnet) and similar lakes. The bluegill historical average catch rate is 29.09 fish/trapnet. The 2008 bluegill average size was small (0.05 pounds and 3.86 inches) from trapnets. The bluegill historical average weight is 0.15 pounds from trapnets. The 2008 catch rate of quality size (6.00 inches) and larger bluegill was low (3.89 fish/trapnet). Bluegill growth rates were below the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-2 and within the normal ranges for ages 3-6. The 2006 year class comprised 57% of the 2008 total bluegill catch. The 2005 year class comprised 38% of the 2008 total bluegill catch. Low numbers of young of year "YOY" bluegill (6.00 YOY/haul, 1.46 inches average length) were captured from the 2008 shoreline seine hauls.

No spring electrofishing surveys for largemouth bass were conducted during 2006 or 2008. The 2004 spring electrofishing survey largemouth bass catch rate (66.86 fish/hour) was moderate to good, but below the Camp Lake historical average (150.92 fish/hour). Previous spring electrofishing surveys of largemouth bass documented high numbers (113.89 fish/hour and 0.99 pounds average weight in 1993, 272.00 fish/hour and 0.45 pounds average weight in 1992) in Camp. The largemouth bass average weight and length were 2.11 pounds and 15.00 inches from the 2004 spring electrofishing survey. The largemouth bass historical average size was 1.18 pounds and 11.79 inches from spring electrofishing in Camp. Low numbers of YOY largemouth bass (5.50 YOY/haul, 1.77 inches average length) were captured from the 2008 shoreline seine hauls.

Northern pike numbers were abundant in 2008 (11.25 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal ranges for Camp (25th=3.88, 50th=10.00, 75th=11.00 fish/gillnet) and similar lakes. The northern pike historical average catch rate is 8.03 fish/gillnet. The 2008 northern pike average size was moderate (2.97 pounds and 23.06 inches) from gillnets. The northern pike historical average weight is 3.00 pounds from gillnets. The 2008 catch rate of preferred size (28.00 inches) and larger northern pike was low (0.75 fish/gillnet). Northern pike growth rates were within the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-3, but below the normal ranges for ages 4-8. The 2005 year class comprised 39% of the 2008 northern pike gillnet and trapnet catch.

Walleye numbers were low to moderate in 2008 (3.25 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal ranges for Camp (25th=1.38, 50th =3.25, 75th=9.71 fish/gillnet) and similar lakes. The walleye historical average catch rate is 5.57 fish/gillnet. The 2008 walleye average size was moderate (1.81 pounds and 15.81 inches) from gillnets. The walleye historical average weight is 2.06 pounds from gillnets. The 2008 catch rate of quality size (15.00 inches) and larger walleye was low to moderate (2.50 fish/gillnet). Walleye growth rates were above or within the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-8. The two dominant walleye year classes were 2005 (35%) and 2007 (30%) observed in the 2008 walleye net catch. Recent walleye stockings occurred in 2001 (480 fingerlings, 24 pounds; 135 yearlings, 27 pounds), 2003 (165 yearlings, 50 pounds), 2005 (3,002 fingerlings/yearlings, 194 pounds), and 2007 (98,181 unmarked fry). The 2001, 2003, and 2005 stockings (OTC marked) were part of the DNR walleye stocking research study. Abundant numbers of YOY walleye (83.08 YOY/hour, 6.55 inches average size) were captured in a 2007 fall electrofishing survey. The YOY walleye may have been from a 2007 spring fry stocking, natural reproduction, or a combination of the two.

Yellow perch were low to moderate in 2008 (17.00 fish/gillnet) compared to the normal ranges for Camp (25th=16.25, 50th=29.50, 75th=85.67 fish/gillnet) and similar lakes. The yellow perch historical average catch rate is 50.09 fish/gillnet. The 2008 yellow perch average size was small to moderate (0.11 pounds and 6.32 inches) from gillnets. The yellow perch historical average weight is 0.12 pounds from gillnets. Yellow perch growth rates were generally below the Spicer Area normal ranges for ages 1-4. The 2005 year class comprised 59% of the 2008 yellow perch gillnet catch. There were moderate to low numbers of YOY yellow perch (14.50 YOY/haul, 3.06 inches average length) captured from the 2008 shoreline seine hauls.

Other notable fish species captured in the 2008 survey nets included moderate numbers of white sucker (3.25 fish/gillnet) and low numbers of black bullhead (0.22 fish/trapnet). Historical average catch rates were 2.25 white sucker/gillnet and 18.01 black bullhead/trapnet for Camp Lake.

Current fish management activities on Camp include protecting the important aquatic habitats such as emergent and submergent vegetation through the permit process, encouraging land owners to implement best management practices in the watershed, and stocking walleye fingerlings as warranted. All fish species in Camp Lake will be surveyed again in the summer of 2010. ?
