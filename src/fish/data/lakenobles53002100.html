* indicates privately stocked fish. Private stocking includes fish purchased by the DNR for stocking and fish purchased and stocked by private citizens and sporting groups.

Stocking Fish Sizes
Fry - Newly hatched fish that are ready to be stocked usually called "swim-ups". Walleye fry are 1/3 of an inch or around 8 mm.
Fingerling - Fingerlings are one to six months old and can range from a size of one to twelve inches depending on the species. Walleye fingerlings range from three to eight inches each fall.
Yearling - Yearling fish are at least one year old. A one-year-old fish can range from three to twenty inches depending on the species. Walleye yearlings average from six to twelve inches.
Adult - Adult fish are fish that have reached maturity. Depending on the species, maturity can be reached at two years of age. Walleye reach maturity between the ages of four and six years.

Status of the Fishery (as of 06/12/2006)

West Graham is a 515 acre lake located in Nobles County two miles southwest of the town of Kinbrae. The lake has 3.5 miles of shoreline and a maximum depth of 8 feet. West Graham is primarily managed for walleye with black crappie and channel catfish as secondary species. Walleye fry and fingerlings are stocked in successive years, with the third year left blank. West Graham suffered a partial winterkill during the winter of 2000-01. Numerous carp, bigmouth buffalo, walleye, black crappie and channel catfish washed up along the shoreline after ice-out in April. A population assessment was conducted on West Graham during the week of 06/12/06. Three gill nets and nine trap nets were used to assess the fish population.

The walleye catch rate was above average for lakes similar to West Graham, and increased from the 2002 survey. It looks like the walleye population has recovered from the partial winterkill of 2001. Lengths of walleye ranged from 6.9 to 20.2 inches with an average length of 14.1. Anglers interviewed during the week of the assessment reported catching walleye trolling with artificial lures. Winter anglers also reported some success. Recent walleye stockings include 14,508 fingerlings in 2005 and 534,154 fry in 2004. 75% of the walleyes sampled during the assessment were from the 2004 fry stocking.

The black crappie abundance is well above average. Lengths of black crappie ranged from 5.8 to 11.9 inches and averaged 9.3. The average weight of black crappie swimming in West Graham is nearly half a pound. Great natural reproduction has produced four consecutive year classes. Spring crappie fishing has the potential to be dynamite.

The channel catfish population has increased significantly from the previous survey. Channel catfish stockings in 2001 and 2004 seem to have taken nicely. The average size is 13.3 inches. The chances of a fishable channel catfish population in the near future look promising.

The black bullhead population is high when compared to lakes similar to West Graham. Lengths of bullhead caught ranged from 5.0 to 8.8 inches. Although the number of black bullhead has increased significantly since the last survey, it is hoped the increased walleye and channel catfish populations will begin to forage on bullheads and keep their numbers in check.

The northern pike catch rate was down significantly from a historic high in the last survey. Lack of flooded vegetation available for spawning pike during the spring can cause natural reproduction success to be sporadic or nonexistent.

The yellow perch population remains low but still typical of lakes like West Graham. Lengths of perch caught in our nets average 8.3 inches and are from a single year class.

Other species sampled in our nets include bigmouth buffalo, common carp, and white sucker.

Many of our shallow lakes in the prairie pothole region are impaired because of poor land use practices. Water quality and clarity can be improved by implementing Best Management Practices. An example of this would be planting and restoring native grasses and forbs along shorelines and waterways to buffer the effects of erosion and nutrient loading. For more information on BMPs and what you can do contact your local Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) or DNR office.
